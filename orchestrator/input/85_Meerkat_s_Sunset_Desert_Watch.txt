Once upon a time, in the Kalahari desert where the red earth stretched to every horizon and the sky was the biggest thing in the world, there lived a magic meerkat named Mesa. [long pause]
Mesa was the sentry. Every morning she climbed to the highest point she could find — the top of a termite mound, the crown of a dead tree stump, the shoulder of the tallest member of the family who had learned to hold still while she stood on him — and watched. [sighs with the calm authority of someone who takes their job seriously]
She watched for eagles. For jackals. For anything that moved wrong on the wide red desert. [beat]
This was important work. Everyone understood that. But Mesa had another watch that was entirely hers alone, that she did not report on to anyone.
The sunset lookout.
Every evening, as the desert cooled and the shadows grew long and the light began its extraordinary transformation from white to gold to rose to deep red, Mesa climbed her termite mound and watched. Not for danger. Just for the sunset itself. [softly]
The desert sunset was the finest thing she had ever witnessed, and she had witnessed it almost every day of her life, and it had never been the same twice.
[with growing realization] Tonight the western horizon went from pale yellow to a gold so deep it was almost orange, then to a red that had no name in any language Mesa knew, then to a violet that spread upward and turned the whole vast sky above her into something she could only call, inaccurately, a painting.
The star dunes in the middle distance turned the color of embers. The sparse desert grasses caught the red light and glowed. [pacing]
Mesa stood on her termite mound and felt the whole evening settle into the desert around her, warm and quiet, the day's heat releasing slowly into the cooling air.
[softly] The other meerkats went underground one by one. Mesa stayed a little longer.
When the last color left the sky and the first star appeared, she descended. She had done her job all day. Now the day had done its job for her — had given her something so beautiful it filled something up inside her that she hadn't known was empty.
That, Mesa thought, was a fair exchange.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
