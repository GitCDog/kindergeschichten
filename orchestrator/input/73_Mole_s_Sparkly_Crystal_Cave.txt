Once upon a time, deep beneath a meadow where the roots of old things reached down and the earth kept its secrets in the dark, there lived a magic mole named Mira. [long pause]
Mira had velvety black fur, strong digging paws, and eyes that were small but caught what little light there was with extraordinary efficiency. She had tunneled through clay, through chalk, through layers of packed earth that contained the pressed memories of a thousand years. She knew the underground the way some creatures know the sky. [sighs with the comfort of someone perfectly suited to their world]
But she had never found the crystal cave. [beat]
The crystal cave was something her grandmother had mentioned once, in the way of things that might be memory or might be dream — a place deep underground where something glowed. Where the earth itself had made light, over a very long time, from nothing but pressure and patience.
Mira had been digging toward it, in her spare moments, for two years. [softly]
She dug east from the meadow. She dug down below the clay. She found the chalk again — softer here, older — and followed it deeper. Her paws knew the work. Her nose read the earth in front of her the way a reader reads a page: this layer, then this layer, then something different, something she hadn't felt before. [with growing realization]
A change in the texture. A coolness. A faint, faint something that wasn't smell and wasn't sound but was — light. Underground glow. Light in the dark where there should not be any light.
She pushed through. [pacing]
The crystal cave was not large — she could cross it in twenty paw-lengths. But every surface was covered in formations of white crystal, each one faceted, each one catching the underground glow from some source too deep to see and throwing it back multiplied. The whole cave shimmered like the inside of a geode — like being inside a star. [softly]
Mira sat in the center of it for a very long time. Then she turned around and dug back carefully, marking every turn.
She went home. She told her children. She brought them back the next day, one by one.
Some things, she understood, are only truly found when they are shared.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
