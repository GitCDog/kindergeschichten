Once upon a time, in a village where the autumn leaves were always crisp and golden and the smell of pumpkin pie drifted from every window, there lived a little witch named Willa. [long pause]
Willa had a pointy hat that was two sizes too big and a broomstick named Twiggy that had a very strong opinion about which direction to fly. She was nine years old and had been practicing magic for exactly one year, which meant she was good at some things — turning stones into toads was easy — and not so good at others. [sighs] Broomstick racing, for instance.
The Annual Pumpkin Patch Broomstick Race was the most important event of the autumn season. Every young witch and wizard in the county entered. The winner got a crown made of golden leaves and a pumpkin so large it needed three people to carry it. [beat]
Willa wanted that pumpkin very, very much.
On the morning of the race, she polished Twiggy until the twigs gleamed. She pressed her hat firmly down over her ears. She lined up at the starting stone with eleven other young witches, all of whom seemed considerably more confident than she felt. [softly]
"Ready?" called the race marshal. "Through the pumpkin patch, around the old scarecrow, and back to the starting stone! On your marks —"
Willa gripped Twiggy tightly.
"— get set —"
Twiggy immediately tried to go left. [abruptly]
"— go!"
The race was chaos. Twiggy zigged when Willa said zag. They flew sideways through a row of pumpkins, scattering orange gourds in every direction. They spun around the old scarecrow three times by accident. Willa's hat flew off and had to be caught from a nosedive. [pacing] Every time she thought they were catching up, Twiggy found something new to investigate — a crow, a wind chime, an interesting smell.
They came in last.
Willa landed and sat on the grass, out of breath, her hat lopsided. Twiggy floated beside her looking completely unrepentant.
[with growing realization] Then she started laughing.
Because when she thought about it — the pumpkins flying, the spinning scarecrow, the lost hat — it was actually the most fun she had ever had in her entire life.
She patted Twiggy fondly. "Same time next year?" she said.
Twiggy dipped its twigs in what Willa chose to interpret as enthusiastic agreement.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
