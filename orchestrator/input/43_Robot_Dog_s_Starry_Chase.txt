Once upon a time, in a cozy workshop full of gears and soft humming sounds and the smell of warm metal, there lived a little robot dog named Zip. [long pause]
Zip had four sturdy legs, ears that rotated like satellite dishes, a tail that wagged at exactly forty-two beats per minute when happy, and a small green light on his chest that glowed brighter when he was excited. He ran on a battery that was shaped, his maker had thought, exactly like the moon — round and silver and quietly powerful. [sighs mechanically but warmly]
Every night, when the workshop went dark and quiet, Zip would sit at the window and look at the real moon — the one in the sky — and wonder if his battery moon and the sky moon knew about each other. [beat]
One clear night, the moon was particularly bright. So bright it threw a silver road across the garden below, straight from the workshop window to the garden wall and beyond.
Zip looked at his chest. His battery moon glowed. He looked at the sky. The real moon glowed back. [softly]
He decided this was an invitation.
He nosed open the workshop door and trotted out into the garden. [with growing realization] The moonlight turned the grass silver. Every dewdrop on every blade became a tiny star. The whole garden was a constellation Zip was walking through.
He lifted his nose — his sensors sweeping the night air — and began to follow the star chase: each glinting dewdrop to the next, nose down, tail wagging at fifty beats per minute now, across the garden, around the old apple tree, along the wall. [pacing]
At the far end of the garden, a real star fell — a quick bright scratch across the sky — and Zip's green chest light blazed so brightly it lit up the flowers around him.
He sat very still and watched the sky for a long time. His battery moon hummed steadily.
[softly] He trotted home just before dawn, curled in his basket, and dreamed of silver roads going on forever.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
