Once upon a time, in a forest of tree ferns and ancient kauri on a long island at the bottom of the world, there lived a kind kiwi named Koa. [long pause]
Koa had brown feathers so fine they looked almost like fur, a beak so long it seemed designed for a world of great depth, and eyes that were small and round and kind — though she could barely see in daylight, which was fine, because Koa was thoroughly nocturnal and had no interest in daylight whatsoever. [sighs contentedly]
She went out every night. The night was hers. [beat]
She walked the forest floor on sturdy legs, probing the soft earth with her long beak, finding worms and grubs and the occasional beetle with an efficiency that other creatures found quietly impressive. The brown feathers were perfect camouflage against the dark forest floor. She moved without hurry and without noise.
But once a month, on the night of the full moon, something special happened. [softly]
The star fruit vine — a wild, sprawling thing that grew over the old kauri near the stream — put out one single fruit. Round, yellow-green, and faintly luminous in the moonlight, as if it had absorbed enough starlight over the month to give a little back.
Koa always knew when it was ready. [with growing realization]
She could smell it from the far end of the forest — a smell unlike anything else in the night: sweet and sharp and clean, like the air before rain but sweeter, like something the stars themselves might smell of, if stars had a smell.
She would walk there slowly, through the fern forest, under the great kauri, along the stream bank to the vine. She would stand before the star fruit for a moment. [pacing]
Then she would eat it — slowly, the way things worth waiting for should be eaten.
[softly] It tasted like the night tasted on its very best nights: deep and cool and full of things you couldn't quite name but somehow knew. It tasted like belonging completely to a world that was made for you.
Koa would sit by the stream afterward until the moon set. Then she would go back to her forest and her work and her good dark life.
Some things, she knew, are worth waiting a whole month for.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
