Once upon a time, in the corner of a garden gate where the wood was old and the paint had peeled in interesting ways, there lived a smart spider named Sylvia. [long pause]
Sylvia had eight legs, eight eyes, and a mind that worked like the webs she built — everything connected to everything else, no loose ends, every thread exactly where it needed to be. She was the finest web-builder in the garden, and she knew it, in the modest way of someone who has simply put in the work and can see the results. [sighs with quiet pride]
But this autumn she had decided to build something different. Not a web for catching — a web castle. [beat]
She had been planning it since summer. The dew drop castle of her imagination had seven rooms, six bridges, and a great hall at the center wide enough to turn around in comfortably. Each thread would be placed with architectural intention. Each junction would be reinforced. The silky web would catch the morning dew so that by dawn every surface would be studded with droplets, and the whole structure would glow like something made of glass and cold fire.
She began on the night of the first frost. [softly]
She worked for five nights. Thread by thread, junction by junction, room by room. Her legs moved with such precision that even the spiders watching from nearby webs — because spiders do watch each other, though they pretend not to — stopped pretending and simply stared. [pacing]
On the sixth morning, the dew came.
[with growing realization] Sylvia sat at the edge of her dew drop castle and watched the light arrive. It touched the outer threads first, making each droplet flare silver. Then the bridges. Then the rooms — each one a different angle to the light, each catching it differently, the whole structure shifting and sparkling as the sun moved. [softly]
A garden spider three gates down — old, experienced, not easily impressed — crawled slowly along the fence and looked. Said nothing for a long time.
"How long did that take?" she finally asked.
"Five nights," said Sylvia.
The old spider nodded slowly. "Worth it," she said.
Sylvia thought so too.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
