Once upon a time, on a coastline where the cliffs were white and the sea was always moving and the wind always had something to say, there lived a sparkly seagull named Silver. [long pause]
Silver had white and gray feathers that the coastal wind had polished to a particular sheen, yellow eyes that missed nothing, and a passion for shiny things that she made no effort to conceal. Her nest was, by any measure, the most decorated nest on the cliff — lined with silver foil, three bottle caps, a fishing lure, and the wing-mirror from a bicycle that she had taken from the harbor car park and refused to give back. [sighs with serene satisfaction]
But she had heard of a shell treasure that she had not yet found. [beat]
The shell treasure was described by the elder gulls as a shell unlike any other — large, spiraling, its interior a deep iridescent pink that caught light and turned it into something close to music. It was said to be somewhere on the beach below the northern cliff, but the northern cliff had a long beach and Silver had only two eyes. [softly]
She began her search on a Tuesday, which is as good a day as any for a quest.
She flew the length of the beach three times, low and slow, her yellow eyes scanning the tideline. She found things — good things, shiny things, several she pocketed for later — but not the shell treasure. [with growing realization]
On her fourth pass, she landed beside an old man who was doing the same thing she was: walking slowly, looking down.
"Shell hunting?" she asked.
The old man looked at her — not many seagulls spoke to him — and said: "I am. My granddaughter is coming to visit and she loves shells."
They searched together for an hour. The old man knew things Silver didn't — how the tide moved things, where they collected, which patches of sand held the best finds. [pacing]
He found it first. He held it up and Silver saw — yes. The deep pink spiral. The light moving inside it like something alive.
[softly] He gave it to Silver. "You were looking for it longer," he said.
Silver held it in her beak for a moment. She felt its weight. Its warmth from the sun. Then she set it back on the sand in front of him. "For your granddaughter," she said. "Some treasures know who they're meant for."
She flew back to her cliff, empty-beaked and entirely satisfied.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
