Once upon a time, in a jungle so full of noise and color and life that even the leaves seemed to have opinions, there lived a playful parrot named Rio. [long pause]
Rio had colorful feathers that changed shade depending on the light — turquoise in the morning, green at midday, somewhere between gold and copper in the late afternoon — and a voice that could do anything. Any sound. Any voice. Any call. He had, on several occasions, convinced the entire east side of the jungle that it was raining when it wasn't, purely for his own amusement. [sighs, somewhat proudly]
What Rio loved most was jokes. Specifically, telling them. And most specifically, throwing the first ever Jungle Joke Party. [beat]
He sent invitations through the canopy — a series of calls that translated roughly as: come to the big fig tree at sunset, bring something funny, stay for the mango.
More animals arrived than he expected. [with growing realization] The toucan came, and the tapir, and the three capuchin monkeys who always traveled together and always finished each other's sentences. A slow-moving sloth arrived early — she had started the journey three days ago when she heard the invitation. A nervous iguana arrived last and sat at the very back.
Rio opened proceedings. [pacing]
His first joke landed beautifully. The capuchins fell off their branch.
The toucan's joke was very long and extremely confusing and nobody was sure if it was over or not, but the toucan seemed satisfied and that seemed to be enough.
The sloth told a joke so slowly that by the punchline three animals had fallen asleep — but those three, when they woke up and heard it, laughed the longest. [softly]
The nervous iguana at the back raised a claw. Everybody turned.
She told a joke in a voice barely above a whisper. It was, quite without question, the funniest joke anyone had ever heard in the jungle. The capuchins fell off their branch again. Rio laughed so hard a colorful feather came loose. Even the trees seemed to shake.
[beat] After, when the mango was eaten and the stars were out, Rio sat in his fig tree and thought about the nervous iguana's quiet voice and the loudest laughter of the evening.
Sometimes the best things, he decided, come from the quietest places.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
