Once upon a time, deep inside a fallen log in a forest clearing, there lived a tiny termite named Theo. [long pause]
Theo was small even by termite standards, with delicate pale wings he rarely used and antennae that quivered constantly, reading the air for information. He was part of a great colony — thousands of termites, all working, all purposeful — but Theo had an idea that was entirely his own, and he had been thinking about it for a very long time. [sighs with the quiet intensity of someone with a plan]
He wanted to build a wooden castle. [beat]
Not the tunnels and chambers of ordinary termite architecture — those were excellent and necessary, and he respected them deeply — but something more. Something with towers. A great hall. Passageways wide enough for three termites walking side by side. A design with intention behind it, not just function.
He had drawn the plans on a piece of smooth bark with a chewed stick. He had showed them to his friend Nell, who was practical and whose opinion he trusted. [softly]
"It's ambitious," said Nell.
"Yes," said Theo.
"Will the colony help?"
Theo looked at his plans. "I don't know. I'm going to ask."
[with growing realization] He asked. He expected resistance — termites were busy, termites had work, termites did not generally pause their established routines to build things that were primarily beautiful. But he had made his plans very clear, and he had explained not just the what but the why: a castle at the center of the log, a place that was theirs, a monument to what they could do when they worked together toward something extraordinary.
Twelve termites volunteered that evening. [pacing]
They worked for three weeks. The wooden castle grew — tower by chamber by passageway — shaped from the log's own material, every wall smooth and exact. Theo directed and dug alongside everyone else. Nell turned out to have an instinctive understanding of architecture that neither of them had known about.
When the last passage was finished and the first light came through the entrance Theo had designed — a perfect arch, angled to catch the morning sun — [softly] the whole tunnel friends colony came to see.
The light fell through the arch and lit the great hall in warm amber.
No one spoke for a while.
Then everyone, all at once, got back to work — a little prouder than before.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
