Once upon a time, in the fork of a tall elm tree at the edge of a busy town, there lived a clever crow named Clem. [long pause]
Clem had glossy black feathers that caught the light like oil on water, sharp bright eyes that missed nothing, and a treasure nest — a collection of shiny pebbles, bottle caps, a square of silver foil, and one particularly magnificent glass marble that caught the sun and threw rainbows across the bark. Other crows admired it. Clem acted as though this were normal. [sighs modestly]
But there was one pebble Clem wanted above all others. [beat]
She had seen it from the air three days ago — a small, smooth pebble on the pavement below the clock tower, clear as water with a silver thread running through it. Like a frozen bolt of lightning captured inside a stone. She had circled it six times. Each time something had interrupted — a cat, a bicycle, a child who came too close.
On the seventh day, Clem made her plan. [softly]
Early morning. The street quiet. She dropped from the elm like a black arrow, landed two pebble-lengths away from the stone, and walked — with great dignity, as crows do — the final distance.
She picked it up. [with growing realization] It was even more beautiful close up. The silver thread caught the morning light and flickered.
Then a small voice said: "Oh! That's mine!"
Clem froze. A small girl, perhaps five years old, stood on the pavement with her hands clasped. "I dropped it yesterday," she said. "I've been looking for it. My grandma gave it to me."
[long pause] Clem looked at the pebble. She looked at the girl.
She set the pebble down carefully on the pavement between them. [pacing]
The girl picked it up with both hands and held it to the light, and her face did something that Clem thought was probably the closest a human face could get to how Clem felt when she found a truly exceptional shiny pebble.
[softly] Clem flew back to her nest. She looked at her collection. She thought about the girl's face.
Some things shine brighter, she decided, when they're exactly where they belong.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
