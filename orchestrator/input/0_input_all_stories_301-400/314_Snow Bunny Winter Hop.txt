Somewhere in the northern forest, where the snow was deep enough to swallow a small rabbit entirely if she wasn't careful, a tiny snow bunny named Bun was conducting her evening practice.

The winter hop required a specific technique.

Ordinary hopping — the kind rabbits did in all seasons — would not work in deep snow. You would sink. Your legs would disappear, your momentum would die, and you would be stuck, which was undignified. Instead, Bun used what she thought of as the skim hop: a flatter, wider trajectory that kept her mostly on the snow's surface rather than punching through it.

The ice burrow was her home — a chamber she had dug into the compressed snow beneath an old spruce tree, reached through a tunnel that kept the wind out and the warmth in. It was not large, but it was hers, and it smelled of clean cold and spruce resin, which she found extremely pleasant.

Tonight she was practicing loops.

A loop was a complete circuit around the large birch grove — approximately four hundred meters — conducted at full speed on the skim hop, which required maintaining the flat trajectory all the way around even on the corners, where the temptation was to slow down.

She completed the first loop in good time.

The second loop she made a mistake at the east corner — sank briefly, scrambled, recovered — and lost several seconds. She was critical of herself about this for approximately four meters and then stopped being critical, because there was no point in carrying that kind of weight on a winter hop.

The third loop was perfect.

She arrived back at the birch grove entrance breathing hard and very pleased with herself. The forest was quiet and blue-dark, the snow undisturbed except for her loop tracks, which formed a neat circle around the trees.

Bun looked at the tracks she had made.

Then she went down her tunnel, into the warm ice burrow, and tucked her nose under her tail.


The ice burrow was warm from her own body heat by the time she woke. Outside, the spruce tree had collected another inch of snow on its lower branches. Bun's loop tracks from the previous night were still visible, faint impressions in the surface.

The winter hop would improve with practice. It always did. She would be faster by February, and by March she would be running on snow that other rabbits still found difficult.

For now, she tucked her nose under her tail and let the morning come.

[pause] Good night my little friend
