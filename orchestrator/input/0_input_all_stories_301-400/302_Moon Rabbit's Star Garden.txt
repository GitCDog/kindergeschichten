On the moon, on the side that always faces away from Earth, there was a garden that no telescope had ever quite managed to find. It belonged to a curious moon rabbit named Pip, and it was the most unusual garden in the solar system.

Pip grew star carrots.

Star carrots were not like ordinary carrots. They were thin and bright and cold to the touch, and they glowed very faintly — just enough to light the path between the rows at night, which on the moon was very long indeed.

The night garden was Pip's domain, and she tended it with great seriousness.

Every lunar morning she would check the star carrot seedlings — pressing her paw gently into the grey dust to feel for moisture, which was scarce and had to be coaxed from the ice below the surface. Every evening she would count the glow of the grown carrots and make sure none had been disturbed by meteor dust.

One night, a young star fell from the sky.

It wasn't a full star — just a small fragment of starlight that had broken loose from a constellation and drifted too close to the moon. It landed in Pip's garden with a tiny bright flash and lay there, cooling slowly.

Pip hopped over and looked at it.

It was about the size of her ear. It glowed softly, steadily, without flicker — the way only real starlight glows.

"Are you lost?" she asked.

The starlight fragment didn't answer in words. But it pulsed once, gently, as if to say: not anymore.

Pip decided to plant it.

She dug a small hole between two rows of star carrots, placed the fragment carefully inside, and covered it with lunar dust. Then she waited, as gardeners do.

By the next morning, something was growing. A new kind of plant — not quite a carrot, not quite a star — pushing up through the grey soil with a light that was warmer than anything else in the night garden.

Pip sat back on her haunches and watched it unfurl.

The best things in gardens, she had always known, were the ones you hadn't planned for.
