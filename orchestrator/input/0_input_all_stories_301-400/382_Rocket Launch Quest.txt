The brave rocket's name was First, because she was.

The first launch of the year, the first vehicle of her class, the first attempt at a mission profile that had been designed over six years and tested in simulation more than two thousand times and was now, on a clear morning in January, about to be tested for real.

The launch team had been in mission control since four in the morning.

The space rise began at zero seven thirty-two.

First ignited her main engines at T-minus ten seconds — a controlled, gradually increasing thrust that built toward launch — and when the hold-down clamps released at T-zero, she rose immediately and deliberately from the launch pad, the star path ahead of her calculated and stored and ready.

The first thirty seconds were the most dangerous.

Leaving the ground was the hardest part: the atmosphere was thickest at the bottom, the vehicle was heaviest with full propellant, and the forces were greatest. First moved through the thickest part of the air with her nose at the precise angle calculated to minimize aerodynamic stress.

Then the air thinned and the forces reduced and First began to accelerate properly.

At forty-five seconds she passed through maximum dynamic pressure — the point where the aerodynamic forces on her structure were highest — and her engineers watching the telemetry exhaled.

At two minutes she jettisoned her first stage.

The star path ahead was clear.

First was above the atmosphere. Below her, the curve of the Earth was visible — the thin blue line of the atmosphere, the darkness of space beginning above it.

The launch quest continued upward.


First reached her target orbit at T plus thirty-eight minutes. The mission control team had been tracking every second of it, and when the confirmation came through, the room was briefly very loud.

Then quiet, and back to work.

First's star path was now the orbital path — a fixed ellipse around the Earth, silent and precise, doing the work she had been launched to do.

The launch quest was complete. The mission had begun.
