The parrot feather had been bright green once — the particular green of a living wing — but now it was a softer, older green, found on the forest floor where it had spiraled down three weeks ago and been picked up by a girl named Mia.

Mia carried the curious parrot feather everywhere.

Not because it was useful, exactly. Feathers are useful in specific contexts — for writing with, if you have ink and no pen; for tickling, if you have a suitable target — but Mia carried it because of the color trail.

The color trail was what happened when she held the feather up in sunlight. The green shifted through a range that no paint could replicate — bright yellow-green at the tip, deeper emerald toward the quill, with a structural iridescence that changed depending on the angle: sometimes blue, sometimes gold, sometimes a color that didn't quite have a name.

She had shown it to everyone she knew.

Her grandmother said it was the color of deep forest shade.

Her friend said it looked like a traffic light that had made a creative decision.

Her teacher held it up to the classroom window and let the light catch it, and the whole room went slightly quiet.

The sky glide was something Mia imagined when she held it up against the sky. What must it be like, she thought, to be a wing — not to have a wing, but to be one? To have your whole body shaped for the single purpose of moving through air?

She held the feather at arm's length and walked fast.

The feather responded to the air passing over it, vibrating very slightly, the vane catching the breeze the way a wing catches it — not fighting it, not ignoring it, but negotiating with it at every fiber.

She didn't become a bird.

But she understood, for a moment, what it would mean to be one.


The feather went everywhere Mia went that autumn. It sat in her coat pocket when it wasn't being held, and its color trail would catch her off guard sometimes — she would reach in for something else and find it there, still green, still iridescent, still doing something interesting with whatever light was available.

It was, she decided, the best thing she had found on a forest floor, and she had found many good things on forest floors.
