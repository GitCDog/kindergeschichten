The submarine was named Deep Blue, and she had been exploring the ocean for six years.

Not a military submarine — a research one, run by an oceanographic institute, staffed by scientists who had dedicated their careers to finding out what the ocean kept in its deepest parts. There was a great deal to find. The deep sea was the least explored environment on the planet, and Deep Blue was one of the few instruments capable of reaching it.

The deep dive on this mission would take them to four thousand meters.

The curious scientist leading the mission was named Dr. Patel, and she had been on eleven previous dives and found something remarkable on nine of them. She had stopped expecting remarkable things and started expecting interesting things, which was a more sustainable approach and had the advantage of making everything interesting.

The descent took two hours.

The pressure outside the hull, at four thousand meters, was four hundred times the surface pressure. Deep Blue was built for this — tested, certified, rigorously maintained — but Dr. Patel felt it anyway. The weight of water above them was not abstract.

The sea explore began with the lights.

When the exterior lights came on at depth, the world outside was revealed one meter at a time: the seafloor, covered in sediment that had been settling for thousands of years. A brittle star moving slowly across it. A cloud of small crustaceans disturbed by the submersible's arrival.

Then something Dr. Patel had not seen before: a formation of rock that didn't match the geological survey, with a different color and texture, at the wrong depth for its type.

She noted it and began recording.

Nine of eleven dives. Ten, now.


Dr. Patel's findings were published eight months later. The geological formation was new to the literature — a previously uncharted ridge of volcanic origin, running perpendicular to the mapped structures. Other submarines would come to investigate.

Deep Blue went on to her next mission six weeks after this one, carrying a different science team to a different part of the ocean, doing what she had been built for.

The deep dive continued.
