The pirate map had been made to look old, but it had been made last Tuesday.

The cartographer was a girl named Nell, who had researched pirate maps extensively before making hers and had made some considered design decisions. She had aged the paper with tea. She had drawn the coastline in a wobbly, dramatic style. She had placed the X mark at a location that was genuinely hard to find without the clues she had embedded in the decorative borders.

This was, Nell had decided, the important thing: the X mark should require actual navigation.

The island path shown on the little pirate map was her back garden and the three gardens adjacent to it, all accessible through a well-known gap in the left hedge. The treasure was a tin box buried near the old rose bush by the south wall.

The map had been given to her brother Kit that morning.

Kit had studied the map for twenty minutes with the seriousness he brought to all puzzles. He had noted the compass rose (oriented correctly), the scale bar (roughly accurate), and the warning text in the corner that said Beware the Roses in a handwriting that was clearly Nell's but which Kit charitably decided might be a clue.

It was a clue.

The island path required going past the rose bush twice before going to it directly, which Kit figured out on his second circuit.

He dug six centimeters down with the garden trowel and found the tin.

Inside: five chocolate coins (golden foil), a certificate of completion drawn in Nell's careful hand, and a note that said: Well done, Navigator.

Kit held up the certificate.

Nell, watching from the kitchen window, gave a thumbs-up.

The pirate map adventure was complete, and had been, as planned, exactly difficult enough.


Nell accepted the certificate back with appropriate ceremony and kept it in her project folder. The pirate map went on her wall, slightly crinkled from its adventure, the X mark faded now in the fold.

She was already working on the next one. The next island path would be harder. The treasure would be better.

Kit would need at least an hour. Maybe two.
