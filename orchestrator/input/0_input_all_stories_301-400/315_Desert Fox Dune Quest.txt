The desert at night was a different world from the desert at day.

In the day, the sand dunes were blinding and the heat was a presence that pressed on you from all sides. In the night, the same dunes turned cool and silver-blue under the stars, and the heat quest became something quite different: a quest for warmth rather than shade.

The brave desert fox's name was Fen, and she had been making the dune quest for three years — ever since she was old enough to leave the family den alone.

The dune quest was not a journey to any particular place. It was a nightly circuit of her territory, marked at various points by scent posts she had established along the dunes' crests and hollows. Checking each post, renewing her presence, reading whatever other animals had left in her absence — this was the work of the night.

The sand dunes shifted between visits. This was the desert's particular inconvenience: what was familiar at the beginning of the week might be altered by wind into something new by the end of it. Dunes moved. Paths disappeared. New hollows formed where there had been crests.

Fen was accustomed to this. She navigated not by specific landmarks but by the relationship between things — the angle of the north star above the ridge, the direction the wind came from, the sound of the dry grass in the hollow near the dry stream bed.

Tonight, on the third dune, she found something unexpected.

A beetle, flipped onto its back, legs windmilling helplessly against the air.

She nudged it with her nose. It righted itself and was motionless for a moment — presumably reconsidering its evening plans — then walked very quickly in the direction of a thorn bush.

Fen watched it go.

She continued the heat quest, her paws barely marking the cool sand, her ears catching everything.


Fen completed the heat quest by the fourth hour after midnight, arriving back at the family den with the information she had collected: the territory was secure, the scent posts were current, a new family of beetles had established near the dry stream bed.

She curled in the entrance of the den for a while, sand cooling against her fur, and watched the stars.

The desert at the end of the night was her favorite version of itself — cool and quiet and full of the particular peace of a world that had been checked and found in order.
