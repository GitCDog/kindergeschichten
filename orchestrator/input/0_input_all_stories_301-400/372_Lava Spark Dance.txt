When the volcano was active, the lava spark was the most visible part.

Not the lava itself — that was slow and heavy and moved with the weight of melted rock. But at the place where the lava met the air, where its surface cracked and cooled and re-cracked, small fragments of superheated material would fly free, catching the dark air as sparks.

The fire jump of each spark lasted only a second or two. The temperature that made them glow dropped the moment they left the lava's body, and the glow dimmed from bright orange to dull red to dark in the space of the flight.

The playful lava spark who went the highest — higher than any other spark on this particular evening — was a tiny piece of basaltic glass that was already cooling when she separated from the flow. She rose three meters above the flow front and was, briefly, the highest thing on the slope.

From there she could see — in the fraction of a second before her light went out — the whole picture: the dark mountain, the red line of the flow's edge, the heat dance shimmering above it, and far below, the lights of the village.

She came down as a piece of dark rock the size of a thumbnail.

The heat dance continued after her, as it continued after every spark. The lava surface was constantly producing and losing small bright fragments, the flow edge a line of light that advanced slowly, methodically, without hurry.

A photographer had set up three hundred meters away with a long lens, trying to capture the lava spark in flight.

It was difficult. Each spark was unpredictable and brief.

But the heat dance of many sparks together — that was something she could capture and did.


The photographer reviewed her images that evening in the research tent.

The heat dance was in fifty of them — the shimmer above the flow's edge, visible even in still images as the distortion of the air. The fire jump of individual sparks had been caught in three frames: small, bright, indistinct, beautiful.

She chose the best one for the folder labeled: Worth Keeping.
