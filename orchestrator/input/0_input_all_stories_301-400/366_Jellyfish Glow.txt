The jellyfish moved through the water in the way that jellyfish move: by pulsing.

Each pulse contracted the bell and drove water out below, producing a small jet that moved the jellyfish upward and forward. Then the bell relaxed and the jellyfish drifted, trailing tentacles below. Then another pulse.

The silly jellyfish — silly in the sense of being utterly free of agenda — had no name and no plan and no particular direction. She went where the current went, with occasional adjustments of her own.

The sea float was her whole existence.

What the jellyfish did have was a light pulse. She was a bioluminescent species — not the most spectacular bioluminescent species, not the deep-ocean type that put on dramatic displays, but a moderate glower: a faint blue-green light that pulsed with her contractions. When she pulsed her bell to move, she also pulsed light.

This correlation — movement and light together — meant that in dark water, she was visible as a moving point of cold fire.

She moved through the night sea in her slow, pulsing way.

The light pulse was not purposeful. It was a byproduct of the chemistry that powered her movement. But from the outside — from the perspective of something watching the dark water — it looked purposeful. It looked like an intention: I am here. I am moving. I am this light in this darkness.

A research submersible spotted her at two hundred meters.

The scientist watching the camera feed said, "Oh."

It was the sound of seeing something beautiful without preparation. The jellyfish pulsed past the camera lens — blue-green, cold, slow, completely indifferent to the attention — and was lost in the dark beyond the lights.

The scientist sat for a moment.

Then went back to her work.


The scientist went back to her work and then paused, and then wrote a note to herself: look up bioluminescent jellyfish species at 200m, this region.

The jellyfish was long gone into the dark water, pursuing whatever jellyfish pursue at that depth, which was primarily the current and whatever the current brought.

The light pulse continued, invisible now, no camera to catch it, serving its original function or no function at all — the two possibilities indistinguishable from inside a jellyfish.
