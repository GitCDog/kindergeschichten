The little drone's name was Zip, and Zip had been flying for three years with a pilot named Rae.

Rae was not a professional pilot. She was a photographer who had learned to fly drones because cameras on drones could go places cameras on tripods couldn't. She had started with a basic model and had graduated to Zip — a camera drone with a stabilized gimbal and a range of two kilometers — after a year of practice.

The sky hover was Zip's specialty.

While other drones climbed and traveled, Zip could hold position in a wind — correcting constantly, invisibly, her four rotors making small adjustments hundreds of times per second to keep the camera perfectly still relative to whatever Rae wanted to photograph.

The view above was the point.

From thirty meters, the world below looked different. Not completely different — recognizable, still the same world — but rearranged. The things that seemed important from the ground (fences, hedges, the height of buildings) became less prominent. The things that seemed minor (the layout of paths, the pattern of fields, the shape of a river's curve) became the whole picture.

Rae had discovered this in her first year of flying and found she couldn't stop thinking about it.

Every place had two versions: the one you walked through, and the one you could only see by being above it. She had started making projects about this — paired images, ground and air, same location.

Today's flight was over a community garden.

From the ground: a pleasant arrangement of raised beds, a shed, some people working. From Zip's height: a pattern — the geometry of the beds, the diagonal path, the circle of the central seating area — that no one working in the garden could see.

Zip held position.

Rae took the photograph.


Rae brought Zip back to her hand as the battery indicator reached twenty percent. She reviewed the photographs on the tablet while Zip cooled.

The view above the community garden was exactly what she had hoped for — the geometry clear, the two versions of the same place sitting beside each other in her screen.

Zip's data was good. Tomorrow she would do the ground-level images for the pair.
