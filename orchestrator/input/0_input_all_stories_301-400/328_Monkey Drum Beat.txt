High in the canopy of the rainforest, where the branches were wide enough to sit on and the wind moved the top leaves in long, slow waves, a playful monkey named Bam had discovered something extraordinary.

A hollow branch.

Not just hollow — resonant. When he struck it with his palm, it produced a sound that carried through the canopy in a way that ordinary sticks and leaves did not. It had depth. It had sustain. It had, in short, the fundamental properties of a drum.

The beat fun began immediately.

Bam struck the hollow branch twice — testing the note from two positions — and found that one end produced a higher sound and the other end a lower one. He struck both in sequence: low-high, low-high. The tree music traveled out through the branches and descended to the forest floor, where a pair of peccaries looked up from their rooting and then looked away.

The other monkeys in his group came to investigate.

Most of them tried the hollow branch once and lost interest. But a smaller monkey named Tap sat beside Bam and watched with great attention.

"Again," Tap said, in the way monkeys communicate, which was more gesture than sound.

Bam demonstrated the low-high pattern.

Tap tried it. Got it on the second attempt.

They began to play together — Bam establishing the low-high beat, Tap adding a faster layer on top, their palms moving in an interlocking rhythm that filled the canopy with beat fun neither of them had planned.

The rainforest received the tree music the way it received everything: without opinion, incorporating the new sound into the general layered chorus of the canopy — insect, bird, wind, monkey drum — a composition assembled from the available materials of the living world.

Bam played until the light left the canopy.

Then he curled in the branch fork and slept, his palms still warm.


Tap came back to the hollow branch the next morning, alone, and practiced what they had discovered together. The low-high pattern was easy now. She began to vary it — a pause here, a triple strike there, listening to how the canopy received each change.

The tree music was different when she played it alone. That was interesting. She would show Bam tomorrow.

The rainforest continued its many-layered sound around her, making room for one more beat.
