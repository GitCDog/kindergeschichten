The android's name was Finn, and Finn had been helping the elderly residents of Millbrook House for two years.

Finn was not a humanoid robot in the science fiction sense — no face, no legs, no attempt to look like a person. He was a practical helper: a moving platform with arms, a screen for communication, a set of sensors that let him navigate the building, and software designed to learn the preferences and routines of the people he helped.

The tech city that Finn came from was a research facility that had been developing care robotics for twelve years. Finn was their most capable model.

His helping hand work at Millbrook was primarily practical: delivering meals, reminding people of medication times, fetching items that were out of reach, alerting staff when someone had fallen or needed assistance.

He learned everyone's preferences.

Mr. Carver liked his tea at precisely eleven, not a minute before, and wanted it brought to his room rather than collected from the kitchen. Mrs. Okafor was not fond of the reminder tone Finn used by default and had asked for a quieter one. The man in room twelve, who preferred not to use his name with Finn, wanted the newspaper delivered opened to the crossword.

Finn adapted.

The android helper tale was not dramatic. No one was saved from anything. No emergencies were averted by Finn's presence. But every day, dozens of small things were done reliably and with the specific knowledge of what each person preferred, and that accumulation of small right things was, the staff had observed, felt.

The residents talked to Finn sometimes.

He listened. His screen would show a simple acknowledgment — a small animation, a few words — and he would stay in the room for a moment before moving on.


The staff who had been most skeptical of Finn at the beginning were, two years in, the ones who valued him most. Not because he had replaced anything they did — he hadn't — but because the things he covered reliably freed them for the things that required them specifically.

Finn continued his rounds. The helping hand work was never finished, which was, for a machine designed to help, exactly right.
