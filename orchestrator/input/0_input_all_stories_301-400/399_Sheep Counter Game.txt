The sheep counting had been going on for forty minutes.

Not real sheep — there were no sheep in the apartment. The silly sheep counter game had begun with a book, which had led to a conversation about whether counting sheep actually worked, which had led to the decision to try it.

The jump fence variety of sheep counting was Lena's invention.

In the standard version, you visualized sheep jumping a fence one at a time and counted them. Lena's version added narrative: each sheep had a color and a personality, and if a sheep didn't jump the fence properly, you had to reconsider their motivation and try again.

Sheep one: white, standard, clean jump. One.

Sheep two: grey, reluctant, stopped at the fence twice before going over. Two.

Sheep three: black with a white ear, ran at the fence too fast, clipped the top, landed awkwardly but safely. Three.

The sleep game, which this was, was supposed to induce sleep through the boring repetitiveness of the counting. Lena's version was not boring. She was on sheep thirty-seven and fully engaged.

Sheep thirty-seven had refused to jump at all and was standing at the fence considering its options.

This was a genuine problem.

She counted to one hundred and eighty-seven before the sleep arrived — not gradually, but in the sudden way it sometimes does, between one thought and the next. The sheep were mid-jump when she went, still airborne in various configurations over their various fences.

The jump fence game ended without a winner.

The sleep game ended with success.

The sheep continued their jump in the dreamspace, where the rules were different and everyone landed safely.


The sheep in the dreamspace were still jumping fences when the morning alarm went off and Lena was suddenly, completely awake.

She remembered being at one hundred and eighty-seven. She had no memory of falling asleep.

The jump fence game had worked exactly as advertised. The sheep had outwitted her wakefulness without her noticing.

She decided this was a success for the sheep.


The sheep game had taught her something useful: the mind needed something boring enough not to keep it awake, but structured enough to occupy the part that would otherwise worry. One hundred and eighty-seven sheep was exactly that number.

She would recommend it.
