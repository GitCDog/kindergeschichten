The vine was as old as the tree it lived on, which was old enough that nobody living remembered when either had been young.

It hung from the highest branch of a strangler fig that had, over many decades, overtaken the original tree beneath it entirely. The vine itself was thick as a forearm and rough-barked, with a natural loop at the bottom where it had grown around a branch that had long since rotted away, leaving the loop as if it had been designed for small hands to grip.

It had not been designed. But it served perfectly.

The tiny vine swing was how the children of the family who lived at the forest edge came to understand flight. Not airplane flight. The older, more fundamental kind — the kind where your body is in the air and the earth is below you and the only thing keeping you aloft is a loop of living wood.

The tree climb to reach the launch point was half the experience. The vine hung beside the tree's main trunk, and to swing on it properly you had to climb to the branch above and drop down, catching the vine at the moment you passed it.

Getting that moment right took practice.

Once caught, the green ride carried you out in an arc across the small clearing below the fig tree — out over the ferns and the mossy stones, ten feet up, the green world swinging beneath you in a wide sweep. Then the arc reversed, and you swung back, and at the perfect moment you let go and landed on the soft earth.

Or you held on and went again.

The older cousin had learned, after years of riding the vine, how to generate a second arc by pumping her legs — extending the time in the air, going higher, the green ride becoming something more than its original trajectory.

She showed the younger ones how.

They went higher.


By evening, the youngest child had made it to the launch branch alone. She stood there for a long time — longer than the older cousin had expected — looking at the arc and thinking.

Then she reached for the vine and took the green ride.

She didn't let go at the end. She swung back, and forward again, and stayed in the air for much longer than she had planned, which was exactly right.
