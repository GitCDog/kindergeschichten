The ocean crest was exactly the right height.

Too small and there was no jump. Too large and you were swimming through rather than over. But at chest height — at the height this wave was as it rolled toward the beach — the jump was perfect. You could see it coming, time your approach, and be airborne for just long enough to feel it.

The playful wave jumper's name was Sol, and Sol had been jumping waves on this beach for seven summers.

She had a system.

It involved reading the wave set — the sequence of waves that arrived in groups, each set slightly different in timing and height. Not every wave in a set was jumpable. The first wave in a set was often the highest, the water bounce the most dramatic. The middle waves were more reliable. The last wave in the set, as it passed and the water pulled back, was the moment to reposition.

Sol counted.

One, two, three — first wave, jump. Airborne, land, stand. The water bounce forward, knees absorbing.

Four, five — middle wave, solid jump. Water laugh from Jess beside her who got the timing wrong and went through instead of over.

The seventh wave in this set was the best.

Sol saw it coming from farther out — a slightly higher line, moving with more purpose. She adjusted her position in the surf, the sand shifting under her feet in the pull of the wave before it.

Then jump.

The water crest caught her at hip height and lifted — genuinely lifted, a brief weightlessness — and she came down on the far side as the water rushed beneath her and shallowed into the foam of the shore.

She turned back to sea.

The next set was already forming at the horizon.


Sol stayed in the surf until her fingers pruned, which she used as a reasonable measure of enough. The water bounce of the shore was slower now, the set spacing out as the tide changed.

She walked up the beach to the towel, sat down, and looked back at the ocean.

Thirty-two jumps, by her count, though she may have lost track after twenty. A good wave jump afternoon, by any standard.
