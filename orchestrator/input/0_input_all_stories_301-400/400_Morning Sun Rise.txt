The curious morning sun arrived at five forty-two.

This was not a surprise — the morning sun arrived every day, as it had for four and a half billion years, which was a record of consistency that was hard to beat. What was surprising was that someone was awake to see it.

The girl's name was Bea, and she had woken early for no particular reason and had stayed awake for the day start the way she sometimes did in summer — not reluctantly, not excitedly, but with a calm interest in what the morning was going to do.

The golden rise began as a line of color at the horizon.

Not orange, exactly — the pre-sunrise color was its own thing, a warm red-gold that was specific to those few minutes before the disc appeared. It lit the underside of any cloud in the east and turned the top of the hill visible from Bea's window into a dark silhouette against something bright.

Then the sun itself.

The leading edge appeared first — a bright point, quickly becoming an arc, then a semicircle, then the full circle of the sun's disc clearing the horizon. It took approximately two minutes from first point to fully risen.

Bea watched the whole sequence.

She had seen it before — a dozen times, maybe more — and it was never the same, because the clouds were always different and the quality of the light depended on the humidity and the dust in the air, and two minutes of a summer morning were never exactly two minutes of any other summer morning.

The golden rise was complete.

The morning sun was up.

Bea looked at the light on the ceiling of her room — warm and gold and slanted, exactly the light of early morning — and felt the particular contentment of having watched something begin.


Bea stayed at the window until the golden rise was fully established and the light had changed from the special morning quality to the ordinary brightness of mid-morning.

She made a small mark in her journal: date, time, quality of the sunrise (clear, good color, thin clouds in the east).

The day start had been noted. The sun was up.

She went down for breakfast, the morning still new.
