Behind the waterfall, where the falling water made a curtain of white noise between the cave and the outside world, a small bat named Pip had made his home.

The cave was not large — about the size of two rooms — but it was perfectly situated. The waterfall kept predators away (nothing wanted to pass through a wall of falling water) and the hidden glow of the cave in morning, when the sunlight hit the waterfall at the right angle and the refracted light filled the cave with rainbow, was the most beautiful thing Pip had ever seen.

The echo song was his navigation system.

Pip sent out sounds — thin, high-frequency pulses that human ears couldn't catch — and listened for what came back. The cave's interior was perfectly known to him through this system. He knew every stalactite and ledge and crack. He knew where the waterfall sound entered and how it reflected off the back wall.

He used the echo to hunt, too. Small insects, drawn to the cave's shelter, would venture in from the waterfall curtain, and Pip would track them through the dark using nothing but their acoustic reflections.

One evening, a girl reached through the waterfall.

She had been looking at it from outside and had wondered what was behind it, and she had simply — with the particular directness of children who wonder things — put her hand through.

Pip, hanging on his ledge, watched the hand appear and withdraw.

Then, after a pause, the girl herself appeared — soaking wet, eyes wide, standing in his cave looking at the waterfall from behind.

The light through the water made her face glow with moving colors.

"Oh," she said. Just the one word.

The echo song carried her voice back to her, softened and strange, as if the cave itself were answering.

She stood there for a long time, dripping and entirely still.


The girl came back twice more that summer. Each time she stood behind the waterfall until she was fully soaked and fully present, watching the light make its rainbow inside the cave.

On the third visit, Pip watched her from his ledge. The girl had learned to stand still, which was the right approach. She was beginning to understand the cave on its own terms rather than her own.

He blinked once, in what might have been approval.

[pause] Good night my little friend
