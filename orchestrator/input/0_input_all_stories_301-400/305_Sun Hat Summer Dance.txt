In a field at the edge of the village, every summer, the hats came out.

This was not a formal arrangement. It had simply accumulated over the years, the way traditions do: one summer a woman had worn a wide straw sun hat to the field while she read, and found that the shade dance of the brim moving in the breeze was so pleasant that she came back the next day. And the next. And she told her neighbor, who brought her own hat.

Now, on every sunny summer Saturday, a cheerful gathering of sun hats assembled in the field.

The champion of this gathering was a hat named Brim.

Brim was wide and golden, woven from pale straw, with a ribbon of forget-me-not blue that had faded somewhat over the years but retained its essential blueness. She had been in twenty-two summers and showed the dignity of experience: slightly curved at the brim from years of shaping to the particular angle that gave the best shade, with a small repair on the left side where the weave had loosened in a wet summer and been carefully re-tucked.

The shade dance was something Brim had learned to perform in wind.

When the summer field breeze arrived from the west, Brim would tip slightly forward on her wearer's head, casting a longer shadow. A stronger gust would tip her back, and the shadow would shorten, and the sun would catch the straw in a way that made it glow.

Children who played in the field would sometimes stop to watch the hats move in the wind.

"Why do they dance?" one child asked.

A grandmother who was reading under Brim said, "Because summer is short and the wind is generous."

The child thought about this and then ran off, which seemed like a reasonable response.

By evening, the hats were carried home. The field returned to its ordinary self. But the grass still held the gentle impressions where the hat-wearers had sat, and the shade patches where each brim had danced lingered for a few minutes after they were gone.
