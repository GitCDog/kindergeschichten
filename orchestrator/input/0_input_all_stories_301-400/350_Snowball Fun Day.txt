The snow had arrived overnight and was exactly the right kind: not too dry, not too wet, with a thin crust on top from the brief warm spell at midday that had subsequently refrozen. Packing snow. Throwing snow. Snowball snow.

The playful snowball named Round came into existence at nine in the morning when a girl named Bea scooped up her first handful.

A snowball starts as a decision.

Bea packed the snow between her gloved palms, adding more, turning it, pressing the surface tight. Round grew from nothing — first a handful, then a small sphere, then something you had to use two hands for.

The throw game began with Bea and her brother Sam trading throws across the back garden. Neither was a particularly accurate thrower at this range, which meant the game was mostly about the misses — the puffs of snow where a ball landed in a drift, the spectacular spray when one hit the fence.

Neighbors joined.

By eleven o'clock there were seven children in the garden, the original Bea-and-Sam game having expanded organically as others appeared with coats and scarves and the universal recognition that this was a snowball morning and you had to be involved.

The fort was proposed at eleven-fifteen and construction began immediately.

Round did not survive to see the fort. He had been thrown — a good throw, it should be said, that caught Sam between the shoulder blades with a satisfying thump — and had burst into his component snow, adding himself back to the general stock.

Other snowballs came after him.

The fort went up. The winter fun reached its midday peak. Someone's hot chocolate appeared from inside at some point and was consumed in cold gloved hands.

By afternoon the snow was trampled and the fort was losing its corners and the children were satisfied.


The last activity of the day was making a snowman at the garden's edge, which used the remaining good snow and gave everyone something to agree about.

By evening it had one coal eye — the other was missing and no one could remember when — and a stick arm pointing at the sky for reasons that had seemed important during construction and were already forgotten.

It was a good snowman. The winter fun day had been complete.
