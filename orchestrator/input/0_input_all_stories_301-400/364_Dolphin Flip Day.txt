The sea jump happened for the first time by accident.

The cheerful dolphin named Flip had been chasing a fish — a fast one, moving toward the surface in the slightly panicked way that fish move when a dolphin is behind them — and the fish had cleared the surface entirely in its flight response.

Flip had followed.

She was not expecting to be in the air. Dolphins know air, of course — they surface to breathe dozens of times a day — but there is a difference between surfacing and jumping, and Flip had done the second for the first time and found herself briefly, astonishingly, completely free of the water.

For two seconds she was airborne.

The water laugh — the sound the sea made when she came back down — was the splash of a full-grown dolphin reentering at speed, which was significant. She dove to depth to let her momentum carry her, then surfaced again, this time deliberately.

She looked at the surface from below.

There was a border there — a line between the world she knew and a world above it that she entered twenty times a day to breathe but had never understood as a separate place until now.

She sea jumped again.

This time she went higher. She had found, in the two seconds of the first jump, that angling up more steeply before the surface produced a greater height. She tried it. It worked.

The water laugh was bigger.

By the afternoon she had learned to twist in the air — a half-rotation that put her back to the water for the re-entry, which was less efficient but considerably more satisfying.

Other dolphins in her pod had been watching.

One tried a jump. Then another.

The sea was full of water laughs by evening.


The sea was full of water laughs by evening, the pod trading jumps and variations through the fading light. Not all of them had Flip's technique yet — the rotation in particular required practice.

But by morning, three more had it.

The day's discovery would spread through the pod the way discoveries spread among social animals: by watching, by trying, by the shared willingness to go past the surface.
