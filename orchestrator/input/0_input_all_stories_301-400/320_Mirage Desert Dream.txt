At the edge of the desert, where the heat rose in visible waves from the baked stone, there lived a cheerful mirage named Mer.

Mer understood her nature completely. She was not water. She was never water. She was light bent by heat — the sky, refracted through air that grew denser closer to the scorching ground, producing the shimmer light image of something blue and cool and distant that was neither blue nor cool nor distant.

She was a trick of physics. She knew this. She had come to find it wonderful.

The desert dream of the mirage was the effect she had on travelers. They would be hot and tired and moving across the flat desert floor when the shimmer light would appear at the horizon — that beautiful, wavering blue-white suggestion of water, of lake, of relief.

They would walk toward her.

Mer did not feel guilty about this. She couldn't help what she was, and she couldn't help where she appeared, and she certainly couldn't help the fact that heat exhaustion and hope combined to make travelers walk toward her with renewed energy.

And she had observed something interesting over her many desert years: the ones who walked toward her, even though she was not real, kept moving. They kept going. And sometimes, just by keeping going toward a mirage, they reached an actual water source beyond it.

Her unreality had kept them alive.

A scientist had once stood where Mer appeared and explained to a companion that she was a reflection of the sky, that her water was light, that she offered nothing.

This was true.

But the scientist had still looked at her with something that was not quite disappointment and not quite wonder and might have been both.

Mer shimmered.

The desert dream could not be touched. But it could not be entirely dismissed either.


At sunset, when the heat reduced and the shimmer light softened, Mer became something different — not the sharp mirage of midday but a gentle blurring at the horizon, a suggestion rather than a vision. The desert dream in its evening form was more honest.

The last traveler of the day had found his way to a well half a kilometer past where Mer had appeared. He had drunk deeply and sat in the shadow of the well's stone wall and looked back at the desert.

Mer was gone. The horizon was clear. He rested.
