The rope bridge had been there as long as the rope bridge needed to be there, which was to say: since the two sides of the river gorge had needed connecting and no other method was practical.

The friendly bridge's name — because the villagers on both sides called it something — was Span. Span was made of four main cables of twisted rope, with crosspieces of split bamboo, and handrails on each side that were thinner ropes tied at intervals to the mains.

The step sway was what everyone who crossed Span remembered most.

Each step transferred a little motion to the bridge, and the motion accumulated across the length until Span was swinging gently from side to side in a rhythm that varied by how many people were crossing and how they were walking. Experienced crossers walked in a slight stagger, off the beat of the natural sway, which helped. Beginners walked carefully in the center, which slowed things down but was perfectly safe.

The river cross was sixty meters at this point — wide enough that the far side felt genuinely distant at the start and genuinely close at the end, which was satisfying.

A child crossing for the first time held the handrail with both hands and moved very slowly.

The river below was green-brown and moved steadily.

The child stopped at the middle and looked down.

Span moved gently under them — not alarmingly, just truthfully, because a rope bridge moves and doesn't pretend otherwise.

"Don't look down," said an older cousin from the other side.

The child looked down for another moment anyway.

"It's a long way," the child called back.

"Don't think about that. Think about where you're going."

The child thought about the other side and began walking.


The child reached the far side and turned back to look at Span.

The river cross looked longer from this side. The bridge looked thinner. The sway that had seemed terrifying from the middle looked, from the end, like something entirely manageable.

Most things did, from the end.

'Again?' the cousin asked.

The child thought about it seriously. Then walked back onto the bridge.
