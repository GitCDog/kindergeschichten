In all the desert, no one paid less attention to a single sand grain than the other sand grains.

This was simply the nature of deserts: when you are one of trillions, individuality is not the organizing principle. You are part of a dune, part of a flow, part of the great shifting geography of sand — never a specific thing but always a component of something vast.

The curious sand grain who thought about this most often had no name, because sand grains don't have names. But if she had had one, it would have been something like Sil, because she was silicate, mostly, with a thin coating of iron oxide that gave her a faint amber tinge.

The tiny path began the day the wind changed.

Sil had been near the surface of a large dune for approximately thirty years, which was recent in sand grain terms. A shift in the prevailing wind direction lifted her — just her, briefly alone — off the dune's crest and carried her six meters before setting her down again.

In six meters she had seen: the underside of a hawk's wing, the exact shadow of a thorn bush, the way the desert floor looked from two meters up (flatter than it seemed from ground level), and a brief, dazzling view of the mountains at the horizon before she came down.

None of this was remarkable.

But in the microseconds of the desert world flight, Sil had been singular. Not one of trillions. One.

She came to rest in a new position, a small distance from her previous dune, settled among strangers. The wind had made a tiny path that only she had traveled.

Tomorrow the wind would move her again, or not. She would be part of a dune again, or the face of one.

But for those six meters, she had been exactly, specifically, herself.


The desert wind returned three nights later and moved Sil again — twelve meters this time, over the crest of a new dune and down the other side, which she had not seen before.

The tiny path grew longer. Each wind added to it. Each landing was a new position, a new set of neighbors, a new angle on the mountains and the sky and the small, specific geography of a single grain's world.

She moved through the desert one tiny path at a time, going everywhere and nowhere, which was exactly enough.
