The river had a spirit, as all rivers do, and her name was Sona.

Sona was not visible in any ordinary way. She was not a figure standing at the riverbank or a face in the water. She was the river — its current, its sound, its temperature, the particular way it bent around the large boulder at the third curve, the way it widened at the flood plain and narrowed at the gorge.

The flow song was what travelers heard when they camped by the water at night.

People called it different things. Some called it the sound of the water. Some called it the rapids. Some said it was the wind in the willows along the bank. But those who had traveled rivers their whole lives knew that each river had a distinct voice, and that voice was the spirit singing.

Sona's song changed with the season.

In spring, when the snowmelt swelled her banks, her flow song was fast and high and urgent — a rushing, tumbling sound that carried the urgency of volume and cold. In summer, when she ran low and slow, her water whisper was intimate and warm, the sound of water talking to stones it had known for centuries.

In autumn she was something else entirely: a medium note, neither fast nor slow, with a particular quality that people described differently depending on who they were. Some said it sounded like remembering. Some said it sounded like readiness.

A fisherman named Ren had sat beside Sona for forty years.

One evening he said to his apprentice: "Listen to what the river is telling you."

The apprentice listened.

"I can't tell what it's saying," the apprentice admitted.

"Not saying," Ren said. "Singing. And you don't translate a song. You just let it reach you."

The apprentice closed his eyes and let the water whisper move through him.

After a while, something in him settled.

The river ran on.


The apprentice came back to the river many times that autumn. He didn't always fish. Sometimes he just sat and listened to the water whisper change as the level dropped and the stones it moved over changed with it.

He was learning to hear the difference between one river song and another. It took a long time — Ren had said it might take years — but he was patient.

The river had been singing longer than anyone. It would wait for him to catch up.
