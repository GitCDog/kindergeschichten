The tiger cub's name, if tigers used names for each other — which they didn't, but if they had — would have been something quick and orange, because that was what he was: quick and orange and still learning what those things meant in practical terms.

The stripe path through the jungle was the cub's practice ground.

His mother had shown him the route twice: a circuit of approximately two kilometers through the dense undergrowth, following the dry streambed for its first section, then cutting through the bamboo stand, then up along the ridge where you could see the valley below, then back down through the fern zone to the base rock where they denned.

He was running it alone for the first time.

The jungle run was different at cub-height than it was at adult tiger height. The ferns were taller relative to him. The bamboo was denser, requiring more shoulder-work to push through. The ridge, which his mother cleared in four easy strides, took him eight.

But the dry streambed was perfect.

He found, on the streambed, the particular joy of running fast on a clear surface — his legs working in the elastic, ground-covering stride that tigers are built for, the stripe path of dried clay smooth under his paws. He went faster than he had meant to and had to slow down before the curve where the streambed bent left, because he hadn't yet learned to carry speed through curves.

He would learn.

He reached the base rock in better time than he had expected.

His mother was waiting. She looked at him with the calm assessment of an expert evaluating a student — not unkindly, but precisely.

She turned and led him back on the route again.

The second run was better.


By the fifth solo run, the cub had the ridge section sorted — not matching his mother's four-stride efficiency, but a consistent seven that he could sustain without losing pace. Progress was precise and honest in these things.

The jungle run became his evening ritual. He ran it in rain and he ran it in heat. He ran it when the bamboo was thick and when it had been thinned by a fallen tree.

He learned the stripe path the way his mother knew it: through the body, not the mind.
