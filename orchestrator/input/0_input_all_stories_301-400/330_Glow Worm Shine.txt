In a garden where the grass grew long at the edges and the stone wall was softened by decades of moss, the glow worms came out when the night was warm and the summer was at its deepest.

The tiny glow worm who lived in the moss at the base of the wall had no name, because glow worms don't have names. But she had a light.

The night light she produced came from a chemical reaction in her abdomen — bioluminescence, the same phenomenon that made deep-sea fish glow, that made certain jellyfish shine. In a glow worm it was a steady, cool green — not a flash like a firefly, but a continuous, patient glow.

She held it out into the dark.

The forest shine of a glow worm colony — when many were glowing at once — was one of the quieter marvels of the natural world. Not spectacular from a distance. Intimate. The kind of thing you had to be close to see.

Tonight there were eleven glow worms in the garden wall's moss, and their combined light made the stone seem to breathe.

A family walking home from the village saw it.

The father stopped first. Then the mother. The children walked ahead and then came back when they realized their parents had stopped moving.

"What is it?" the youngest asked.

"Glow worms," the father said quietly. "Don't go closer."

They stood at the gate and looked. The small green lights held steady in the moss — not moving, not flickering, just present. The wall glowed in its ancient stone sections with a light that had no mechanical source, no battery, no switch.

Living light, the father had read once, was the oldest light.

The family stood until they were cold and then walked home, but slowly, and looking back.


The family stood at the gate until they were cold. Then they walked home slowly and quietly, the way you do when you've seen something you want to keep close.

The glow worms shone on without an audience, as they always had. The night light they made was not for watching. It was for each other — a conversation in cool green, held in the moss, in the summer dark.

The stone wall breathed its living light until nearly midnight.
