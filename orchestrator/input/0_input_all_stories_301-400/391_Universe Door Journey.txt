The magic universe door was not where most people expected to find it.

They looked for it in spectacular places: at the centers of galaxies, in the extreme conditions near black holes, at the edges of the observable universe where space itself was doing strange things. These were all reasonable guesses, based on reasonable assumptions about where a door to elsewhere might be kept.

The door was in a garden shed in Hertfordshire.

The shed belonged to an elderly woman named Mrs. Orr, who had never noticed the door. The shed had an ordinary door that faced west, and then, in the corner behind the lawnmower, what appeared to be a faint rectangular outline in the wooden wall that did not correspond to any structural feature.

This was the portal jump.

It had been there for forty years, which Mrs. Orr would have known if she had been the kind of person to look at the backs of garden sheds carefully, which she was not.

The child who found it was a girl named Sam, who had gone into the shed to retrieve a football and had noticed the outline while waiting for her eyes to adjust to the dim interior.

She pressed on the outline.

The rectangle lit up, very faintly.

She considered her options.

Then pressed harder.

The door opened inward, which was impossible given that the shed's exterior wall was there. But the door opened inward, and through it was not the garden exterior but a place of lights — point sources of light in a warm dark, too numerous to count, the world travel of interstellar space visible as something you could step into.

Sam stood in the door for a long time.

Then she took one step.

Then another.


Sam took seven steps into the light before the portal jump began to close. She turned and saw the rectangle of the shed interior shrinking.

She stepped back through.

The outline in the shed wall was just an outline again — faint, rectangular, no light behind it.

She stood in the shed for a long time with the lawnmower and the smell of cut grass and the knowledge of the seven steps she had taken into somewhere else.
