In the deep heart of winter, when most living things had retreated into sleep, a tiny frost sprite named Floss did her most important work.

Floss was responsible for the ice petals.

Ice petals were what happened when frost found a window — a proper frost, the kind that came at midnight when the temperature dropped fast and the moisture in the air had nowhere else to go. Floss would arrive at the glass and, with the very tip of one crystalline finger, draw the first line.

From a single line, everything else followed.

The ice petals grew outward from that first mark in fractal branches — each branch splitting into two, each of those splitting again, building into the most intricate patterns that most people slept through and only noticed in the morning, already half-melted in the winter sun.

Floss preferred them at their peak.

She would step back from the finished window — hovering in the air outside, her breath making tiny frost clouds — and look at what she had made. No two windows were ever the same. The pattern depended on the glass, the temperature, the moisture content of the air, the exact angle of the first line.

The winter bloom was different every night.

One window she particularly loved belonged to a girl named Bea, who had asked — seriously, as a serious question — whether the frost patterns were art.

Floss had considered this. She didn't make them for decoration. She made them because frost made them. She was simply the beginning of the process.

But the girl's question stayed with her.

On Bea's window that winter, Floss drew her finest first line — not straight, but curved, the gentlest possible arc — and watched what grew from it.

The pattern that developed over the next two hours was the most beautiful ice petals she had ever made. Not because she had tried harder, but because she had asked herself the question while she worked.

Whether that made it art, she still wasn't sure.

But it was the best thing she had ever made.
