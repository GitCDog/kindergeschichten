The moon rock had been sitting on the shelf of the geology lab for forty-three years.

It had been collected during a lunar mission, bagged and numbered and returned to Earth, and had subsequently lived out its museum-adjacent life in a series of storage and display situations before arriving at the university geology department, where it sat in a locked case with a small placard that said: LUNAR BASALT, COLLECTED 1972. DO NOT TOUCH.

The silly moon rock's name — the students had given it one, which was against protocol but had happened anyway — was Skip.

Skip was not actually skip-shaped. He was an irregular fragment about the size of a large potato, dark grey with small crystalline inclusions that caught the light when the display case's built-in bulb hit them at the right angle. He had traveled two hundred and thirty-eight thousand miles and was now behind glass in a climate-controlled room.

The bounce step of Skip's imagination — the thing the students talked about when they discussed him — was the low-gravity environment he had come from. On the Moon, the gravitational acceleration was one-sixth of Earth's. You could jump six times higher for the same effort.

A person who could jump a meter on Earth could jump six meters on the Moon.

Skip, being a rock, had not jumped. Rocks did not jump even on the Moon, unless thrown.

But the astronaut who had picked Skip up from the lunar regolith had been moving in the low-gravity bounce step that was the Moon's specific gift to anyone walking there — each footfall rising higher than Earth-walking intuition expected, each step a small, slow, extended arc.

Skip had been collected mid-bounce.

He was, in a sense, a moment of low gravity preserved in rock.

The students thought this was the best thing about him.


The student who had named Skip wrote his name on the inside cover of his lab notebook and thought, not for the first time, about the person who had picked up this specific rock from the lunar regolith in 1972.

That person had bounced across the surface to collect it. The low-gravity bounce step had brought a specific hand to a specific rock in a specific moment.

Skip sat behind his glass, holding that moment, forty-three years on.
