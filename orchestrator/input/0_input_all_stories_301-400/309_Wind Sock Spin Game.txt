At the end of the small airfield runway, attached to a tall orange pole, there was a wind sock named Windy who took her job very seriously.

Windy's purpose was to show the pilots which way the wind was blowing and how hard. A gentle breeze made her extend partly, pointing softly in the direction the air was going. A strong wind extended her fully, making her stream horizontal and taut as a fish on a line.

But the best moments — the moments Windy lived for — were the gusts.

A gust would arrive suddenly, and Windy would spin. Not a full rotation — wind socks are attached at one end, always — but a fast, spiraling surge that made her whole fabric body ripple and twist in a way that she privately thought of as the sky spin. It was better than being still. It was better even than the steady pointing she did on days with a consistent breeze.

The spin game had its own rhythm.

Gust: surge, ripple, twist, settle.

Gust again: surge, ripple — this one longer — settle more slowly.

A third gust: and now the breeze play was continuous, not quite letting her settle before the next push arrived, so she was in constant low-level motion, her striped orange fabric dancing in a slow, sustained oscillation.

A small plane taxied past and the pilot looked at Windy to check conditions before takeoff.

Windy was pointing at eleven o'clock — wind from the northwest, moderate.

The pilot gave a thumbs-up through the windshield.

Windy didn't have hands, but she rippled once in acknowledgment.

The plane turned into the wind and accelerated down the runway. Windy watched it lift off — a moment she always found moving, despite having seen it hundreds of times. Something that was on the ground and then, with the help of the very thing she measured, was in the air.

The wind carried the sound of the engine away.

Windy returned to her steady point, northwest, moderate.


The afternoon brought a shift — the wind swung south and Windy pivoted smoothly to follow it, her fabric settling into the new direction without complaint. Pivoting was easy. That was the thing about being a wind sock: you never argued with the wind. You simply showed it truly.

A second plane taxied out at four o'clock. Windy was pointing south-southwest, light to moderate.

The pilot checked and gave her the same thumbs-up as always.
