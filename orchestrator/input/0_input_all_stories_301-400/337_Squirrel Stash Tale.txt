The squirrel's name was Pip, and Pip was the most organized creature in the wood — except for the part where she forgot where she had put things.

This was the fundamental squirrel paradox: meticulous in the stash, unreliable in the retrieval. Pip would spend careful October afternoons burying acorns and beechnuts and hazel cobs in precisely selected locations, pushing each one exactly two centimeters into the soil, pressing the earth flat over the top, then turning around three times — a ritual that seemed to help, somehow, without being scientifically defensible.

The nut pile at the base of the big chestnut was her central store. This one she never forgot. It held thirty-seven nuts packed efficiently together, accessible through a small earth tunnel Pip had engineered over two seasons.

The distributed caches were less reliable.

Winter prep for a squirrel was partly the stashing itself and partly an ongoing program of optimistic searching. Pip would return to areas where she had a feeling she had buried something, dig tentatively at three or four spots, find what appeared to be someone else's nut twice, find her own nut once, and lose the thread entirely by the fourth attempt.

This was normal. The nut pile covered the uncertainty.

What Pip had not accounted for was the bird.

A jay — one of those species that was as good at caching as squirrels, and significantly better at finding — had apparently been watching Pip's autumn stashing operation with professional interest. The jay had subsequently retrieved six of Pip's distributed caches.

Pip had noticed the shortfall and responded with a new caching technique: burying false acorn-shaped stones in the most visible locations, reserving the real nuts for spots she found and immediately forgot.

The jay found the stones.

Was puzzled.

The actual nuts remained buried.

Winter was going to be fine.


Pip spent February visiting the nut pile three times a week and the distributed caches whenever she had a feeling about a location. The jay had given up on the false stones and was working a different section of the wood.

The winter prep had held. The stores were sufficient.

In March she would begin the spring work: clearing the nut pile, rebuilding energy, watching for the first hazels to start buds. But March was still weeks away, and the woodpile was warm, and the nut pile was exactly where she'd left it.
