The volcano had been dormant for two hundred years, which meant it was sleeping rather than dead.

The difference was important. A dead volcano was a geological past tense: it had finished. A dormant volcano was a present tense that had paused, its eruption tale incomplete, its fire core still alive in the sense that the magma below was still there, still hot, still moving in its slow thermal cycles.

The brave volcano's name, in the local tradition, was Fumo, and Fumo's fire core ran three hundred kilometers deep.

The people of the valley below had lived with Fumo for generations. They understood dormancy because they had always known it — the slight sulfur smell on east wind days, the warm springs on the mountain's south side, the ground that vibrated very faintly during certain seasons.

These were not warning signs. They were Fumo's ordinary state: alive but resting.

A geologist named Dr. Ava had come to study Fumo's fire core using seismic instruments that could map the magma chamber below the surface. She spent three months on the mountain, placing sensors in a ring around the summit.

The data she collected was, she told her colleagues, the most complete picture of a dormant volcano's interior she had ever assembled.

The fire core was active — movement in the chamber, heat cycling, gas accumulation. But the conduit to the surface was sealed by the solidified lava of the last eruption, and the pressure below was not sufficient to break it.

Two hundred years of sleep.

The eruption tale was not over. It was simply paused at a particularly long breath.

Dr. Ava completed her study and went back to the university. Fumo went on breathing his slow, sulfurous breath into the sky above the valley.


Fumo's slow breath continued after Dr. Ava left. The sulfur smell on east wind days. The warm springs on the south side, where people came from the village to soak in the water that the fire core heated from below.

The eruption tale was incomplete. It had paused at two hundred years and would pause for longer still, or would not.

The valley below was green and growing and attentive to the mountain in the particular way that people are attentive to things they live alongside and cannot fully predict.
