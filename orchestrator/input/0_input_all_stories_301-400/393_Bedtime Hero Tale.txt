The bedtime hero's name was Dusk, and his night quest began when the house went quiet.

This was not the complete quiet of the middle of the night — it was the settling quiet of eight-thirty, when the last footsteps had crossed the landing, when the last door had closed, when the sounds of the day had been replaced by the sounds of a house at rest.

Dusk patrolled.

His route was fixed by long practice: start at the landing, check the stairs, move to the living room, the kitchen, the back door. At each point he would assess — was everything in order? — and move on, satisfied or note.

He was a cat.

The night quest of a house cat was invisible to the family who shared the house with him, partly because they were asleep when most of it happened, and partly because they thought of him as someone who slept most of the time. This was partially true. He did sleep most of the time.

But the shadow win — the nightly satisfaction of a territory checked and secured — required that he also be awake some of the time.

The route tonight was uneventful. No intruders. No unusual smells. The back door was sealed against the cold. The kitchen floor was clean, which was not a security consideration but a comfortable thing to walk on.

He reached the landing.

The child's door was slightly open, as it always was. Dusk pushed through and jumped onto the foot of the bed.

The child didn't wake.

He circled once and settled, his weight warming the duvet, his purring too soft to be heard but felt through the mattress.

The bedtime hero was in position.

The house was safe.


Dusk stayed at the foot of the bed until two in the morning, when a sound outside — a fox, probably — pulled him to the window. He assessed the situation (fox, harmless), completed the rest of his patrol circuit, and returned to the child's room.

He settled again at the foot of the bed.

The shadow win of a quiet night was the best kind. Nothing had needed heroism. Everything had simply been kept.
