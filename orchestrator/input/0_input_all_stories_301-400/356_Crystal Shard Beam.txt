On the windowsill of the geology room at the school, between a chunk of iron pyrite and a cross-section of ammonite, there was a curious crystal shard named Ray.

Ray was quartz — a clear, double-terminated crystal about fifteen centimeters long, slightly smoky at one end and perfectly clear at the other. He had come from a mine in Brazil, passed through many hands, and arrived at the school as a donation ten years ago.

The light split was his great talent.

When the morning sun hit Ray at the right angle — which happened between nine and nine-thirty on clear days — the light entered his clear end, traveled through the crystal's internal geometry, and split on the way out. Not into a full spectrum the way a prism did, but into two or three displaced beams that moved across the wall in lazy arcs as the sun climbed.

The color beam from quartz was subtle. Not the dramatic rainbow of a cut glass prism. More like the ghost of a rainbow — the same colors but faint, as if the crystal were being polite about its refractive abilities.

Students who noticed the beams on the wall would sometimes stand still and watch them move.

The movement was slow — the sun's arc was slow — but it was consistent. You could watch one beam's position against the wall and it would be visibly different after five minutes. The crystal was a clock, of a kind, tracking the sun's climb with light.

A teacher had explained this to the class once and then stood at the front watching the color beam with the same attention she brought to everything she genuinely found interesting, which was extensive.

Ray sat on the windowsill and light split through him.

The room was quiet and ordinary, and made briefly extraordinary by a fifteen-centimeter piece of rock.


At nine-twenty the sun had moved past the angle that activated Ray's refraction, and the color beams on the wall were gone.

But the student who had been watching was late to the first class of the day and had to explain why, and the explanation — 'I was watching a crystal split light' — was accepted without question by a teacher who understood that some things were worth being late for.

Ray sat on the sill and waited for tomorrow.
