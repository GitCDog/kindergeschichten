The pine cone had been falling since noon.

Not straight down — it was September and the wind was working, and the curious pine cone had separated from the branch in a gust and was making the most of the descent. It spun. This was not accidental: the pine cone's shape, with its overlapping scales arranged in a specific mathematical pattern, interacted with the air in a way that generated rotation.

The fall spin began as the cone separated from the branch and was still gaining momentum when it cleared the tree line.

At height, the forest floor was a green-brown blur. The spin gave it a kaleidoscope quality — the same view, rotating.

The pine cone landed with a soft, final impact in the leaf litter at the base of its parent tree.

Forty years of growth, sealed in scales. Seeds between each scale — two of them, each with a small papery wing — ready for the next stage.

A squirrel found the pine cone within the hour.

The squirrel bit it experimentally, found nothing edible at this stage — the seeds were too tightly enclosed — and moved on.

A child found it two days later. She picked it up and turned it in her hands, feeling the geometry of the scales — the spiral arrangements that mathematicians had a name for, that architects tried to replicate, that had simply grown this way because this way was most efficient.

She threw it in the air and caught it.

Then threw it higher and watched the forest floor spin, for a moment, exactly as it had seemed to spin for the pine cone on the way down.

She kept it. It ended up on her windowsill between a stone from the beach and a button from a coat she had outgrown.

The pine cone's long fall had ended somewhere much smaller than the forest, and perfectly good.


The parent pine stood above the spot where the cone had landed and went on growing. It would shed a thousand more cones this season. Most would be eaten. A few would be cached. Fewer still would germinate.

But one had already gone on to a windowsill and a child's collection, which was not the usual path for a pine cone but was not without value either.

The forest floor held the rest of them. Patient and covered in leaf litter, waiting for the slow mathematics of germination to decide what happened next.
