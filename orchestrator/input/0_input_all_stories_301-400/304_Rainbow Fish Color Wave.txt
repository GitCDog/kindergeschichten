In the coral reef below the sunlit surface of the sea, there was a little rainbow fish named Arc whose scales changed color depending on the light around her. In deep water she was violet. In shallow water she was gold. In the open blue, she was a shifting mixture of green and blue that made her nearly invisible.

This was inconvenient when she was trying to be found, and very useful when she was trying not to be.

The bright sea was Arc's favorite place to be when the sun was high. The color wave — the way light refracted through the surface water and scattered across the reef — turned everything into a moving painting, and Arc, swimming through it, became part of the art.

One morning, she found a fish she had never seen before: small, silver, plain as a coin.

The silver fish was sitting very still near a sea fan, watching Arc with enormous eyes.

"You change color," the silver fish said.

"I do," Arc said. "The light does it, mostly."

"What does it feel like?"

Arc had never been asked this. She thought about it carefully, turning slowly in the color wave so that her scales moved from gold through green to a brief, brilliant orange.

"It feels like listening," she said at last. "The light says something, and I answer."

The silver fish considered this for a long time.

"I don't change," the silver fish said. "I'm always silver."

"Silver is its own answer," Arc said kindly. "Look what it does in the light."

The silver fish looked down at itself. In the scattered color wave of the reef, its plain scales were catching every color at once — a shifting, full-spectrum shimmer that made it look like a fragment of sunlight given a fish shape.

The silver fish had never noticed this before.

It turned slowly, just to see what happened.

Arc swam on through the bright sea, changing with the light, a living answer to the reef's many questions. Behind her, the silver fish turned again, watching itself become something it had never known it was.
