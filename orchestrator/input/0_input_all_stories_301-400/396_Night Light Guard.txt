The night light had been on since Milo was three.

He was eight now, and he had not thought about whether he still needed it. It was just on, the way certain things simply were: the night light on, the window facing west, the particular creak of the third stair. They were the conditions of sleeping in this room.

The tiny night light was a small disc plugged into the wall socket nearest the door. Its glow was the color of a warm ember — not white, not blue-green like some lights, but amber, which the sleep specialists said was better for night vision and easier on the eyes.

The dream guard was what Milo called it when he was three. He didn't call it that anymore. He called it the night light, which was what it was.

But the night light's job had not changed.

It prevented the total dark.

Total dark was different from dark. Dark was manageable — the dark of a room where you knew where the furniture was, where you had been for years, where nothing unexpected had ever happened. Total dark was the dark where you couldn't see your own hand and the known room became uncertain.

The night light prevented that uncertainty.

Its soft glow showed the shape of the door, the outline of the bookshelf, the pale patch of the window. The room remained itself.

Milo had a friend who slept without a night light and seemed entirely comfortable with this.

Milo had considered turning his off.

He had not done it yet.

The night light waited in its socket, amber and steady, the soft glow making the room exactly what it was — familiar, present, safe — and Milo slept inside its small assurance.


Milo woke once in the night — a brief, partial waking, the kind where you don't know where you are for one disorienting second.

The night light was there. The door outline was visible. The bookshelf was where it always was.

He was in his room.

He went back to sleep in approximately four seconds, which was the night light's whole job, performed perfectly and without acknowledgment.
