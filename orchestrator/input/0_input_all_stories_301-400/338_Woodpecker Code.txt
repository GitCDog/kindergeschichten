The forest had many sounds, but the brave woodpecker's was the most distinctive.

The woodpecker's name was Tick, and her tree knock was known across the half-kilometer radius she had established as her territory. Not all woodpecker knocking was the same — Tick had observed this carefully during her years in the forest. Some knocking was for food. Some was for communication. Some was territorial. And the fastest, sharpest, most regular knocking was the drumming that she produced on the dead elm at the territory's center: the forest code.

The forest code was Tick's.

She produced it in sequences of precisely timed strikes — not random, but structured, with consistent patterns that she returned to across the season. The dead elm resonated perfectly. Other woodpeckers in the wider forest used different trees and produced slightly different rhythms, and the whole forest was therefore threaded with woodpecker drumming that an expert could read like a language.

A researcher had spent a summer recording Tick's drumming.

She found seventeen distinct patterns, each one used in different contexts. Morning drumming was longer and louder. Alarm drumming was faster, more erratic. The particular pattern Tick produced at dusk, just before roosting, was the shortest and lowest — a quiet, measured sign-off that the researcher had come to think of as goodnight.

Tick drummed the dusk code as the light left the elm.

Her bill was adapted for this — reinforced skull, shock-absorbing space between bill and brain, a tongue that wrapped around the skull like a seatbelt. She drummed without damage, which was the remarkable engineering that made the tree knock possible.

The forest code went out into the evening air.

No one answered tonight. They didn't always.

Tick returned to her roost hole in the dead elm and arranged herself in the particular woodpecker sleeping posture — upright against the inside wall, tail braced for support.


The researcher published her findings the following spring. The paper described seventeen distinct drumming patterns, their contexts, and the provisional meanings she had assigned to each.

In the acknowledgments she had written: 'To the woodpecker at the dead elm, whose consistency made this study possible.'

Tick, who could not read, drummed the morning code at dawn as usual, establishing the forest code anew, as she did every day, as she would do for years to come.
