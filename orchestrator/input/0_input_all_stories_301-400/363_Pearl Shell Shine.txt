On the ocean bed where the water was deep and cold and still, in a field of similar shells that had accumulated over many years, there was an oyster named Opal who was growing a pearl.

It had begun three years ago, as pearls do: with an irritant. Something small had found its way between Opal's shell and her soft body — a fragment of shell, perhaps, or a grain of sand. The pearl itself was the solution. Layer by layer, Opal had coated the intruder in nacre — the same iridescent material that lined her shell — until it was smooth and no longer irritating.

The hidden shine was building.

Three years in, the pearl was about five millimeters across. Perfectly round, because the nacre deposited evenly in all directions, building a sphere. Luminous in a way that non-pearl things weren't, because the nacre layers refracted light differently — each layer adding a slightly different wavelength, the combined effect being the pearl's particular shine that was deeper than surface shine, as if the light came from inside.

Opal was unaware of this. She was an oyster. She was not producing a pearl on purpose. She was managing an irritation, and the pearl was the management.

A diver found Opal on the ocean bed.

She recognized an oyster that might be worth opening, and brought Opal up.

The shell was opened carefully. The pearl sat in its mantle tissue — perfectly formed, perfectly round, its hidden shine catching the surface light for the first time.

The diver had found perhaps a hundred pearls in her career. This one was one of the largest she had seen outside of a cultivated farm.

She held it up.

It caught the sunlight in the particular way that only nacre catches light, looking lit from within.


The diver brought the pearl to the surface and opened her hand to show the light.

The ocean continued below her, full of oysters producing their patient nacre, each one managing an irritation and producing, as the solution to an irritation, something that humans had valued for thousands of years.

The pearl was placed in a cloth. The diver dove again.
