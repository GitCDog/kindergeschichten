The satellite's name was Iris, and she had been watching the Earth for seven years.

Not watching in a dramatic sense — Iris had no intelligence, no judgment, no preferences about what she saw. She was a platform carrying instruments: a camera with a resolution of fifty centimeters, a spectrometer that measured the wavelengths of light coming up from the surface, and a radar system that could see through clouds.

The orbit loop took ninety-four minutes.

Sixteen times a day, Iris completed one circuit of the Earth. Each circuit covered a slightly different path across the surface — the Earth rotating beneath her as she traveled — so that over the course of sixteen days she imaged the entire planet.

The earth watch her instruments conducted was comprehensive.

She had watched forests grow and burn and regrow. She had watched cities expand at their edges. She had watched glaciers retreat — centimeter by centimeter on each pass, the change invisible in any single image, unmistakable over seven years of data.

She had watched the seasonal shift of the whole planet's vegetation — green moving toward the poles in spring, retreating in autumn — a wave of photosynthesis at planetary scale that no ground-based observer could perceive but that Iris, from her orbit loop, made visible.

A research team used Iris's data to track crop health in regions where ground-based monitoring was limited. Every fourteen days they received a new pass over their study areas, the spectrometer data telling them whether the crops were stressed, whether water was adequate, whether pest damage was affecting yield.

The tiny satellite passed overhead and saw.

What she saw, other people used.

Iris continued her orbit loop, indifferent and thorough.


The research team received Iris's latest pass data on a Wednesday and spent Thursday morning reviewing it.

A region that had been green two weeks ago was showing stress indicators. They flagged it for ground-level investigation.

Iris knew nothing of this. She continued her orbit loop, instruments on, data transmitting on schedule.

The earth watch and the human response were two parts of the same system, and the system was working.
