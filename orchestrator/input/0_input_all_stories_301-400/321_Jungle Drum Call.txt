Deep in the green heart of the jungle, where the trees were so tall their tops were lost in cloud and the air tasted of rain and earth and ten thousand growing things, there was a brave jungle drum named Tum.

Tum was carved from a single log of mahogany by hands that had made drums for five generations. His skin was stretched tight and perfectly tuned, and his voice — when struck — carried through the forest like nothing else could. Not like a shout, which dispersed into leaves and branches. More like a wave, which moved through the air as a complete thing, arriving at its destination intact.

The forest call was what the tribe used Tum for.

Each combination of beats meant something different. A slow, even rhythm meant all is well. Three quick beats followed by a pause meant someone is coming. A long rolling pattern that lasted thirty seconds and ended with a single sharp stroke meant gather at the river.

Tum had been sending the tribe beat since before any living person could remember.

On the day of the great gathering, Tum sent his longest and most complex call — a pattern that took three minutes to complete and had not been sounded for eleven years. Even the birds, who were accustomed to the drums, went silent.

From across the forest, from distances that no shouted voice could reach, answers came back. Other drums — smaller, each with its own voice — repeating and confirming and adding their part to the message. The jungle filled with the conversation of drums, each one a station in a network that had no wires and needed none.

The child who was learning to play Tum for the first time listened to the answering drums and understood, without being told, what she was joining.

Not just a skill. A language. A way of speaking across distance that the forest itself carried forward.


The answering drums fell silent one by one as the message was received and confirmed across the forest. In the quiet that followed, only the ordinary sounds of the jungle remained — insects, birds, the wind moving through the canopy.

Tum rested after the long performance. The child who was learning placed her hands flat on his skin and felt the last vibrations fade.

The forest call had gone out. The forest had answered. The work was done.
