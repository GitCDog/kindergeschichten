Inside the clockwork museum, behind a glass case that visitors pressed their faces against every day, a collection of gears performed their silent work.

The collection had been assembled by the museum's founder over forty years and represented every size of gear from a watch gear barely visible to the naked eye to a floor-standing gear three meters across that had come from an old mill and still bore the marks of its working life.

The gear spin was continuous.

The smallest gears turned fastest. The largest gear in the collection turned once every four hours, its circumference moving through the slow, patient arc that required — one of the museum's information placards explained — sixty-seven thousand rotations of the smallest gear for each single rotation of the largest.

The clockwork land of the display was powered by a motor concealed in the base, and the museum opened at nine and closed at five, and in those hours the gears turned and the visitors watched.

One visitor who returned most often was a retired engineer named Otto, who came every Thursday and stood in front of the case for an hour and watched the gears.

The staff had asked him once what he was looking for.

"Nothing specific," he said. "I'm watching the turn game."

The turn game was what he called the relationship between the gears — the way each one's rotation was determined by every other, the locked logic of the system. Change one gear and every other was affected. The clockwork land was a single thing in many parts.

He had spent his career designing such systems. But watching them was different from designing them.

Designing required knowing the answer. Watching allowed not knowing it.


Otto's hour at the display case ended at noon, as it always did. He thanked the attendant who hadn't needed to be thanked, and walked home.

The gears continued to turn in the empty room. The clockwork land required no audience to operate. It simply was — the small gears fast, the large gear slow, the math of their relationship exact and unvarying.

Next Thursday, Otto would be back.
