The deer trail did not appear on any map, which was appropriate since it had been made by deer rather than mapmakers.

It ran from the water meadow at the valley floor, up through the mixed woodland, across the ridge where the beeches grew, and down the other side to the salt lick near the old quarry. For most of its length it was barely visible — a slightly flattened line through the undergrowth, a narrowing between trees that could easily be missed.

But it had been traveled continuously for long enough that the roots across it were worn smooth and the overhanging branches had been shaped by the passage of many years of antlers.

The friendly deer who used it most reliably was a doe named Hind.

Hind made the trail journey at dawn and again at dusk — the two windows when deer preferred to be moving. She moved in the soft steps of an animal that had learned to be quiet not through fear but through long practice: placing each foot deliberately, reading the ground before committing her weight.

The forest path told her things.

Fresh scent on the north-facing slope: another deer, heading east. Broken twig at knee height: something larger had passed recently, moving fast. The particular quality of the air near the ridge — a slight human smell, old and dissipating — meant someone had walked here in the last day or two.

None of these things were alarming. They were information.

Hind reached the salt lick in the low light of dusk, made her crossing, and stood for a moment on the quarry edge looking out over the valley. The lights of the village were coming on below.

She turned back into the woodland.

The soft steps, the familiar trail, the dusk air closing around her as she moved toward home.


Hind reached the den site — a depression in the fern bed under two fallen birch trunks — and stood for a moment, ears working, reading the air one more time before settling.

The forest path had told her everything she needed. The dusk journey was complete. The territory was as it should be, the night was safe, the fern bed was dry.

She lowered herself onto the soft ground and let the forest close around her.
