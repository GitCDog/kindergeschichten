The dune buggy race happened every year at the start of summer, across the most spectacular stretch of desert dunes anyone had found in the region.

The buggies were not racing cars in the usual sense. They were homemade — each one built over the winter by its driver from whatever was available, which meant they were all completely different and all completely themselves. There were four-wheeled ones and three-wheeled ones and one that had started as five-wheeled and had been adjusted to four after its first test run revealed a fundamental instability.

The littlest buggy in the race was named Dash.

Dash was small — half the height of the next biggest competitor — but her wind wheels were perfectly sized for the sand race's demands. Big wheels worked better on flat desert. Small wheels were more agile on dunes. The race included both.

Her driver was a girl named Petra who had built Dash from scratch over four months, consulting no plans, making it up as she went, testing each component individually and then together.

The starting horn sounded.

The dune race was five kilometers across terrain that changed every year because dunes moved. What had been a fast flat section last year was now a series of soft ridges. What had been a sharp descent was now a gentler slope.

Petra had walked the course the day before and memorized what she could.

On the first dune, three bigger buggies went wide. Dash went narrow, finding a ridge line that Petra had spotted and held in her memory overnight. Wind wheels spinning, Dash crested the dune and was first over the top.

She didn't stay first. Bigger engines caught up on the flat sections.

But on every dune, the sand race replayed itself: the big buggies wide and grinding, Dash finding the line that only a small and agile thing could find.

She crossed the line fourth.

Petra climbed out and patted Dash's windshield.


Petra pushed Dash into the shade of the race tent and started making notes on the clipboard she carried: what had worked, what hadn't, what she would change before next year.

The sand race already felt like last year in the way that finished things do. The next race had begun, at least in her mind, in the form of ideas and adjustments.

Dash sat in the shade, wind wheels still, looking — in Petra's estimation — entirely pleased with herself.
