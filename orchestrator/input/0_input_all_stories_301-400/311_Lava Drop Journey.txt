Deep inside a volcano that had been sleeping for two hundred years, a curious lava drop named Lev finally began to move.

She had been part of the magma chamber for as long as she could remember — thick and orange and impossibly hot, surrounded by millions of other lava drops in the slow, grinding darkness of the earth's crust. She had always known, in the way lava knows things, that the journey was coming. The pressure was building. The volcano edge was calling.

The heat journey began.

Lev rose with the magma column, slowly at first and then with growing urgency as the pressure above decreased and the rock that had been holding everything down gave way. The temperature around her changed — not cooler, never cooler in here, but different, as if the heat itself was moving rather than just existing.

She reached the vent.

And then she was outside.

The world above the volcano edge was extraordinary. Cool air hit her surface instantly, beginning the crust — a thin dark skin forming over her brightness, cracking as she moved, revealing the orange-red glow beneath. She was both solid and liquid at once, which was a strange way to be, but the only way she knew.

She flowed.

The hillside received her and she moved down it, following the slope, consuming the dry grass at her edges (which turned to ash), cooling at the surface but still liquid and pressurized inside.

Around her, other lava drops were joining, merging, becoming a flow rather than individuals.

Lev became part of something larger.

She reached the coast as the sun was setting, and when she hit the ocean she made a great hissing column of steam that rose two hundred meters into the evening air. She hardened instantly. The heat journey was complete.

She became new rock, added to the edge of the island.

New land, where there had been none before.


Lev cooled over the next decade, becoming rock in the particular way that lava becomes rock: from the outside in, slowly, the hot liquid core hardening last and most reluctantly.

Birds landed on her eventually. Then a small plant found a crack and put down roots. Then another plant. Then the soil began.

The heat journey had ended, but something else had started, as it always did, from the places where lava came to rest.
