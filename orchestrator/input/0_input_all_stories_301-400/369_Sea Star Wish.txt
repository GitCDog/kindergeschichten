On the rock that was exposed at low tide and submerged at high, there was a sea star named Ray who had lived there for eleven years.

Ray was a five-armed sea star — a common species, though common was a relative term since every sea star was specific and individual in ways that were not visible to casual observation. Ray's particular specificity was his color: a deep orange that faded to pale yellow at the arm tips, which was not typical for his species in this location.

He moved slowly.

Sea stars move on hundreds of tiny tube feet — hydraulic structures on the underside of each arm that grip and release in waves, producing movement that was purposeful without being fast. Ray covered perhaps a meter per hour on a good day. He had never needed to move faster.

The wave magic happened at high tide.

When the water covered the rock, Ray's world expanded. The exposure zone that he crossed at low tide — a place of danger, of birds and drying — became simply part of the ocean, and he moved freely across it.

A child found him at low tide one afternoon.

She picked him up carefully, with both hands, and held him up to look at the underside: the thousands of tube feet, still working in the air, grasping at nothing.

"Can he feel me?" she asked.

"Probably," her father said. "Put him back."

She set Ray back on the rock exactly where she had found him.

The sea star wish, the local name for finding a sea star, meant you were supposed to make a wish before putting it back.

The child closed her eyes for a moment.

Then she set Ray down.

He began his slow movement back toward the water's edge, orange in the afternoon light.


Ray continued his slow patrol of the rock face, reaching the edge of his territory by afternoon and turning back.

He had been wished on more times than he knew. He had no way to know. He was a sea star on a rock, moving at a meter per hour, existing in the precise way that sea stars existed.

But the child's eyes had closed, and the wish had been made, and Ray moved on in his orange patience.
