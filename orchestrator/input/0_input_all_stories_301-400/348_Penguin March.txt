The march had been underway for eleven days.

The little penguin who had the most difficulty with the ice line was a young emperor named Tuck, who had only made this journey once before and had not yet developed the full Antarctic marching physique — which was, essentially, a lot of fat and a great deal of determination.

Tuck had the determination.

The team walk was not fast. It couldn't be, across sea ice in the middle of the Antarctic winter, in temperatures that made breathing uncomfortable and wind that came from no direction and every direction. The penguins moved in a slow, steady huddle — not organized by anyone, emerging from individual decisions about warmth and shelter that collectively produced the group.

The huddle rotated.

This was the thing about the march that Tuck found most remarkable: no individual penguin stayed on the cold outside of the huddle for long. The ones on the windward edge slowly worked their way to the center; the ones in the warm center gradually emerged at the leeward side. The whole mass rotated slowly, like a large, very cold, very deliberate pinwheel.

No penguin decided this. It simply happened.

Tuck had been on the outside twice and the warm center once. The center was extraordinary — a relative warmth produced entirely by the bodies of thousands of penguins, a pocket of life in the winter darkness.

He held it while he had it, knowing the rotation would move him out again.

The ice line stretched ahead. The colony's breeding ground was still forty kilometers away. The march continued.

Tuck kept his feet moving in the team walk rhythm — one foot, then the other, the waddle that was not graceful but was extremely effective across ice — and leaned into the wind.


The team walk reached the breeding ground on the fifteenth day.

The colony had been here for centuries. The ice was marked by the long history of arrivals — worn flat in paths, shaped by millions of similar feet over many seasons. Tuck settled into his position in the new group, the huddle already reforming around him.

He had made it. The ice had held. The march was finished.
