The iceberg had been drifting for two years.

It had calved from a glacier in the far south — a piece of ice the size of a small country, broken free by the slow mechanical failure of the ice shelf and set adrift in the Southern Ocean. Over the first year it had lost a third of its volume to the warmer water it encountered as it drifted north. The sea drift continued through the second year, and it was smaller now — still enormous, but no longer country-sized. More like a large city.

The brave iceberg's name, if one were to name it, was Berg.

Berg was not conscious. This point should be made clearly. But he had a presence that ships gave a wide berth to and that scientists visited in small vessels to measure and sample and photograph.

The photographs never captured it properly.

The scale was wrong in photographs. Berg was ninety meters above the waterline and six hundred meters below it, and the camera couldn't show both at once. The bulk of him — the seven-eighths that was underwater — was invisible to anyone who hadn't learned to account for it.

The giant float was nearing the end.

As Berg drifted into warmer water, the melt accelerated. Pieces calved from his edges daily, splashing into the sea in explosions of white. His below-water mass, shifting as it melted, caused him to tip slowly — a process that would eventually, when the balance tipped far enough, produce a roll that would put his underside face up and his above-water mass below.

He would look entirely different.

He would still be Berg, reformed.

The sea drift continued north, the sun moving across him daily, the ice releasing back to the ocean it had come from.


The last visible piece of Berg was a fragment about the size of a house, floating north of where the main body had been. It was still cold inside and warm at the surface, the melt continuing slowly.

A gannet landed on it and looked around.

The sea drift continued. The fragment was another week from full dissolution. Then it would be ocean, which was where it had come from, four centuries ago, as precipitation on the glacier.
