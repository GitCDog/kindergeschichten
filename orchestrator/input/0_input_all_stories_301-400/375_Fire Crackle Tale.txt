The wood burn began with a small sound.

Not the dramatic whoosh of a large fire being lit — this was a household fireplace, not a bonfire, and it started modestly. A newspaper twist, a few small sticks arranged in the careful way that the house's oldest adult had spent decades perfecting, a larger log on top of the structure with its bark facing down.

The fire crackle was what the family called the first five minutes.

In those five minutes the fire found itself — the flame running from the newspaper to the kindling sticks, the sticks establishing their burn, the heat conducting up to the larger log and beginning the slow work of bringing it to combustion temperature. The cozy sound of this process was specific and well-loved: the soft percussion of wood cells expanding and cracking in the heat, the occasional small pop as a pocket of sap vaporized, the gentle hiss of moisture.

The cheerful fire crackle had a different rhythm in different woods.

Birch crackled briskly and frequently. Oak burned more quietly, its dense structure releasing heat steadily. Pine made the most dramatic sounds — loud pops and occasional small sparks that required the fireguard — but produced less heat for its drama.

Tonight's wood was a mix of oak and apple. The family had pruned the apple tree in September and the wood had dried well.

The apple wood smell was the best.

Every tree had a specific smell in the burning. Apple was sweet and complex, something between fruit and baking, entirely its own. The room filled with it.

The youngest child pulled a blanket onto the sofa and turned to face the fire.

"Stay," she said, to no one in particular.

The cozy sound continued, apple wood and oak, the fire crackle keeping the room warm and the evening going.


By nine o'clock the fire had settled into a long slow burn that would need no attention until before bed, when the father would put on the final log and close the damper. The cozy sound had gentled from the active crackling to the occasional shift of wood in the grate.

The room was warm. The child was still.

Apple wood, oak, the particular smell of a household fire in autumn. The fire crackle tale went on.
