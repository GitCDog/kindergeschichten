High above the land where ordinary clouds drifted and dissolved, there was a brave cloud castle that never came down. Its towers were made of the thickest cumulus, its walls packed tight by the wind king himself, and its sky bridge stretched from the east turret all the way to the horizon where the sun went to sleep each evening.

The cloud castle's name was Alto, and Alto had one rule: only those who were brave enough to cross the sky bridge could enter.

The sky bridge was made of mist — not solid at all, really, just a very determined arrangement of water droplets that held together through sheer collective stubbornness. It swayed in the wind. It glowed gold in morning light and went silver at dusk.

One morning, a small girl who had been watching Alto from her rooftop for years decided she had waited long enough.

She climbed to the highest point of the hill behind her house, spread her arms wide, and jumped.

The sky bridge caught her.

It was softer than she expected and bouncier too, and she had to slow her pace or she would have bounced clear off the edge. She walked carefully, arms out for balance, the wind king's breath pushing steadily from her left.

The brave cloud castle grew larger as she crossed. Its towers shimmered. She could hear, faintly, a sound like the inside of a seashell — the deep resonance of a structure made entirely of moving air.

At the gate, which was formed by two tall columns of cloud that leaned toward each other, she stopped and put her hand forward. The cloud was cool and damp against her palm.

The gate parted.

Inside Alto, everything was white and luminous and vast. The courtyards were wider than any she had imagined. Fountains of mist rose and fell in gentle arcs. The wind king's throne was at the far end — a chair built from the oldest, densest cloud in the sky — and it was empty.

The wind king, it turned out, had been waiting outside the whole time.

He was not large and frightening. He was a small, weathered figure who smelled of rain and distance, and he said simply: "You crossed the bridge."

"I did," she said.

"Then Alto is yours to visit, whenever you need sky."

She looked up at the towers and felt the kind of joy that only comes from arriving somewhere you have earned.
