The coral reef was approximately eight thousand years old.

It had begun as a small colony of coral polyps — each one a tiny animal no bigger than a grain of rice — settling on a patch of rock in shallow water and secreting their calcium carbonate skeletons. Generation after generation, skeleton on skeleton, the reef had grown.

Now it was a color home for approximately four hundred species.

The curious fish who knew it best was a small wrasse named Wrap, who had lived on the reef for three years and had memorized its geography the way a person memorizes a neighborhood: by walking it daily until it was simply known.

Wrap's territory was the east face of the reef, from the sea floor at twelve meters up to the surface zone. It was, in reef terms, an excellent territory: varied depth, good light, diverse coral types, a small cave at eight meters that she used as a shelter in weather.

The sea life of the east face was busy.

A school of fusiliers — small, silver-blue, constantly moving — streamed past each morning, and Wrap had learned their schedule well enough to be in a certain spot at a certain time to catch the small creatures the fusiliers displaced.

A pair of clownfish occupied an anemone near the cave. They were territorial and predictable. Wrap gave them their space.

The occasional turtle passed through, and the reef rearranged itself around the turtle's passage the way a crowd makes way for something larger than itself — not in coordination, just in the sum of individual decisions.

Wrap patrolled the east face.

The color home was well. The coral was growing where it should. The sea life was present and abundant.

She returned to the cave at dusk and rested in the flowing water.


The reef went on after Wrap returned to the cave. It went on all night, as it always did — the nocturnal shift of creatures replacing the day shift, the functions continuing, the polyps feeding in the dark on the plankton that rose from depth each night.

The color home was never still. It was a living system in a very old building, and the building and the life were the same thing.
