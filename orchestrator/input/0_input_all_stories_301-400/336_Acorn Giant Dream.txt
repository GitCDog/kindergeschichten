The acorn had fallen from the oak in October, rolled three meters, and come to rest against a stone in the leaf litter. This was a normal acorn year — thousands of them on the forest floor, most of which would be eaten, some of which would be cached and forgotten by squirrels, and a very few of which would germinate.

The magic acorn's name, if an acorn could have a name, was Orn. And Orn had already made a decision — not consciously, since acorns don't make decisions consciously — but in the biochemical sense: she was not going to be eaten, or cached, or decay on the surface. She was going to grow.

In January, the decision manifested as a root.

Thin as a hair at first, pushing down through the cold soil with the exact chemical determination of a seed with something to prove. The soil resisted, as soil does, but Orn's root chemistry dissolved the resistance particle by particle.

By March, she was a seedling.

By May, her first leaves were above the soil, smaller than a thumbnail, but green — genuinely, fully green, with the chlorophyll already working, converting the spring light into the sugars that would feed the next stage of growth.

A child found her in June.

"A baby oak," the child said, crouching down to look.

The child's mother crouched beside them. "If you leave it, it could grow as large as that one," she said, nodding toward the great tree fifty meters away.

The child looked between Orn and the great oak, doing the mental arithmetic of distance and time.

"How long?"

"Two or three hundred years."

The child was quiet for a moment.

"That's okay," the child said finally. "I'll check on it."

Orn grew, in the oak dream of acorns — the one that takes centuries, the one that ends in something extraordinary.


The child checked on the oak seedling every weekend for two months. By autumn it had grown three more leaves. By the following spring, it was knee-height and unmistakably an oak.

Otherpeople had started to notice it too. The child had not told them about checking on it. Some discoveries belonged to you alone, at least for a while.

Oak dream: the one where a thing no bigger than a thumb becomes, given enough time, the thing that casts the widest shade.
