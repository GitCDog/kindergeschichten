The ash cloud had been drifting for three days.

It had begun as something more dramatic: a column of volcanic material that had risen thirty kilometers above the eruption site in the first six hours, the force of the explosion driving ash and gas and rock fragments to the edge of the stratosphere.

From there, the curious ash cloud had been taken by the winds of the upper atmosphere, which were faster and less predictable than the winds at ground level, and which had carried her south and then east and then split her into several smaller clouds that were now drifting independently across a band of sky spanning two thousand kilometers.

The gray swirl of the cloud moved at fifty kilometers per hour.

From the ground, the effect of her passage was a dimming of the light — the ash particles too fine to see individually, but collectively thick enough to scatter the sun's light and shift its color. Where the main body of the ash cloud passed overhead, the sky turned a particular dusty orange that people described as strange but not frightening.

Birds noticed the light change before people did.

A murmuration of starlings that had been performing their evening display interrupted it — the whole mass shifting direction at once, as if reconsidering — and then resumed, but lower than usual.

The gray swirl drifted east.

Three days after the eruption it would cross the coast and thin over the ocean, its particles too spread to affect anything except, fractionally, the color of the horizon at sunset for a few days.

Then it would disperse into the general dust that everyone breathed and no one thought about.

The sky drift ended not with a landing but with a disappearance.


The sunsets were different for two weeks after the eruption — the remaining ash particles in the stratosphere scattering the low-angle light into colors more saturated than usual. People photographed them. A few noticed they were different from ordinary sunsets and looked up why.

The gray swirl that had been a cloud was now part of the atmosphere, indistinguishable from it.

The sky drift had ended. The sky itself had received it.
