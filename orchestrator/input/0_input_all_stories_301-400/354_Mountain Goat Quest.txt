The rock climb began where the trail ended.

This was the mountain goat's element: the terrain that trails couldn't follow, the angles that paths could not achieve, the faces of limestone and granite that required four hooves and a specific set of instincts that had been developing for several thousand years.

The brave mountain goat's name was Crag, and Crag had been climbing since he was a kid — which, for a mountain goat, began within days of birth and progressed rapidly. He was four now and considered himself an intermediate climber: capable of most terrain, still occasionally surprised by what he found at the top.

The peak quest had started at dawn.

The lower slopes were grass and loose scree — straightforward, the kind of terrain that provided good training but no particular challenge. Crag moved through it at a comfortable pace, his splayed hooves working on the rock in the way that goat hooves work: each split hoof clamping independently around whatever surface presented itself, the hard outer edge for grip, the soft inner pad for traction.

The upper route steepened.

Here the rock was a challenge: vertical faces of perhaps four meters, broken by ledges that were sometimes wide enough for all four hooves and sometimes barely enough for two.

Crag moved up the first face in three confident moves.

The second face required assessment. He looked at it for a minute — read the route, identified the holds, planned the sequence. Then went.

The peak was a flat section of perhaps ten square meters at the summit. From here the whole valley was visible — the lower pastures, the thread of the river, the road, and the village beyond.

Crag looked at it all with the direct, unsentimental attention of an animal at the top of something it had climbed.

Then he started back down.


Crag took the descent more carefully than the ascent, which was the correct approach — down was harder, requiring more precise weight placement and more trust in the hooves.

He reached the lower slope in the same time as the climb, which pleased him. He found the grazing area and dropped his head to the thin mountain grass.

The peak quest was done. The peak was still there, available, for whenever he chose to go again.
