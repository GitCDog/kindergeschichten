In a kitchen that existed somewhere between the Milky Way and the Andromeda galaxy, a kind star baker named Stella was working on the most ambitious cake of her career.

The galaxy cake.

It had been commissioned by the constellation Orion for his annual celebration, and it needed to be spectacular. Stella had been planning for three cosmic weeks and had assembled her ingredients with care: flour milled from asteroid dust (very fine, very dry), sugar crystallized from the rings of a distant planet, eggs from a bird-star that nested in the Crab Nebula, and butter made from the cream of a comet's tail.

The cosmic kitchen smelled extraordinary.

Stella mixed the batter with a spoon made from a collapsed star's outer shell — dense and heavy, perfect for working through thick cosmic dough. She poured the batter into a pan shaped like a spiral galaxy and placed it in her oven, which ran on the light of a small private sun she kept for the purpose.

While the cake baked, she made the frosting.

Galaxy frosting was not one color. It was every color — the dark blues and purples of deep space, the warm gold of star-forming regions, the bright white of new stars just igniting, the soft red of nebulae cooling into dust.

She piped it on in spirals, working from the center outward, the way galaxies actually grow.

When she was done, she placed the cake on the table and stood back.

It looked exactly like a galaxy in miniature. You could see the core — dense and golden — and the spiral arms reaching outward in sweeping curves, and at the very edges, the pale suggestion of stars too far from the center to be easily counted.

Orion arrived and was quiet for a moment.

"Stella," he said. "You've made the universe edible."

Stella considered this an excellent review.

The galaxy cake served seventeen constellations with slices to spare.


Stella packed the remaining slices into small star-boxes for the minor constellations who hadn't been able to attend the celebration. Each box had a card with the constellation's name written in silver ink.

The galaxy cake had been her finest work. She knew she would attempt to match it next year, and probably fall short, and that was exactly as it should be. The standard she set for herself was always one cake ahead.
