Every night, when the last light went out in the last window and the town was finally, fully asleep, the dream clouds came.

They arrived from the east, which was the direction that dreams came from according to the old stories, though the mechanism was more complicated than direction. They drifted low over the rooftops, slow-moving and luminous, trailing the soft glow that you could only see if you were awake to look, which very few people were.

The little dream cloud's name was Float, and Float was responsible for this particular town's dreams.

The night float was Float's evening work.

She would arrive over the first house and release a small quantity of dream material — not quite fog, not quite light, not quite thought — that drifted down the chimney and under doors and through the cracks in windows and found its way to sleeping minds.

She had a repertoire.

For the children: adventure stories, flights, the particular shape of beloved things. For the adults: more complicated material, drawn from what they had carried with them into sleep. For the elderly: sometimes sharp, vivid memories. Sometimes peace.

Float moved house to house.

She had no favorites among the sleepers, but she had noticed which ones woke with the most animation — who was at breakfast talking about their dream, who seemed lighter than the night before, who had resolved something in sleep that waking had made difficult.

The sleep ride she gave was not guaranteed. She provided the dream material. What each sleeper made of it was their own business.

She finished her rounds before dawn and drifted back east, empty and satisfied.

The town woke into morning.

The dreaming, for another night, was done.


Float returned to the east at four in the morning, empty and satisfied. The night float was finished. The town would wake with its dreams half-remembered or not remembered at all, just a residue of feeling.

This was fine. Dreams were not for memory. They were for the night, for the sleep ride through the hours, for whatever the mind needed when the day was done.

The dream cloud's work was invisible and sufficient.
