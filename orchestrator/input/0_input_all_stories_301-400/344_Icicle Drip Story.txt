On the north-facing eave of the old farmhouse, a row of icicles had been building since the first cold night of December.

The silliest of them — the one furthest to the right, who had a slight curve to her that the others lacked — was named Drip, and Drip had feelings about the cycle she was part of.

The frozen cave of the eave was Drip's home. Cold at night, she was solid and still. As the day warmed, she experienced the thing that defined her existence: the water drop.

The melt began at her pointed tip.

Drop by drop, the ice that had been her lowest centimeter overnight became liquid and traveled the length of her remaining surface and fell: one drop every few seconds, precise and regular, each one catching the winter light as it separated and became briefly a sphere before vanishing.

Some drops fell into the snow below, making small round holes like tiny impact craters.

Some fell onto the stone windowsill and crept toward the drain.

One fell on a child's upturned face — a girl named Clara who had come to watch, as she did most middays when the icicles were melting.

Clara liked the water drop sequence. She had calculated, with some careful watching, that a medium icicle in direct midday sun dripped approximately once every three seconds. She had told no one this, because there was no particular occasion to tell anyone about icicle drip rates.

But she knew.

By three o'clock the melt slowed as the sun moved behind the roof. By four the drops had stopped entirely. By nightfall Drip was rebuilding — the meltwater from the day refreezing, her length returning, her curve slightly more pronounced than the night before.

Tomorrow: melt again.

The frozen cave held everything steady overnight.


By March, the icicles were gone. Drip had become water drop by water drop until there was nothing left to drip from, and the eave was bare again.

But Clara remembered the calculation: one drop every three seconds, medium icicle, direct midday sun. She wrote it in her notebook, which was becoming a notebook about specific things she had timed and measured.

It was a good notebook.
