The forge fire had been lit at six in the morning.

By the time the brave blacksmith arrived at seven, the fire was at working temperature and the metal in the forge — a piece of mild steel bar stock, eight millimeters across — was showing the first faint color of heat.

The blacksmith's name was Mara, and she had been working at this forge for twenty-two years.

The blacksmith hammer — a cross-peen of two kilograms — was her primary tool, and she had used it enough that her right hand had adapted to its handle in ways that were visible to anyone who knew what to look for. The palm was slightly thicker on the hammer side. The forearm was more developed on that side. Her grip was automatic.

The metal shape process began when the steel reached yellow-orange.

Mara pulled it from the forge fire with tongs and placed it on the anvil. The first hammer stroke was to establish the work — a decision about direction, about where the metal was going, communicated to the steel in the immediate, specific language of the hammer.

The metal responded.

This was the thing about blacksmithing that Mara had found compelling from the first lesson: the metal moved under the hammer. Not simply deformed — moved, flowed, responded to the direction of the blow in ways that required reading rather than simply hitting. You had to understand where the metal wanted to go and work with it, not against it.

She worked the bar into a hook — a simple project, twenty minutes of work — and the forge fire did its part of the work between each heating.

The hammer, the forge, the anvil, the metal. The four elements of the blacksmith's conversation.


Mara finished the hook and placed it in the quench bucket — a hiss and a cloud of steam — then lifted it out and set it on the rack to cool.

The forge fire was banked down for the morning. The hammer was returned to its place on the bench. The anvil, cooling, made the small ticking sounds of metal releasing heat.

Twenty-two years. The conversation between hammer and metal went on. She would be back tomorrow.
