Puff was a little cloud with a gift, and the gift was this: she could change her shape faster and more completely than any other cloud in the sky.

This made her very popular at high altitudes.

The shape change would begin with a thought — not quite a thought, since clouds don't think exactly, but something like an intention — and Puff would redistribute her water droplets, thinning some parts and thickening others, until the change was complete.

She had been a horse seventeen times. She had been a rabbit — her most requested shape — thirty-two times. She had been a ship at sea (which required a lot of lateral extension), a sleeping cat (which was her personal favorite), and once, memorably, a very accurate portrait of the sun, which the sun itself had noticed and had seemed pleased by.

Today Puff was practicing something new.

She had decided to try a dragon.

Dragons were difficult because they had so many distinct elements: the long neck, the wings, the body, the tail, the suggestion of fire at the open mouth. Getting all of these to coexist simultaneously in one small cloud required a level of water droplet management that Puff had not yet mastered.

Her first attempt produced something that looked like a dragon that had had a bad day.

The second attempt was closer, but the tail kept drooping because she was having trouble maintaining the density at its far end while also sustaining the wings.

The third attempt came together all at once, in the way these things sometimes do — suddenly, completely, without the effort of the previous two. The dragon shape floated in the sky, neck arched, wings spread, tail curling gracefully to one side.

Below her, on a hillside, a child pointed upward.

"Dragon!" the child said.

Puff held the shape as long as she could — four minutes and seventeen seconds, her personal record — and then the wind arrived and scattered her back into a formless sky float.

She began gathering herself for a fourth attempt.
