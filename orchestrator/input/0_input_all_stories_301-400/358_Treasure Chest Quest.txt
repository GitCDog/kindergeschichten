The treasure chest had been in the attic for forty years.

Its name — not an official name, just the name the family had always used — was Old Brown, for the obvious reasons of age and color. Old Brown was a proper chest: hinged lid, iron corners, a hasp with a padlock that had lost its key in approximately 1987 and had not been opened since.

The lock puzzle occupied three generations of the family.

The first generation had simply left the key-loss as an interesting fact. The chest was in the attic, it was locked, and one day the key might turn up.

It didn't.

The second generation — the current adult members of the family — had made more determined efforts. They had tried other keys from the house's collection of miscellaneous keys (seventeen attempts, zero successes). They had contacted a locksmith, who had come to the attic and assessed the lock and named a figure that seemed too high for the contents of a chest that might be empty.

The third generation — a twelve-year-old named Finn — took a different approach.

He researched the lock type from photographs he took with his phone. He found that padlocks of this type from this era had a limited number of master key patterns. He ordered, online, a set of skeleton keys from a reputable supplier of antique hardware accessories.

The gold shine appeared on a Saturday morning when the third key in the set turned the lock.

Old Brown's lid opened.

The contents: a collection of sea glass in a tin, a bundle of letters tied with ribbon, a small oil painting of a harbour, and a note in faded handwriting that said: For whoever finds their way in.

The lock puzzle had been solved.

The rest was theirs to discover.


The family spent two afternoons reading the letters, which were from someone's great-grandmother and spanned twelve years and contained more interesting information than any of them had expected about a life no one had known much about.

The oil painting went on the wall in the living room.

Finn kept the note — For whoever finds their way in — in his room, folded, in a small box on his shelf.
