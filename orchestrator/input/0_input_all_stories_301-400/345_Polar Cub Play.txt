The ice field stretched in every direction as far as a polar bear cub could see, which was farther than most things but not quite to the horizon, where the sky and ice blended in a white-grey smudge.

The curious polar cub's name was Nim, and Nim was eight months old, which was old enough to be interested in everything and young enough to be afraid of nothing.

The arctic play began whenever his mother stopped moving.

She stopped often — to rest, to watch the sea ice for seals, to assess whether the next pressure ridge was worth navigating — and each time she stopped, Nim commenced operations.

He had discovered that snow was moldable.

Not snow in its flat, wind-packed form, which was essentially solid. But snow in the drifts, where it was loose and deep, could be pushed and gathered and piled and collapsed. He had developed a technique involving his nose that was particularly effective for dislodging large sections of drift.

The ice field also offered hills.

Small ones — pressure ridges, mostly, created where sections of sea ice had pushed against each other — but sufficient for an eight-month-old polar bear. Nim climbed them and descended them, sometimes deliberately and sometimes not. The not-deliberate descents were funnier, from an observer's perspective, and his mother watched these without expression while clearly attending.

One morning he found a hole in the ice — a seal's breathing hole, round and perfect, the water below it dark and cold.

He put his nose to it and smelled the ocean.

The smell was enormous — salt and depth and cold and life, the whole arctic environment compressed into one round opening. He sneezed. He put his nose back and smelled it again.

His mother sat behind him and watched.

Arctic play, she had learned, was also arctic education.


Nim's mother watched all of this with the specific attention of an animal that was simultaneously resting and monitoring. She would wait until the investigation reached a natural conclusion, then call him back with the low sound that meant: enough, now.

Arctic play was also arctic learning. She knew this from her own cubhood. You learned the ice by playing on it, and you learned by playing what the ice was for.
