In the high valley where the forest snow came early and stayed late, there was a cabin named Home. Home had one window on the south side, and from that window in winter a light shone into the forest snow in a warm rectangle on the ground.

The tiny cabin light that made this rectangle was a single incandescent bulb in the kitchen, mounted above the table where the family ate dinner each night. It was not bright by any standard. From the road below the valley, if you knew where to look, you could see it only as the faintest suggestion of warmth in the dark slope of the forest.

But from the forest, from the deer trails and the snow paths and the edge of the treeline, the cozy glow was clear and constant.

The animals knew it.

The foxes that hunted the valley's edge knew the rectangle of light meant humans, and humans meant caution, and caution meant a wider arc around the cabin's clearing. But they also knew the light was consistent — it appeared at dusk and went off at ten — and consistency was information they could use.

A family of deer that crossed the clearing on the path to the stream knew the light too, and crossed at the far edge, where the rectangle didn't reach.

The people inside the cabin knew none of this. They ate dinner and talked and washed up and turned the light off at ten, unaware that their kitchen bulb was an anchor point in the forest's nightly geography.

A child pressed her face to the window one night, looking out at the dark.

She could see the light's reflection on the snow — the faint warm glow of the window itself, falling on the white below.

"We can be seen," she said.

Her father looked too. "And so can the stars," he said.


The child went back to bed, and the cabin light glow continued without her — the warm rectangle on the garden snow, the cozy glow for whatever moved in the forest dark, the signal that said: here, people, warmth.

The deer crossed the clearing at their usual time, at the far edge of the light, and continued to the stream.

The light went off at ten, as always.
