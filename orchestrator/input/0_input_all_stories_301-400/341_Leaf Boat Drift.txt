The leaf boat had launched itself.

No one had placed it in the stream deliberately. It had fallen from the oak branch above, landed flat on the water's surface — which it managed because it was large and waxy and the surface tension cooperated just long enough — and then the current had taken it before it could sink.

The tiny leaf boat's name, for the purposes of this story, was Oak, and Oak was now on a stream ride that would take her to places she hadn't expected to see when she was attached to a branch.

The first surprise was the speed.

Streams moved faster than they looked from above. Oak had seen this stream from the branch — a quiet, brownish ribbon through the meadow — and had not prepared for the urgency of the current once she was inside it. She dipped and spun. A submerged rock sent her sideways. An eddy near the bank caught her briefly and turned her a full half-circle before releasing her back to the main flow.

She adapted. Leaves are good at adapting — their entire lives are about managing what the world offers.

The water drift carried her under the stone bridge and she experienced something she had never seen from the branch: the underneath of things. The bridge's worn stonework viewed from below, green with moss and damp, unexpectedly beautiful.

Out the other side into wider water — the stream joining a small river, the space opening up, the current gentling.

She drifted.

A duck investigated her briefly, decided she was not food, and turned away.

A child on the bank pointed at her and said, "Look — a boat!"

Oak settled on the widened river and let the water take her. The bank moved past: rushes, a broken fence post, a dog that came to the edge and watched her go by with some interest.

She had expected to fall and rot. This was better.


Oak drifted around the bend and found the river widening into a marshy area where the current slowed. She settled into the reeds at the bank as the light dropped.

She had been a leaf on a branch for six months. She had been a tiny leaf boat for an afternoon. Both, she felt, had been the right thing at the right time.
