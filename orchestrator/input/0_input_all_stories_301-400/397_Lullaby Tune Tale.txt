The lullaby had no official title.

In the family's records — which were informal and mostly in people's memories rather than written down — it was called the sleeping song, or Grandma's song, or just the tune. It had been passed from grandmother to mother to child for at least four generations, and perhaps more, since the generations before that had not been asked while they were alive to ask.

The cheerful lullaby tune was eight bars long, repeated.

It wasn't a complicated piece of music. The melody was simple enough for anyone to sing on first hearing, the harmony gentle enough for someone without training to add it, and the words — which varied slightly in different family members' versions — were about the reliable return of the sun and the safety of sleep.

Sleepy eyes came at the end of the second or third repetition.

This was reliably observed across the generations, which suggested either that the lullaby had some specific effect on the nervous system (possible, given its tempo and frequency) or that it was the association — this song meaning safety and warmth and the presence of someone who loved you, accumulated over years of it being used exactly this way.

Probably both.

The soft song was being sung tonight.

A mother was in a child's bedroom, the light low, the window dark. The child was in bed, not quite asleep, the day still present in her eyes.

The lullaby tune started.

Eight bars. Repeated.

By the second repetition, the child's eyes were heavy.

By the third, they were closed.

The mother sang one more time, quietly, for herself as much as for the child, the family's four-generation song going forward into one more night.


The mother finished the song and stayed for a moment in the dark, listening to the child breathe.

The sleeping song had worked exactly as it always did. It had been working for at least four generations of this family, possibly more, which meant it had been used thousands of times and had never once failed.

She closed the door quietly and went to bed herself, the soft song still moving in her head as she fell asleep.
