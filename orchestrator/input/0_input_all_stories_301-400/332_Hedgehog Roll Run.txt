Humphrey was a brave hedgehog, and brave hedgehogs were, in Humphrey's considered opinion, the most interesting kind.

The cowardly approach to being a hedgehog was to curl into a ball at the first sign of anything. Humphrey had tried this when he was younger and found it deeply boring. You curled up and waited and the world went on without you and you emerged eventually to find that the danger had passed, but so had whatever interesting thing had been happening near the danger.

The spike ball technique, he had decided, was a last resort.

His preference was the forest run.

The forest run was exactly what it sounded like: running through the forest at the maximum speed a hedgehog could sustain, which was faster than most people expected. Hedgehogs were not built for speed in the way that foxes were, but they were built for sustained, purposeful movement, and Humphrey's legs were in excellent condition from years of nightly circuits.

The spike ball had one advantage over the forest run, which was that it worked without effort. You simply existed inside it. The spines did the rest.

But the forest run had better scenery.

Tonight's route took him along the old wall, under the garden gate (a squeeze, but manageable), across the compost corner (which always smelled interesting), through the gap in the hawthorn hedge, and along the ditch to the stone trough where slugs gathered in wet weather.

The slugs were there.

Humphrey ate several with the practiced efficiency of an animal that did not need to feel bad about this. Slugs were, in the ecology of the garden, his to eat.

He completed the forest run by three in the morning and was back in his den under the woodpile, curled in the ordinary sleeping curl, not the defensive one.


Humphrey was back in the woodpile den at three in the morning, which was his preferred bedtime. His route had been clean and productive. The slugs had cooperated. The large orange cat had been absent.

He curled — not the spike ball, the sleeping curl — and let the night go on without him. The garden was his to patrol but not to worry about.

The spikes, when he slept, were barely visible. In repose, Humphrey looked surprisingly soft.
