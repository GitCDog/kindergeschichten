The nebula was sixty light-years across.

It had been forming for four million years from the remnant gas and dust of a star that had ended its life in a supernova — a catastrophic collapse and explosion that had scattered its outer layers across a region of space that the cloud was still slowly expanding into.

The curious nebula's colors, as seen through telescopes sensitive to specific wavelengths, were extraordinary: hydrogen glowing red, oxygen glowing blue-green, sulfur adding a darker red-orange at the edges.

The space art of the nebula was not intentional.

Art requires intention, and the nebula was a physical process: gas and dust heated by radiation from young stars at the nebula's center, the colors a consequence of the specific atomic transitions of specific elements when hit with specific energies of light.

And yet.

An astronomer named Dr. Chen had spent twenty years studying nebulae, and she would not have called them anything other than art. Not metaphorically. She meant it seriously.

The color mist of the nebula's active regions — where new stars were forming from the denser parts of the cloud, where the radiation from those new stars was ionizing the surrounding gas and causing it to glow — was a system of cause and effect so complex and beautiful that the distinction between process and art seemed, to Dr. Chen, not particularly useful.

She had a photograph of the nebula on her office wall.

Students who came to her office always looked at it.

"Is it real?" they always asked.

"The colors are real," she would say. "The image is a translation. But the colors are exactly what you'd see with the right instruments."

The space art was exactly what it was.


Dr. Chen's paper on the nebula's star formation rates was published the following spring. It included six images, each one a different wavelength, each one showing a different aspect of the same object.

The color mist, in the combined-light image on the paper's cover, was something that the journal's editorial team had debated putting on the cover because it looked too beautiful to be science.

They put it on the cover.

The space art and the science were, in this case, the same image.
