At the top of the dead oak at the end of the meadow, a cheerful owl named Wink kept the night watch. She had been keeping it for six years from the same branch, and she considered it the finest office in the county.

The blink was her signature.

Most owls didn't blink much — their eyes were too large, too specialized for night vision, too important to close. But Wink blinked often and deliberately, with the deliberateness of someone who had decided that blinking was something worth doing properly.

Her night watch was methodical.

She began with the meadow — a slow scan from left to right, her head rotating in that smooth, mechanical way that always startled people who saw it for the first time. The meadow told her things by what moved: a vole, probably, at the far hedge. The grass bending showed the wind direction. The stillness of the central zone meant nothing was there, or something was very still.

Then the tree line.

Then the sky — a brief upward check for anything large and unfriendly, which was rarely necessary but worth the scan.

The tree home behind her was a cavity in the oak, dry and dark and lined with the years of shed feathers she hadn't removed because they made good insulation. Her three owlets were in it, sleeping the deep sleep of things that knew they were looked after.

She blinked.

The meadow was quiet. The vole had moved to the hedge gap and was stationary. A moth blundered past her branch and was categorized and released. The wind picked up briefly and then settled.

Wink hunted twice before midnight and was back on her branch before the dew began.

Owlets sleeping, meadow accounted for, sky clear.

She blinked again, and the night continued.


The owlets stirred around three and she heard them settle again. She fed them at four — a vole, caught cleanly — and returned to the branch before the first suggestion of grey appeared in the east.

The night watch ended when the crows woke, their calls marking the boundary between Wink's territory and the rest of the day. She returned to the roost hole and arranged herself in the dark.

The meadow was accounted for. The night had been well kept.
