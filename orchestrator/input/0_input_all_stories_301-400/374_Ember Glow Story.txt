The fire had been burning since seven and was now, at ten, in its best phase.

Flames had given way to embers. The dramatic part — the light, the heat that pushed you back, the particular sound of combustion — had settled into something quieter. The tiny ember glow was steadier and deeper than any flame, and the warm light it gave was gentler and more generous.

The firepit was in the garden, surrounded by old bricks arranged in a loose circle, and the family had been sitting around it in various configurations since supper.

The ember's name — if anyone had thought to give the particular ember closest to the center one — might have been Core. Core was the remnant of a large beech log that had burned down through its phases: ignition, full combustion, char, and now this last form, an ember the shape of its original piece of wood but light as paper and holding, somewhere inside it, a warmth that continued without visible flame.

The warm light spread across the circle of faces.

It was the oldest light humans had gathered around, and some part of the people sitting there knew this without articulating it — knew that the firepit pulled at something deep and old in them, something that had nothing to do with the modern hour and everything to do with the long previous history of people and fire and circles.

The youngest member of the family was asleep in a chair, her face turned toward the fire dot, the warm light on her closed eyes.

No one moved her.

The embers pulsed very faintly — not randomly but in the small rhythms of combustion ending, heat escaping, the fire dot making its way to grey ash.

By midnight it was ash.

The warmth remained in the bricks until morning.


The family went inside one by one as the evening cooled. The last to go was the father, who stayed until the ember glow was very low and the warmth was mostly in the bricks and not the air.

He put the fireguard in place — a routine gesture, performed without thought, the final action of an evening fire.

The warm light continued for another hour in the hot bricks, invisible from inside the house but present in the garden, diminishing slowly toward morning.
