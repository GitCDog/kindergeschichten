The mountain slope was still in the early morning, before the lifts ran and the regular traffic began. In this window — perhaps two hours, perhaps less — the curious ski trail that ran from the high point down through the birch stand and out onto the long meadow was entirely untracked.

The skier's name was Anna, and she had been waiting for this for three days.

The fresh snow was thirty centimeters deep, fallen overnight, undisturbed. The fast glide on fresh snow was a specific sensation she had been chasing since she first skied twenty years ago: not the speed of packed piste, but the floating, resistance-free movement through powder, where the skis sit just below the surface and the body is suspended in a way that had no good comparison outside of water.

Anna pushed off from the top.

The ski trail curved left through the birches. She followed it not as a fixed route but as a suggestion — taking the turns slightly wider than the trail indicated, finding her own line through the trees, the powder rising in a spray from her outer ski on each turn.

The mountain slope tilted and she let it carry her.

In deep snow, speed was managed by the body's angle rather than the edge work that packed snow required. She leaned back slightly, kept her tips up, let the mountain do what mountains do.

The birch stand opened into the meadow.

Out here the slope was gentler and she straightened up, the fast glide becoming a long, straight run that ended where the meadow flattened out near the tree line. She stopped and turned.

Her tracks ran behind her — two parallel curves through the white, the only marks on the slope.

For about forty seconds, before the wind started filling them in, they were perfect.


Anna skied the same trail three more times that morning, each run slightly different — a new choice of line through the birches, a different weight on the downhill — and by the fourth run the snow was tracked and no longer fresh.

She stopped at the bottom of the meadow and looked back up at the slope.

Her four runs were written in the mountain in parallel curves. The morning had been hers. The fast glide on fresh snow could not be taken back.
