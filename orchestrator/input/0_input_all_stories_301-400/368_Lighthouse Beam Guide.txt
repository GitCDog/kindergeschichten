The lighthouse was named North Point, and she had been at the end of the headland for one hundred and thirty years.

She was older than anyone currently alive who used her, which she found appropriate. A lighthouse's value was in being reliably there — not in being new, not in being updated (though her light had been updated several times), but in being at the point where she had always been, visible from the shipping lane, part of the chart.

The night guide function began at dusk.

North Point's light was automated now — had been for twenty years — and the beam light that swept the sea every eight seconds was produced by a compact modern lamp rather than the original oil mechanism. But the beam traveled the same distance and with the same pattern that the chart showed: one flash every eight seconds, visible for twenty-six miles in clear weather.

Ships knew her by her pattern.

On foggy nights, the fog signal added its low, sustained blast — two seconds on, five seconds off, repeating — that the fishing boats knew as well as they knew their own engine sounds.

A family had recently arrived at the small house below the lighthouse — the lighthouse keeper's house, now converted to a holiday rental — and the children had asked if they could go up.

She had been showing them, three times a day since they arrived, what a lighthouse does: she turned.

The eldest child had stood at the top and watched the beam rotate and gone very quiet in the way that people go quiet when they understand something about scale.

The light swept the water, eight seconds, sweep.

Below, the boats moved on their routes.

The ship safe was the whole purpose, carried forward one rotation at a time.


The eldest child came back to the base of the lighthouse on the last day of the holiday and looked up at the lens assembly through the glass of the lamp room.

She didn't speak for a while.

'It's always been here,' she said finally.

'Longer than any of us,' her father said.

The beam light swept the morning sea, as it had swept the night sea, as it would sweep the sea tonight and every night after, regardless of who was watching.
