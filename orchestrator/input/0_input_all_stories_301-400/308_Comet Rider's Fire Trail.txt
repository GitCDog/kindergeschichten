There were many ways to travel through the solar system, but none of them compared to riding a comet.

Zara knew this because she had tried all of them. She had ridden solar winds, which were fast but directionless. She had hitched a path on an asteroid, which was dull — asteroids didn't go anywhere interesting. She had even, once, followed a space probe all the way to the outer planets, which was fine but uncomfortably quiet.

Comet riding was different.

A comet had intention. It was going somewhere specific — toward the sun, always — and the journey had shape and momentum. The fire tail behind a comet was not actually fire but a stream of gas and dust boiled off the nucleus by the sun's heat, and riding just ahead of it meant traveling in a cone of light that turned the whole journey golden.

Zara's current comet was named Hale — not the famous one, a smaller one that visited the inner system every thirty-one years and was known mainly to the people who knew what to look for.

She stood on Hale's nucleus — a dark, lumpy potato of ice and rock about two kilometers across — and watched the fire tail streaming out behind them for fifty thousand kilometers.

The space trail was getting longer as they approached the sun.

This was the best part of comet riding: the approach. The further they came into the warmth, the brighter the tail, the faster the ice boiled and the gas streamed. They were accelerating. By the time they swung around the sun and began the long journey back to the outer dark, Hale would be moving at seventy kilometers per second.

The fire tail burned behind her like a road made of light.

Zara looked forward toward the growing brightness of the sun and felt the particular joy of moving very fast in a very clear direction.

Some journeys were complicated. This one was simple: toward the warmth, then away, then toward again.


Zara had memorized the fire tail's color — not orange but a complex, shifting white-gold. It was the color of something being transformed rather than simply burning. She watched it for a long time before looking ahead again.

The sun was still days away. The tail was still growing. The journey was at its best in the middle, before arrival, when the fire trail stretched behind her and the destination still held its promise.
