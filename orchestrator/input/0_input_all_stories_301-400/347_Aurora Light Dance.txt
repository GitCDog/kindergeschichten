On a clear night in February, so far north that the sun barely appeared at midday and the stars were out before three in the afternoon, the aurora came.

The magic aurora light did not announce itself. It appeared — first a pale green shimmer at the horizon, easy to miss, something you might think was reflection from a distant city. Then brighter, more defined, beginning to move.

The sky ribbon rose from the horizon and arched overhead.

Watching the color wave of an aurora was not like watching a firework, which was brief and singular. It was more like watching weather — large-scale, slow-moving, complex, always changing but never completely surprising. You could see it develop. You could see what was coming, a little.

A girl named Lena was watching from the wooden steps of the research station, wrapped in four layers and a hat, her breath making small clouds in the minus-twenty air.

The aurora's color wave shifted — the pale green deepening, a red appearing at the top of the arch, then purple at the edges. The ribbon rippled, which was the movement that always took her breath away: the whole vast structure moving in waves, as if something very large were shaking it from one end.

She did not move for forty minutes.

When she finally went inside, her eyelashes had frost on them.

She knew, from the physicist who had explained it to her, that the aurora was charged particles from the sun interacting with the atmosphere. She held this knowledge and also held the experience of watching it, and she found that neither diminished the other.

The sky ribbon continued after she went in, indifferent to observation, performing for no one.

In the morning, the sky was clear and blue and completely ordinary.


The aurora faded before midnight. Not dramatically — the sky ribbon simply gentled, the color wave dimming, the movement slowing. By one in the morning the sky was ordinary dark again, the stars visible but the aurora gone.

Lena went inside and wrote three sentences in her journal. She could have written more, but three sentences was what it had been: something seen, something felt, something that would stay.
