The fox moved through the forest the way water moved through a landscape — following the easiest path, but always moving.

Her name, if foxes had names, might have been Blaze for the color of her coat — the particular red that the low morning light turned copper, the red that the forest light scattered into orange and gold depending on the angle. The red blur in the undergrowth. The quick fire that moved between the trees and was gone.

The fox tail was behind her always, full and white-tipped, perfectly counterbalancing her turns.

The forest dash was not panic. It was joy.

Blaze ran because running felt correct at this hour, in this light. The morning air was cold and clear and the forest smelled of fungi and wet earth and the particular mineral smell of early autumn that had no better name. Her legs were working exactly as legs were built to work, the muscles firing in sequence, the pace sustainable for miles.

She ran the length of the stream, turned left at the oak, crossed the field at a dead run because the field was open and open space was a decision to be made quickly and committed to — no half-measures in the open.

The far hedge. The lane. Over.

The wood on the other side was beech — older trees, wider spaced, the floor clear except for the copper leaf litter. Good running. Her paws found purchase on the leaf-covered soil, each stride quiet.

She slowed near the old badger sett — not from fear of the badgers, who were sleeping, but out of a general principle: slow near occupied territory.

Then fast again.

The forest dash carried her in a wide circuit back toward her home territory, her breath making small clouds in the morning air, her tail streaming behind her like a white flame.


The circuit completed, Blaze arrived at her earth as the sun was fully risen. She entered without slowing, the earth entrance exactly calibrated to her body width after years of use.

Inside was dark and warm and smelled of herself and her family. She curled in the usual way — nose to tail, the red blur now simply a fox in a fox-shaped space — and let the morning go on above her.

The forest dash was done. The wood was mapped. Everything was as it should be.
