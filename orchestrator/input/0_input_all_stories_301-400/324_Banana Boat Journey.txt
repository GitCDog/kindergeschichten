The banana boat was not a boat made of bananas.

This was the first thing anyone asked when they saw it, and the friendly banana boat — whose actual name was Nana — wanted it on the record. She was a boat that carried bananas. A commercial vessel with a shallow hull, a wide cargo hold, and a route that took her up and down the jungle river four times a week.

The river ride was her whole life, and she loved it.

The jungle trip began each morning at the dock below the plantation, where workers loaded the green banana bunches into her hold in long efficient lines. Each bunch was numbered and tagged. The smell was extraordinary — green and raw and slightly sweet and completely unlike ripe bananas, being more like the smell of the tree than the fruit.

Then the cast-off, and the river.

Nana rode the current where the current ran in the right direction and motored against it where it didn't. She knew the river so well she could navigate it in the dark — which she had done, twice, during power failures that had taken out the dock lights.

The jungle pressed close on both banks. In the morning, parrots flew across the water in flashing red and green. In the afternoon, the light through the canopy turned the river gold. Once, a tapir had waded into the shallows to drink and had watched Nana pass with the calm attention of an animal that didn't know she was impressive.

At the far dock, the bananas were offloaded.

Nana turned around and made the return journey empty, which was her favorite way to travel — riding higher in the water, quicker and more responsive, the jungle trip still going but without the weight of it.

She made harbor at dusk, exactly on schedule.


The best part of the return journey was the silence where the cargo had been. The hold echoed differently when empty — a larger sound, more resonant. Nana had never told anyone this, because boats don't have conversations, but she held it anyway.

The river ran her home in the dusk with the jungle on both sides and the first stars appearing above the canopy, and she was content with the thing she had been built for.
