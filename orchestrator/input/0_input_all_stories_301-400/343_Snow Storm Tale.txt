The storm's name was White, and White arrived from the north without announcement on a Thursday afternoon when everyone had been expecting it for days.

The weather service had given warnings. The schools had been consulted. The icy gust had been tracked across the sea on satellite images — a great circling system of cloud and cold that had organized itself somewhere over the Arctic and was now here, fully formed and purposeful.

White was brave in the way that large things are brave: without effort, through sheer scale.

The first flakes arrived at two o'clock — small and separate, falling straight down through still air, settling on the pavement as individual crystals before the temperature dropped enough for them to hold. Then more, and faster, and by three o'clock the icy gust had arrived with the main body of the storm and the world went white.

The white world appeared in stages.

First the grass disappeared. Then the rooftops. Then the cars. By four o'clock the street had lost its edges and the garden walls had lost their tops and the long path to the park gate was indistinguishable from the park.

Inside the houses, warm and still, people watched from windows.

A child pressed her face to the glass and made a breath-fog circle on it, then drew a snowflake in the fog with one finger.

Outside, White continued the work of transformation. Sound changed — not quieter exactly, but different in character, the snow absorbing the frequencies that would otherwise bounce off hard surfaces and returning a muffled, padded version of the world.

By evening, White had settled into her steady phase — not the arriving storm but the present one, still falling, still covering, still quietly making the world new.

The child was already asleep when the snow stopped.

The white world held, clean and unmarked, until morning.


In the morning the white world was ordinary again — familiar, textured by footprints and boot trails and a bird's precise signature across the garden. But in those hours of arrival, White had made the town new.

The child who had drawn the snowflake on the window found it still there in the morning, preserved by the cold, her small mark on the white world's edge.
