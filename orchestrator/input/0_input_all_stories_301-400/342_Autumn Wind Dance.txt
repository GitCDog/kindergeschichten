Every October the wind held its great cheerful autumn festival.

No invitations were sent, because the wind didn't need to invite the leaves. The leaves were ready. By mid-October they had loosened their hold on the branches — the tree's deliberate release, the stem growing a separation layer that let go cleanly in the first good gust — and the leaf swirl could begin.

The autumn wind's name, in the local tradition, was Merry, and Merry was particularly exuberant this year.

She arrived from the northwest on a Tuesday morning and hit the beeches first, because the beeches were always first. Their leaves turned later but released earlier, and the color dance they produced was the purest: amber and copper, with a few still-green stragglers that Merry pulled along despite their reluctance.

Then the oaks. Slower to release, tougher in their hold, their leaves darker and more stubbornly attached. But Merry was patient. She worked the oaks over several hours, returning in gusts, each one claiming a few more.

By afternoon the air was full.

A woman who had been watching from her garden for twenty years said that this year's color dance was the finest she had seen. Merry, who could not hear this but was somewhere overhead carrying leaves across the village, was doing her best regardless.

The children ran through the deepening piles in the park. Each step sent a small explosion of color into the air — a miniature leaf swirl per footfall. They ran for an hour without a clear purpose and with great purpose simultaneously.

Merry moved east as the evening came, her gusts gentling. Behind her, the trees were lighter. The leaves she had moved were settling into their winter positions — against fences, in ditches, beneath hedgerows.

The autumn festival wound down, as it always did, into the quiet of November's beginning.


By evening the parks were deep in color, the leaf swirl of the afternoon settled into drifts against benches and walls. Children who walked home from school kicked their way through them with the wholehearted pleasure of people doing exactly what the season was designed for.

Merry moved further east, gentling into the quiet of night. Behind her, the trees stood lighter and the autumn color dance was done for another year.
