In the base of the old eucalyptus tree, in a colony of four hundred thousand, there was a curious fire ant named Dot who had an unusual habit: she noticed things.

Not the things that mattered for ant work — pheromone trails, food sources, queen signals — she noticed those too, as all ants did. But she also noticed the tiny world in a way that her colony-mates didn't seem to.

She noticed the exact texture of bark. She noticed how the light changed at different depths of the nest. She noticed that rain, when it fell above the nest entrance, changed the sound underground from silence to a distant percussion.

The team work that ants were famous for was invisible from inside it.

From Dot's position — a single worker among four hundred thousand — she couldn't see the elegant organization of the colony. She could see the ant in front of her and the ant behind her, and she could read the chemical signals that told her where to go and what to do. But the whole was invisible from inside the part.

One morning, she emerged from a secondary entrance she hadn't used before and found herself outside the colony's normal territory. She was on a different root, facing a different direction, looking at a section of the tiny world she had never mapped.

New bark texture. Different light angle. A small seed she didn't recognize.

She picked up the seed — which was large relative to her, but she was an ant and large was relative — and brought it back to the nest entrance, following the pheromone trail backward.

The seed was added to the colony's store.

Dot returned to her regular route, carrying the small expanded knowledge of a new corridor and a strange seed, which the colony would absorb and act on without knowing who had brought the information.

The team work was made of exactly these moments: individual noticing, collective use.


Dot made the same route the following day and the day after. The new corridor was becoming familiar, her chemical trail strengthening each time she passed.

Other ants from the colony began using it. This was how routes became established: one ant's repeated path becoming a chemical road, a road becoming a thoroughfare.

Dot no longer walked the tiny world alone on that route. She had been followed by four hundred thousand without any of them knowing who had gone first.
