The cave had the best echo in the valley, and everyone who knew the valley knew this.

It was a shallow cave — not deep enough to be dark, just deep enough to be interesting — cut into the limestone cliff face where the river bent east. The acoustic geometry was, apparently, excellent. Sound went in, bounced off the curved back wall, and returned with a delay of approximately one and a half seconds, softened and rounded by the journey.

The silly voice who used the echo most was not a real voice, exactly — it was the cave sound, which was every voice that had ever been played into it and returned changed.

The echo voice game had as many versions as players.

The simplest version: stand at the cave mouth and say something. Wait one and a half seconds. Hear yourself, differently.

The repeat game added a layer: could you say the next line of a sentence so that your echo from the previous line landed between the words? Children spent considerable time on this and rarely succeeded, because the repeat game required more precise timing than most people had. But the failures were funnier than the successes.

A boy named Sam had developed the cave sound game of his own invention: he played a known tune — something simple, four or five notes — and then listened as the cave returned it. Then played the next phrase so that it overlapped with the echo of the first. He was building a round with one voice and one cave.

It sort of worked.

It worked enough.

The echo voice reached him from the curved wall, softened and hollow, and he listened to himself differently.

The cave echo game never ran out. There was always more to say, and always the cave to say it back to you.


Sam left the cave before dusk and found, on the walk home, that he was humming the tune he had built with the cave sound. Not the whole thing — just the phrase he had put in, separated from its echo.

The repeat game stayed with you. That was the thing about making music in a cave: the echo taught you what you sounded like from the outside.

He hummed it all the way home.
