In the back of a curiosity shop at the end of Wren Street, on a shelf between a compass that pointed south and a clock with no hands, there was a magic crystal ball named Clear who had been predicting futures since before the shop existed.

Clear was not dishonest about her limitations. She could see possible futures — not certain ones. Futures were not fixed, she had learned. They were tendencies. Directions. The most probable shape of things to come, given everything she knew about the present.

The future glow was the particular quality of light that appeared inside her when she looked forward in time. Not bright exactly — more like the light inside a cloud just before the sun breaks through. Soft. Anticipatory.

One afternoon a boy named Oliver came into the shop and stood in front of her for a long time.

He wasn't a customer. He was the shopkeeper's son, and he came in most afternoons, and he always stopped at Clear's shelf.

"What do you see?" he asked her today, as he often did.

Clear held his question carefully and looked.

She saw: a boy who was good at mathematics standing in a room full of books, older, with a cup of tea. She saw the same boy, different age, on a boat, squinting at the horizon. She saw him at a desk, writing something. She saw him — very old now — in a garden, laughing.

None of these futures were fixed. They were possibilities, gathered around the present like petals around a center.

She had no voice to tell him this. But she glowed.

Oliver watched the vision spark move inside her — a slow, warm light that traveled from her center to her surface and then gentled back.

"Good?" he asked.

The glow pulsed once.

He seemed to understand this well enough. He always did. This was, perhaps, why she glowed more for him than for anyone else who stopped at her shelf.

He went home for dinner.

Clear's vision quest resumed in the quiet of the closed shop, the possibilities of all her futures turning gently inside her glass.
