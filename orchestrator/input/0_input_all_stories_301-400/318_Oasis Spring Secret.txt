The desert knew about the oasis, of course. Everything in the desert did. The birds navigated by it. The lizards oriented themselves toward it. Even the wind seemed to pass through it more gently, as though aware that something delicate was being protected.

But the oasis spring had a secret that even the desert didn't know.

The hidden water was deeper than anyone suspected.

Beneath the pool that was visible — the small, clear oval that the palm trees ringed, that the camels drank from, that the traveling families would find and call miraculous — there was another water. An underground lake of ancient water, held in porous rock three hundred meters below the surface, accumulated over twelve thousand years from rainfall that had fallen on distant mountains.

The spring was just the top of a very long story.

A water sprite named Sip lived at the connection point between the ancient water below and the oasis pool above, and she was the keeper of the hidden water's secret.

Her job was not to guard it, exactly. Water doesn't need guarding — it needs to be understood. The oasis was sustained because the rock let the ancient water rise slowly, steadily, at a rate that the desert above could receive without overwhelming.

Too fast and the oasis would flood. Too slow and the palm shade would die.

Sip maintained the rate.

She did this by knowing the weight of what was above her — the water in the pool, the soil around it, the roots of the palm trees reaching down — and adjusting accordingly, the way a living thing adjusts to the conditions it finds itself in.

No one above the surface knew she was there.

But travelers who found the oasis and drank its water and sat in the palm shade often said that the water had a particular quality — cool and mineral and old, as if it came from somewhere very deep.

They were exactly right.


Sip had been tending the hidden water for longer than the palm trees above had been alive. She would tend it longer than they survived, too, adjusting and maintaining through the slow changes of climate and geology that shifted the desert over centuries.

The travelers who came to the oasis and found it did not know this. They knew only that the water was there, and it was real, and it was enough.

Sip worked below them in the dark and was content with being the unseen reason for something good.
