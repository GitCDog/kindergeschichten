The space suit's name, in the way that working equipment often gets a name, was White.

White had been on three spacewalks.

The cheerful space suit was not a garment in the ordinary sense. She was a personal spacecraft — a self-contained life support system that happened to be worn rather than sat in, providing the astronaut inside her with pressure, oxygen, temperature regulation, communication, and protection from the radiation of unfiltered space.

Each layer of White performed a function.

The innermost layer was a liquid cooling garment — tubes of water circulating against the astronaut's skin, carrying away the heat that the suit's other layers would otherwise trap. The next layers were pressure bladders and structural elements. The outer layer was a white fabric that reflected the sun's thermal radiation.

The star walk began when the airlock depressurized.

Astronaut Dr. Kim floated through the airlock hatch and was outside the station.

Outside meant: the suit and whatever it contained against the rest of the universe. Two hundred and fifty miles below, the Earth turned. Above, no atmosphere, no molecules of air, just sunlight and the cold dark.

White maintained everything. The pressure in her inner layers kept Dr. Kim's body functioning. The oxygen system kept her breathing. The thermal regulation kept her neither frozen nor cooked by the sun that was, without the buffer of the atmosphere, considerably more intense than anything experienced at the Earth's surface.

Dr. Kim worked on the external component she had been assigned to replace.

The zero float of the environment made this both easier (no gravity to fight) and more difficult (no gravity to provide resistance).

White held.

Two hours later, the airlock cycled. White had kept Dr. Kim alive in the most hostile environment reachable by humans, and she had done it without drama, which was the best kind of performance.


Dr. Kim described the spacewalk that evening in her video log: the view of the Earth from outside the station, the way the sun hit the thermal tiles differently when there was no atmosphere to moderate it, the particular quality of silence in a suit — not quiet, because the systems were audible, but sealed, complete, contained.

White was hung back in her locker, checked, inventoried, prepped for the next use.

The star walk had been good work. Both of them knew it.
