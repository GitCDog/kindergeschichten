The friendly star guide's name was Polaris, and she had been guiding travelers for as long as travelers had been looking up.

Polaris was, of course, a real star — the North Star, the star closest to the celestial north pole, the star that appeared fixed in the northern sky while all other stars moved around it as the Earth rotated. But she was also, in the old tradition, a guide: a point of reference that navigators had used for millennia.

The night map she provided was simple.

Find north, and you know the other directions. You know where you are relative to the poles. You know, in the northern hemisphere, that you are looking at the star that sailors had used to cross oceans and travelers had used to navigate deserts and armies had used to move at night.

The light path from Polaris to the eye was four hundred and thirty-three years long.

This was what the star guide's light had been traveling when it arrived at someone's eye tonight: four hundred and thirty-three years of journey through empty space, the light left the star before the telescope was invented, before the scientific revolution, in a world where navigators with no other instruments relied on exactly this star to tell them where they were.

A girl lay in the garden looking up.

She had learned to find Polaris by finding the Big Dipper first, then following the two stars at the end of its cup in the direction they pointed.

She found it now — steady, reliable, exactly where it always was.

The star guide looked down at her.

She looked back.

Across four hundred and thirty-three years of light, the two of them were briefly in conversation.
