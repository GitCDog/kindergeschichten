The sailor hat had been in the family for three summers.

It was a proper naval cap — white with a black brim, a gold anchor badge on the front, and a red elastic strap that went under the chin and had been chewed on at one end by a dog at some point before it entered the family's possession. The dog damage didn't affect the hat's performance.

The brave sailor hat's name was Cap, and Cap had been to sea.

Not naval sea — the sea that mattered for Cap's purposes was a six-meter sailing dinghy called Tern, on which a family of four went every August to the estuary where the river met the coast and the sailing was manageable for people who were good but not professional.

The sea wind arrived from the southwest in August, as it reliably did, and Cap's primary function was to remain on the head of the smallest crew member — a boy named Joss — while this sea wind was in operation.

This required the chin strap.

Joss had, initially, resisted the chin strap on grounds of appearance. Cap had no way to express his views on this but they would have been: the chin strap was designed specifically for the sea wind, and the sea wind did not care about appearances.

Joss learned this when Cap blew off in a gust on the first August outing and was retrieved from the water with a boat hook.

After that, chin strap.

The ship ride that August was the best they had done — five hours on the water, a fair sea wind the whole time, Joss learning to crew properly under a sky that was blue and wide and full of nothing but sun and cloud and sea.

Cap held on.


The last day on the water, Cap had performed his finest service: a gust at exactly the wrong moment caught the boom, the boat heeled sharply, and Cap went sideways but the chin strap held and Joss kept his hands on the sheet and the boat came level.

The sea wind and the chin strap and Joss's hands: a partnership.

Cap was hung up at the end of August smelling of salt and sunscreen and a summer well spent.
