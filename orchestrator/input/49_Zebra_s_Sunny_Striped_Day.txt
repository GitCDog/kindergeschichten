Once upon a time, on a golden savanna where the grass whispered and the acacia trees cast long shadows in the morning, there lived a little zebra named Zara. [long pause]
Zara had perfectly crisp black and white stripes — except for one. On her left shoulder, one stripe was not quite straight. It curved slightly, like a small smile, in a way no other zebra in the herd shared. Zara had spent a great deal of time when she was younger wishing it was straight. She spent considerably less time thinking about it now. [sighs]
Because the striped adventure had changed that. [beat]
It had begun on an ordinary Tuesday, when Zara was grazing at the edge of the herd and a small weaver bird landed on her back — right on the crooked stripe — and said, very matter-of-factly: "I've been looking for this spot for weeks."
Zara turned her head carefully. "That spot in particular?"
"That exact curve," said the bird. "It's the right shape for what I need." He produced, from somewhere, a single long grass stem, and laid it precisely along the curve of the crooked stripe. It fit perfectly. [with growing realization]
"What are you doing?" Zara asked.
"Measuring," said the bird. "I'm building a nest. I need to know the curve of the arc." He nodded, satisfied, picked up his grass stem, and flew away.
[pacing] Zara stood in the savanna sun and thought about this. Her crooked stripe — the one she had spent years feeling self-conscious about — had turned out to be exactly the right shape for something. She just hadn't known what yet.
She walked through the rest of that golden day differently. Chin up. Stripes forward. The savanna sun warm on her back and the grass sweet under her feet. [softly]
The weaver bird came back the next morning to check the measurement again, and three mornings after that.
Zara always stood very still while he worked. She had decided it was an honor.
What feels like a flaw, she understood, is sometimes just a feature waiting to be needed.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
