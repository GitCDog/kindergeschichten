Once upon a time, in a garden where the rain came often and the paths between the flowerbeds were silver with puddles, there lived a silly snail named Sid. [long pause]
Sid moved slowly — slower than slow, slower than watching paint dry, slower than the hour hand on the old clock in the garden shed. He knew this about himself and had made his peace with it long ago. What he had not made his peace with was everyone else acting as though slowness was a problem. [sighs]
So when the garden announced its First Ever Slow Race, Sid signed up immediately. [beat]
The rules were simple: start at the rose bed, finish at the garden wall. Fastest wins. Slowest also wins, because this was that kind of race.
On the morning of the race, the sky turned grey and a light rain began. The other competitors — a tortoise, a caterpillar, and a particularly unhurried beetle — huddled under the broad leaves of the hostas. Sid did not huddle. [with growing realization]
Sid had prepared.
He had spent three days searching the garden for the perfect fallen leaf — large enough to cover him completely, dry and slightly curved so the rain ran off the edges. He had found it under the oak tree on Thursday: a magnificent auburn oak leaf, broad as a dinner plate.
He balanced it carefully on his shell. [softly] A perfect leaf umbrella.
Then he set off.
The rain pattered down. The other competitors got steadily damper and crosser. Sid moved at his usual pace — stately, unhurried, absolutely dry — down the garden path while the rain drummed pleasantly on his leaf umbrella overhead. [pacing]
It took him forty-five minutes to reach the garden wall.
He arrived last. He arrived completely dry. He arrived with the expression of someone who has just had a very pleasant walk in the rain.
[abruptly] The garden erupted in applause — from the earthworms, who appreciated the rain and had come out to watch, and from the robin on the fence post, who gave a short celebratory song.
Sid took a small, dignified bow. [beat]
Going slowly, he had always believed, just means you have more time to be clever about it.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
