Once upon a time, on a shallow pink lake at the edge of a great salt flat, there lived a friendly flamingo named Flora. [long pause]
The pink lake was pink because of tiny creatures in the water that the flamingos ate — and the more they ate, the pinker the flamingos became, which was a system Flora found satisfying in its logic. She was a deep, warm pink, which the other flamingos admired, and she stood on one leg without thinking about it, the way you or I might sit in a chair: simply the most natural position available. [sighs with elegant composure]
Flora loved to dance. [beat]
Flamingo dancing is a serious matter. Not frivolous — structured and precise, with specific head movements and synchronized steps that the whole flock learns together. Flora had mastered the flock dances years ago. But she had also, privately, been working on something else.
A solo dance. Entirely her own. [softly]
She had been composing it for a year — a piece that used the full range of what a flamingo could do: the long neck, the unexpected wingspan, the precision of one-legged balance, and the way that turning on that one leg in still water created circles of ripple that moved outward across the whole pink lake.
She practiced in the early mornings before the flock woke. [with growing realization]
On the morning of the spring equinox, when the light came low and golden across the pink lake and turned everything to rose and amber, Flora went to the center of the shallows alone.
She began. [pacing]
The one-leg stand first — steady, still, reflecting perfectly in the water below. Then the turn — slow and complete, ripples spreading. Then the wings: not for flight but for expression, opening to their full extraordinary span, catching the morning light on every feather. The neck curved. The head dipped and rose in the patterns she had spent a year composing.
The flock woke slowly on the bank and watched in silence. [softly]
When she finished and folded her wings and stood again on one leg, perfectly still, the ripples from her dance were still crossing the pink lake in long slow rings.
A very old flamingo on the bank said: "I've never seen that before."
"No," said Flora. "You haven't."
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
