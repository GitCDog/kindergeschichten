Once upon a time, on the first true morning of winter, when the world had gone white overnight and everything was still and hushed and full of possibility, a sparkly snowman appeared in the middle of the village green. [long pause]
No one had built him — or at least, no one would admit to it. He was simply there: three perfect spheres of snow, a jaunty scarf the color of holly berries, two coal eyes that twinkled with unmistakable warmth, and a carrot nose that curved slightly to the left in a way that gave him a permanent air of mild surprise. [sighs with wonder]
The children found him first, as children always do. [beat]
"He sparkles," said the youngest, who was four and therefore trusted her own observations completely.
The others looked. She was right. In the low winter sunlight, the snowman sparkled — not like ordinary snow, but like something had been mixed into him: crushed ice, or stars, or the particular magic of the first snowfall of the year.
"Good morning," said the snowman.
The children stared. [abruptly]
"Oh, don't look so alarmed," the snowman said pleasantly. "It's a winter wonderland. Extraordinary things happen all the time."
He spent the morning telling them about the winter wonderland — about the sleep of the seeds under the frozen ground, about the way frost writes its name on windows, about the cardinal who was the one spot of red in the whole white world and knew it. [pacing]
He told them that his carrot nose had been chosen specifically for its slight leftward curve, because straight noses looked too serious. He demonstrated, with great dignity, how to properly catch a snowflake on your tongue. He laughed — a soft, snowy sound — at all the right moments. [softly]
By afternoon the sun had moved and he had begun to soften at the edges.
[with growing realization] "Will you be here tomorrow?" the youngest asked.
The snowman considered this. "I will be here in every snowfall," he said. "You'll recognize me by the sparkle."
She nodded, satisfied, and went home for lunch.
And the winter wonderland settled quietly around the village, full of secrets and cold and the particular magic of things that come only once a year.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
