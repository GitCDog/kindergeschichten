Once upon a time, in a forest where the oak trees grew thick and the autumn wind smelled of rain and earth, there lived a curious squirrel named Scout. [long pause]
Scout had a particularly bushy tail — even by squirrel standards — and a memory that was, she had to admit, not always reliable. She had buried hundreds of acorns across the nutty forest over the years. She had found perhaps half of them. The rest, she liked to think, had simply become trees. [sighs philosophically]
But this year was different. This year she had found an acorn unlike any she had seen before. [beat]
It was larger than a normal acorn. Its cap had a silvery shimmer to it. And when Scout held it up to the autumn light, something inside it seemed to glow — faintly, like an ember — as if it contained something more than just a seed.
An acorn treasure. [softly]
Scout carried it carefully — more carefully than she had ever carried anything — and spent three full days choosing where to bury it. Under the old beech? Too exposed. By the stream? Too damp. In the middle of the nutty forest where the light came down in long shafts between the old oaks? [with growing realization]
She dug the hole herself, lined it with soft moss, placed the glowing acorn inside, and covered it over.
Then she did something she had never done before. [pacing] She made a proper map. On a piece of birch bark, with a stick dipped in mud, she drew the three nearest trees and marked the spot with a careful X.
She tucked the map in the hollow of her favorite oak and went home to bed.
[beat] That winter, when she was curled in her drey with her tail wrapped around her nose, she sometimes thought about the acorn treasure in the dark ground below the snow. Waiting. Knowing what it was becoming.
In spring, when the snow melted, she went back to check.
[softly] A small green shoot was pushing up through the soil — strong and curved and reaching, with one tiny silver-tipped leaf at its tip.
Scout sat beside it for a very long time. Then she patted the ground gently and went to bury twelve more acorns, just in case.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
