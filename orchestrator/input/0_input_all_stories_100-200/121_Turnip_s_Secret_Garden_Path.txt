The hidden path had always been there. It ran along the back edge of the garden, tucked behind the tall rosemary hedge, so narrow and quiet that only the smallest residents had ever found it. [long pause]

One of those residents was a tiny turnip named Tilda.

She had found the hidden path by accident on an October morning, when she rolled too close to the hedge and tumbled right behind it. She landed on the path and lay still for a moment, looking up at a thin strip of sky between two tall hedges — pale blue, entirely peaceful — and she thought: this is a garden secret, and it belongs to me. [sighs]

She did not tell anyone at first.

She kept it the way you keep a warm thought on a cold day — close to herself, quietly. She would visit it in the early mornings when dew still clung to everything, rolling slowly along the path's short length, noticing the same things each time and finding them newly interesting anyway. [beat]

There was a beetle who lived under a flat stone and emerged each morning to inspect the path for changes. There was a spider's web strung between the two hedges that caught the morning light and held it in perfect bright drops. There was a smooth round stone — worn by years or wind, she wasn't sure which — that made an excellent seat.

In time, Tilda began to wonder if the hidden path wanted to be shared.

She thought about this carefully, as tiny turnips do. A garden secret shared is a different thing from a garden secret kept. It grows a little, changes shape, becomes two people's instead of one. Sometimes that is worse. [softly]

But sometimes it is better.

She chose one friend — a quiet carrot who noticed things and didn't talk more than necessary. The carrot came and stood on the hidden path and looked at the spider's web and the smooth stone and the beetle making his morning rounds. She didn't say anything for a while. [with growing realization]

"I understand why you kept it," the carrot said at last.

"I understand why I shared it," said Tilda.

They sat on the smooth stone together while the morning warmed, and the garden went on being a garden around them, full of all its ordinary things and all its hidden ones. [softly]

[pause] Good night my little friend
