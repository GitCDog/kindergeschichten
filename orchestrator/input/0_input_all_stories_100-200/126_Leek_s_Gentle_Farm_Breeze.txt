The quiet farm at the edge of the valley was the sort of place where you could hear the wind before you felt it — a low movement through the grasses that arrived like a soft announcement. [long pause]

A friendly leek named Lena had lived on this farm her whole life, and she knew the wind the way you know a neighbor: its habits, its moods, the way it changed between morning and evening.

The morning breeze was the gentlest. It arrived just after sunrise, carrying traces of the river below the valley, and it moved through the farm without urgency, touching things lightly and moving on. The leek leaves lifted a little in it and then settled. The seed heads nodded. The whole quiet farm seemed to exhale. [sighs]

Lena loved the morning breeze best.

She had a theory about it — that the morning breeze was the wind on its way somewhere important, and the farm was one of its stops, and it greeted the farm every morning the way travelers greet a place they've passed through before and remember fondly. [beat]

Today the morning breeze arrived a little later than usual and seemed, to Lena, slightly different. Warmer, or maybe softer, as if it had taken a gentler path to get here. It moved across the quiet farm in its usual way — touching the leeks, the carrots, the herbs in the corner bed — but something about it was new.

Lena thought about this.

After a while she decided the breeze had simply changed a little, the way everything living things change a little all the time, and that was fine. You didn't need things to be exactly the same to feel at home with them. [softly]

The afternoon brought a stronger wind that smelled of coming rain, and the evening brought almost no wind at all. The quiet farm settled down in the stillness.

But Lena held onto the memory of the morning breeze — the particular way it had moved through the leek rows, touching everything gently and asking nothing in return, just passing through and leaving things slightly better than it found them. [with growing realization]

That, she thought, was what it meant to be gentle on a farm that had learned the value of quiet.

The stars came out over the valley one by one, and the last of the evening settled across the leek rows like a soft held breath.
