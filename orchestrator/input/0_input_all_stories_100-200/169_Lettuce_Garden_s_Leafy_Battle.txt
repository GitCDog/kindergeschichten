General Greens surveyed his lettuce garden army from the highest point of the compost wall, and he felt — on the whole — quite good about things. [pause] His leaf army was assembled in tidy rows, their broad green heads held high, their stems strong from weeks of good rain and careful tending.

The enemy, in this case, was the slugs.

The slugs came at night, silently and determinedly, and they had been making progress on the outer rows of the garden for three weeks now. Something had to be done. General Greens had called a council.

"We cannot outrun them," said Commander Romaine. "But we can outlast them."

"And we can out-grow them," said Captain Butterhead, who had been experimenting with growing so fast that the outer leaves regrew before the slugs could finish eating them. [softly] The green battle, she had decided, was a battle of renewal rather than resistance.

General Greens listened carefully to everyone. He was a broad-leafed, deep-green fellow of great experience, and he knew that a good commander listens before deciding.

[pause] In the end, the plan was simple: grow. Just grow. Every night, while the slugs did their work at the edges, the inner lettuce garden would put its energy into the center — growing outward from the heart, steady and inexorable, replacing what was lost with what was new.

The slugs, arriving the following night, found that the outer leaves had already begun to grow back. They ate, and the garden grew. They returned the next night, and the garden grew again. [softly] By the end of the month, the lettuce had grown so abundantly that there was more than enough for everyone — the slugs, the kitchen, the garden, and three neighboring salads.

General Greens looked out over his thriving leaf army and felt something even better than victory. He felt generous. He felt lush. He felt, most of all, magnificently, abundantly alive.
