Spring had arrived three days ago, and Aria the asparagus soup had been made to celebrate it. [pause] She was the color of new grass — pale green and clear, with the particular freshness that only early spring asparagus carries, that flavor that tastes of cold mornings and pale sunlight and the first real warmth after a long winter.

The spring bowl had been set out on the porch table, which was perhaps unusual for soup, but the cook had decided that asparagus soup tasted best when you could smell the outdoor air while you ate it. And she had been entirely right.

A light wind moved through the porch. The garden was just beginning — the first green shoots pushing through the still-cold soil, the cherry tree putting out its first cautious blossoms. The smell of fresh earth mixed with the steam from Aria's bowl in a way that felt perfectly, seasonally correct. [softly] This was what spring tasted like when it was in a bowl.

"You smell like outside," said a young child who had sat down beside the bowl.

"I am outside," said Aria, in her soft, spring way. "I was made from the very first asparagus of the season. I carry every cold morning and every pale sunrise of the past week in me."

The child considered this seriously, then picked up the spoon.

[pause] Fresh taste, Aria knew, was the hardest thing to hold onto in cooking. It came and went in an instant — there for the first bite, then settling into something warmer and more familiar. She had been made quickly and carefully, in a light broth, barely cooked, so that the fresh taste would last.

It lasted beautifully.

[softly] When the spring bowl was empty and the child set down the spoon and looked up at the first apple blossoms on the tree overhead, Aria felt that she had given her very best to the season — a fresh taste carried gently from the cold earth into the warm world, celebrating everything that comes back.
