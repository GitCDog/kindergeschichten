There was nothing dramatic about celery soup, and that was exactly what made it perfect. [pause] Clara the celery soup was pale and gentle and served in a white bowl with a swirl of cream on top, and she was the kind of food that arrived quietly and did its quiet work without asking for any particular attention.

She had a soft taste — clean and herby and somehow both subtle and present at the same time. Not loud. Not sweet or sharp or intense. Just celery — cool-flavored and green-spirited, simmered slowly into something that tasted like the very idea of something wholesome.

"You're very modest," said the sharp cheddar soup in the pot beside her.

"I am exactly as much as I need to be," said Clara gently.

[softly] The cheddar soup did not quite know what to make of this, but it felt vaguely true.

Clara was served to someone who had a cold — the best possible recipient of a light bowl. The person was wrapped in a blanket on the sofa, slightly miserable, and when Clara arrived in her white bowl, the person wrapped both hands around it and looked down at the pale, peaceful surface and the delicate cream swirl, and something in their expression softened.

[pause] She didn't need to be dramatic. She didn't need to be bold. The soft story she told was simple: you are warm now. You are fed. The bowl is gentle. The cream is cool. Your hands are wrapped around something good.

That was all.

But sometimes that is everything.

[softly] The blanket-wrapped person finished the soup slowly, spoonful by quiet spoonful, and by the bottom of the bowl felt measurably better — not from any magic, but from the accumulated warmth and gentleness of a light bowl given with care, which is one of the oldest and most reliable medicines there is.
