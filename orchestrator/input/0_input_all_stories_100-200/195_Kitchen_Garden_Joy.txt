At the back of the house, just past the old porch steps, the kitchen garden grew. [pause] It was not a large garden — a small rectangle of warm soil, three raised beds and a few pots, bordered by a low wooden frame that had been painted green every spring for many years. But what it lacked in size, it made up for in joy.

This was Greta the kitchen garden's view of herself: modest in extent, enormous in usefulness, and full of a backyard world happiness that larger gardens, she suspected, could not always achieve.

Every morning the cook came to visit — first with coffee, just to look, then with scissors for the herbs, then sometimes with a small basket for whatever was ready. [softly] This was Greta's favorite time of day. The growing joy she felt when the cook's hand found a ripe tomato or snipped a perfect stem of basil was different from the joy of sunshine or water. It was the joy of being needed, of completing the circle from seed to kitchen to table.

The tomatoes were particularly proud of their contribution. The zucchini were prolific to the point of excess, which the cook received with patient good humor. The herbs were constant and reliable. The kale persisted through cold and heat alike.

[pause] One morning, a neighbor stopped at the garden gate and looked in.

"It's so small," she said, not unkindly.

"The best gardens are," said the cook. She was kneeling to check on the basil. She looked up and smiled. "I can reach everything from here."

[softly] Greta thought about this. To reach everything. To have everything close enough to touch and tend and harvest without losing anything to distance or neglect. To be exactly the right size for exactly the right life.

The kitchen garden glowed in the morning light, growing joyfully in her small, perfect, beloved patch of warm soil.
