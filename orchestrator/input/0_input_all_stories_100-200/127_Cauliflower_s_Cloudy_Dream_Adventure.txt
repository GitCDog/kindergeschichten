Everyone in the cloud field knew that brave cauliflowers dreamed differently from other vegetables. Their dreams were white and round and billowing, and if you watched a cauliflower sleep, which the other vegetables occasionally did out of curiosity, you could see the florets shift very slightly as the dreams passed through them. [long pause]

Cornelius the cauliflower had been having one particular dream for three nights running.

In the dream he was floating. Not falling — floating, with intention and ease, through a sky that was the same color as himself: a creamy white that held the light differently at different times of day. Below him, the cloud field stretched away in every direction, softened by distance, each plant a small distinct shape in the green below. [sighs]

He wasn't alone in the dream. There were clouds with him — large soft cloud field clouds, the kind that look solid from the ground but feel like cool nothing when you move through them. They didn't mind him. They moved around him and he moved among them and there was no distinction, really, between the brave cauliflower floating in the sky and the sky itself. [beat]

On the fourth morning he woke up and found the cloud field covered in actual fog.

He stood in it for a long moment. It looked exactly like his dream — white, soft, obscuring the edges of things, making the familiar strange and beautiful.

Cornelius walked through the foggy cloud field slowly, the way you walk through a dream you're afraid of waking from. The other vegetables were dim shapes in the whiteness. The paths he knew by heart led in directions he couldn't quite predict. Everything was the same and entirely different. [softly]

"It's like your dream," said a small turnip who had heard about his dream and found it interesting.

"Yes," said Cornelius. "Except I'm awake."

"Is it better or worse awake?"

Cornelius thought about this carefully, as cauliflowers do. The fluffy dream had been wonderful. But the waking fog had a smell, and a coolness on his leaves, and a reality that the dream couldn't quite hold. [with growing realization]

"Different," he said. "Both good."

The fog burned off by mid-morning, and the cloud field returned to its usual green clarity. But Cornelius spent the rest of the day looking up at the sky, where the real clouds drifted past, white and round and unhurried, dreaming their own enormous dreams.

[pause] Good night my little friend
