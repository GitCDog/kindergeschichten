Sam the scarecrow had been standing in the field since early spring, and by now he knew every crow by name. [pause] This was not what his makers had intended. They had put him there to keep the crows away from the seed corn. But Sam had a mild temperament and an open mind, and after the first week he had come to an understanding with the crow community that worked remarkably well for everyone involved.

The deal was simple: the crows could visit Sam whenever they liked, could perch on his outstretched arms and hat brim to rest and gossip and watch the field in the long afternoon, as long as they left the seed rows alone.

The crows agreed. They were pragmatic birds and considered this an excellent arrangement. [softly] Crow chat was, it turned out, one of the finest things the field had to offer. The crows knew everything — where the best berries were ripening, which weather was coming, the news from three fields over. Sam, who couldn't move but could listen, became the best-informed scarecrow in the county.

"There is a hawk over the eastern meadow," reported Cobalt, the senior crow, arriving one Tuesday morning.

"Thank you," said Sam. "I'll keep watch."

[pause] He couldn't actually do anything about a hawk, of course. But Cobalt appreciated the attentiveness. All the crow friends did. They liked that Sam took their field watch reports seriously, that he never dismissed their observations, that he treated their crow intelligence as the genuinely useful thing it was.

By the end of summer, Sam's hat was home to a small bird nest. His arms were the unofficial gathering point for every corvid within a mile.

[softly] And the seed corn grew undisturbed, because the crows had decided that Sam's field was their field too, and they watched over it with the particular fierce loyalty of those who have been treated, unexpectedly, with respect.
