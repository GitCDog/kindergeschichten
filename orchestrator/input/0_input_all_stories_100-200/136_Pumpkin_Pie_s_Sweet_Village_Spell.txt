In the sweet village on the hill above the orchard, where every house smelled of something baking and the cobblestone streets were perpetually warm underfoot, a magic pumpkin pie named Pippa had been cast under the most unusual spell. [long pause]

The spell had arrived on a Tuesday morning in November, delivered by the east wind in the form of a golden powder that settled over the whole sweet village and made everyone in it feel, for exactly one week, perfectly content.

Not happy — content. There is a difference. Happiness is bright and particular. Contentment is wider and quieter, the feeling of not needing anything to be different from how it already is.

Pippa noticed it immediately. [sighs]

She was usually a pie who wanted things. Warmer days, better spices, a crust that held its shape through the full length of the dessert magic. On Tuesday she woke up and found she didn't want anything additional. The morning was adequate. The kitchen was fine. The smell of nutmeg in the sweet village air was enough.

It was restful, she discovered. Not wanting anything extra. [beat]

She spent the first day of the spell simply being in the sweet village — walking the warm cobblestone streets, sitting in the square where the fountain ran all year, watching the other pies and tarts and crumbles going about their usual activities with the same uncharacteristic ease.

By midweek she had a theory about the dessert magic.

Contentment, she thought, wasn't the absence of wanting. It was the presence of appreciation — noticing what was already there, which turned out to be quite a lot. The smell of the sweet village. The warmth of the cobblestones. The way the fountain caught the autumn light. [softly]

On the last day of the spell she stood in the square and looked around at the sweet village she had always lived in, and she thought: I have been here all along and not quite seen it.

The golden powder dissolved on the evening wind. The usual wanting came back, small and manageable. [with growing realization]

But Pippa found she kept the habit of noticing. That was the dessert magic, the part that lasted.

The sweet village was quiet in the evening, the cobblestones still warm, the fountain still running, everything golden in the last of the light.
