In the spring field at the edge of the garden, where the earth had only just woken from its winter sleep, a brave asparagus named Aster was doing something remarkable. [long pause]

He was growing. Not politely or slowly — but with real intention, pushing upward into the cool spring air as if the sky had called him by name.

Every morning the other vegetables in the spring field looked over and found Aster a little taller than the day before. The radishes measured him against the fence post. The early peas, who prided themselves on speed, watched with a mixture of admiration and concern. [sighs]

"How do you do it?" asked a small parsnip, craning her neck.

"I just keep going up," said Aster, which was honest if not terribly detailed.

The truth was something that Aster couldn't easily explain. There was simply something in him that wanted to reach — to discover what the air was like a little higher up, and then higher still. It was a brave asparagus kind of feeling, and he didn't question it. [beat]

By mid-spring he was the tallest thing in the spring field by a wide margin. He swayed gently in the breeze, which felt different up high — warmer, freer, carrying traces of things from far away.

From up there he could see past the garden walls. He could see the meadow beyond, where the grasses were just beginning to show their first green. He could see a farmhouse chimney trailing a soft thread of smoke. He could see, on clear evenings, the hills going blue in the distance. [softly]

One afternoon a small robin landed on him, looked around from the new vantage, and said: "Oh. It's quite good from up here."

"It is," Aster agreed.

The robin stayed for a while. They looked out over the spring field together — the brave asparagus and the small bird — while below them the other vegetables went quietly about their growing, each in their own direction, each reaching toward whatever thing called to them. [with growing realization]

This, Aster thought, was what a tall tale really was. Not a story that stretched the truth, but a truth that stretched upward, toward more sky, toward more light.

He kept reaching. The spring field was golden in the fading afternoon, and everything in it was exactly where it needed to be. [softly]
