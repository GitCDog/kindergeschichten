Most vegetables knew what was above the soil. Rosie the radish root knew what was below it. [pause] She had spent her whole life going deeper — while the other garden plants grew upward toward the light, Rosie dove downward into the rich, dark, secret world of the deep soil.

What she found there would have surprised anyone who had never thought to look.

There were long, golden threads of fungal networks connecting root to root like an underground telegraph. There were air pockets no bigger than a marble where tiny creatures had made perfectly round homes. And far below everything else, where the soil changed from brown to a deep clay red, there was the hidden cave.

Rosie had found it by accident, pushing through a particularly crumbly layer of earth one spring morning. One moment she was in soil, the next she was in a small, perfectly round space — an air pocket large enough for her, cool and dim and smelling of minerals and ancient rain. [softly] A hidden cave, belonging to no one and waiting for someone.

She told no one about it. It was hers.

She visited on quiet afternoons, sliding through the narrow earth path she had kept clear, settling into the cool rounded space and resting in the silence.

[pause] Silence underground was different from silence above. Above ground, quiet was the absence of something. Down here, quiet was its own presence — a full, cool, mineral-smelling completeness, like a room that had been waiting and was now finally occupied.

Sometimes earthworms passed near the cave, their slow rhythmic movement sending small vibrations through the clay walls. Rosie liked this. It felt like company without interruption.

[softly] She was a radish root in a hidden cave deep below the garden, and this was, she had decided, one of the very best places to be. Still and cool and full of the quiet earth's long, patient dreaming, which was very much like her own.
