The garden gnome was three inches tall and had been standing beside the rose bush for eleven years, and not one person in the family had ever noticed that he moved. [pause] But he did. At night, when the garden was dark and quiet and the fireflies were the only light, the brave garden gnome named Gerald got to work.

His secret help was what the garden had always needed — the kind of help that happened invisibly, leaving only results and no explanations.

He found the snail that had been eating the lettuce and politely redirected it toward the compost pile, which had better things to offer. He staked the tomato plant that was leaning dangerously toward the path. He cleared the blocked drainage hole in the pot by the wall that had been causing the lavender to struggle. He found the hidden tools the family had been looking for since May — the small trowel and the dibber — tucked under the rosemary, and arranged them neatly where they would be seen in the morning. [softly] Not too neatly. Gnome work should look like luck.

The family discovered their trowel on a Wednesday morning and were quietly delighted. The lavender recovered the following week and the grandmother declared it had "turned a corner." The tomatoes stood straight and proud in the summer light. Nobody understood quite why the garden was doing so well.

[pause] Gerald listened to their pleasant confusion from his spot by the rose bush and felt the warm satisfaction of secret help successfully given. This was the best kind of help, he had always believed — the kind that solved problems without requiring gratitude, that improved things without anyone having to feel foolish about having needed the help in the first place.

[softly] He settled back into his usual position as the morning came, his small painted face turned toward the garden he had served for eleven invisible, dedicated, enormously useful years.
