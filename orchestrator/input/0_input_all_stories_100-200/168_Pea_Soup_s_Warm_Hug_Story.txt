On the coldest evening of the year, a pot of pea soup named Peri was doing what pea soup does best — being warm. [pause] Not just warm in temperature, though she was certainly that, bubbling gently on the back burner and sending steady ribbons of steam curling toward the low kitchen ceiling. She was warm in the deeper way — the way that food can be warm when it knows exactly what it's for.

She was for hugging. From the inside.

The family that lived in this kitchen had been out all day in the cold. Peri had been listening to the sound of wind against the windows and had been quietly, steadily, preparing herself. More warmth. More depth. Another pinch of mint from the jar by the stove. The green swirl of herself growing richer and more settled as the cold afternoon deepened into evening.

When the door opened and the cold came rushing in with the family, Peri gave a small, satisfied bubble. [softly] Here they were. Here she was.

The soup bowls came down from the shelf. The bread was sliced and buttered. The table was set. One by one, the family sat down, still carrying the cold in the folds of their coats and the redness of their cheeks.

[pause] When the first bowl was filled and placed before the smallest child, something happened that Peri loved above almost everything else. The child put both hands around the bowl and felt the warmth travel up through their palms, and their shoulders dropped, and their face softened, and they breathed out.

One by one around the table, the same thing happened. Cold became warmth. Tired became quiet. Separate became together.

[softly] Peri swirled gently in the pot, still warm, still ready, the green of a spring garden and the warmth of a winter kitchen all at once, giving her warm hug to everyone who needed it.
