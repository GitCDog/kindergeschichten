The harvest day began before the sun was fully up. [pause] Finn was a little farmer kid with muddy boots and a serious expression, and he had been waiting for harvest day all summer. He had planted the seeds in spring. He had watered them through the dry July weeks. He had staked the tomatoes and tied the beans and watched everything grow from small and uncertain into tall and abundant.

And now, finally, it was time.

He started at the far end of the veggie patch with his small basket, moving along the rows methodically the way his grandmother had shown him. Feel for the weight, she had said. A ripe vegetable tells you when it's ready. You can feel the rightness of it in your hand.

[softly] The first tomato came off the vine with a soft, clean snap. It was warm from the sun and smelled exactly like summer. Finn held it in his palm and felt the weight of all those watered mornings in it.

He moved through the veggie patch in the growing light. Zucchini, fat and green. Green beans, perfect in their pods. Cucumbers, cool even in the warm morning. Peppers in red and yellow and green. The harvest basket grew heavy in his arm.

[pause] His grandmother was on the porch when he came back, and she looked at his basket and smiled the way she smiled when something was exactly right.

"Good harvest," she said.

"Everything was ready," said Finn.

She took the basket and began sorting the vegetables on the kitchen counter, and Finn stood beside her and watched, feeling something he couldn't quite name — the particular feeling of having grown something from the beginning to the end, of having been responsible for something living, of having kept that promise.

[softly] The kitchen smelled of fresh vegetables and sun-warm earth, and outside the window the veggie patch waited quietly to do it all again.
