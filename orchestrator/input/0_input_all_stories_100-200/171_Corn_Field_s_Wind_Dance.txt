On the evenings when the wind came through the corn field, something extraordinary happened. [pause] All the tall corn stalks began to dance.

It wasn't the kind of dancing you could plan or practice. It was something that happened when the wind found the field and ran through it from one side to the other in a great, sweeping rush. Every stalk leaned and swayed, their silken tassels streaming, their long leaves rippling in golden-green waves.

The crow friends sat in the trees at the edge of the field and watched.

"It's starting again," said a crow named Cobalt.

"It always makes me want to fly," said his companion, a large crow named Slate, who looked very dignified pretending he didn't feel this way.

In the field, the corn stalks moved in the long, rolling patterns that the wind liked best — a wave from the west side, answering waves rippling east, cross-currents of movement in the tall rows that made the whole field seem alive in a single, breathing way. [softly] The wind dance had rhythms that changed with each gust, never exactly the same twice, always surprising, always harmonious.

Cobalt hopped from foot to foot. Then he dropped from the branch and swooped low over the field, letting the wind carry him in a long arc over the waving stalks.

[pause] Slate watched for exactly four seconds before following.

They rode the wind above the corn field in great loops and dips, the wind dance below them and around them and through them all at once. The stalks swayed. The crows called out in sharp, delighted voices. The sun set orange and vast behind the field.

When the wind finally stilled and the dance ended, the field went quiet again. The stalks stood straight and tall in the calm evening air.

[softly] But somewhere in each stalk, a memory of the wind dance lingered — a warm, swaying, beautiful thing that had moved through them and was still, in some small way, moving.
