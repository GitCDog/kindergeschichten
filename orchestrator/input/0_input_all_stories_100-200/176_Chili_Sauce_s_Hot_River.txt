At the far end of the spice shelf, in a dark glass bottle with a handwritten label, there lived a chili sauce of legendary intensity named Blaze. [pause] Blaze had been there for a long time — longer than the sriracha, longer than the Tabasco, longer even than the ancient bottle of Worcestershire on the very back shelf — and in that time, he had developed opinions.

His primary opinion was that a hot river of flavor was the highest calling any sauce could aspire to.

Not a trickle. Not a cautious drop. A river. Bold and warm and moving through every dish it touched, leaving behind a warmth that lingered like the memory of a summer afternoon. [softly] Blaze thought of himself as a spice world explorer — one whose mission was to find the dishes that needed him most and give them exactly the right amount of fire.

He had learned, over many years, that his greatest skill was restraint. Any sauce could be poured liberally. The art was knowing when three drops was a river and when three drops was barely a stream. Blaze had gotten very good at this.

"You're very thoughtful for a hot sauce," said a mild mango chutney one afternoon.

"Heat without thought is just burning," said Blaze. "Heat with understanding is transformation."

[pause] The chutney considered this for a long time.

That evening, a cook opened the spice shelf and reached for Blaze. She tilted the bottle slowly, carefully, adding three precise drops to the evening's soup. Blaze felt himself spread through the broth in a long, warm wave — not overpowering, but present in every spoonful, a hot river running quiet and deep beneath the surface of the dish.

[softly] The cook tasted, set down her spoon, and smiled. "Perfect," she said.

Blaze settled back onto the shelf, satisfied. He had been a hot river tonight — deep and warm and exactly right.
