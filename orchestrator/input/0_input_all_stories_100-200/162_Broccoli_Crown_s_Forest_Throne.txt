Deep in the vegetable garden, in the oldest and most magnificent corner, stood a broccoli plant of extraordinary grandeur — and perched at its very top, surveying all he could see, was a proud broccoli crown named Rex. [pause] Rex had always felt, in a quiet but unshakeable way, that he was meant to be the forest king of the garden.

His green throne was the highest point in the vegetable row, and from it he could see the whole territory — the carrot fields to the east, the tomato kingdom to the west, the great bean forest stretching northward, and to the south, the mysterious herb patch, always fragrant and secretive.

Every morning, Rex held court. [softly] The garden beetles came to report on the state of the soil. The ladybugs presented their findings on aphid populations. A particularly officious earwig arrived each Tuesday to update Rex on comings and goings near the compost heap.

Rex listened to all of it very seriously. He had opinions. He issued rulings: that the aphids should be escorted gently away from the bean forest, that the ants deserved a thank-you for their work aerating the soil near the root row, that the old fence post on the north side needed a vine to stabilize it.

[pause] "Does he actually run anything?" a young garden spider asked the ladybug one morning.

The ladybug thought carefully. "He listens," she said at last. "He pays attention. He remembers. And somehow, when Rex has heard a problem, the problem tends to sort itself out."

The spider watched Rex on his green throne, looking out at the garden with calm, deep attention.

"I think I understand," said the spider.

[softly] Rex didn't command. He witnessed. He cared. He made the creatures of the garden feel that what they did mattered — because the forest king was watching, was listening, and was quietly, steadily, enormously proud of every living thing in his view.

The evening light turned his crown deep emerald, and the whole garden seemed to breathe easier beneath his watch.
