The salad bowl was a democracy, and everyone knew it. [pause] Unlike a soup, where things dissolved into each other, or a stew, where the strongest flavors won, the salad bowl gave every ingredient an equal voice and asked only that they toss well together and be their best individual selves.

Sam the salad bowl had been hosting toss parties for as long as she could remember. The guests changed, but the format remained: all the mixed friends went in together, the dressing was poured, and then the great joyful toss of combining happened — leaves and vegetables and croutons tumbling in slow arcs, the dressing reaching every corner, everything landing back in a beautiful, rearranged heap.

"Ready?" called Sam.

"Ready!" called the romaine, the arugula, the cherry tomatoes, the cucumber slices, the kalamata olives, the thin-shaved parmesan, and the croutons — who were new to toss parties and slightly anxious about the tumbling. [softly] They needn't have worried. The croutons, as it turned out, were naturals.

The dressing came — a simple vinaigrette that moved through the bowl like a breeze, touching everything lightly and making everything it touched shine.

Then: the toss.

[pause] The great fun toss was always Sam's favorite moment. Leaves lifted and turned, revealing their pale undersides. Tomatoes rolled. Olives gleamed. The croutons — brave and golden — sailed in short arcs and landed with satisfying small plunks. The parmesan settled like snow over everything.

When it was over, every ingredient had been rearranged but was still perfectly itself.

The cook brought the bowl to the table, and the family served themselves. Each plate was different — different proportions, different bits on top — because that was the nature of a toss party.

[softly] Everyone got a version of the same beautiful bowl, and every version was exactly right.
