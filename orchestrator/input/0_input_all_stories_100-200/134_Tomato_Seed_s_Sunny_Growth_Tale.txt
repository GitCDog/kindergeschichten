The growing patch was quiet in the early morning, and a little tomato seed named Tommy lay in the dark soil waiting for what came next. [long pause]

He had been there for six days. He knew it was six days because he had been counting, in the way seeds count: by water events, by shifts in temperature, by the gradual change in the smell of the soil as it warmed and cooled and warmed again.

He was, he knew, on the edge of something.

Seeds know this feeling. It comes just before the beginning — a sort of internal readiness, a tension in the coat of the seed that means the growing is about to start and there is nothing now to do but begin. [sighs]

On the seventh morning, Tommy unfurled.

It was not dramatic, as beginnings go. It was quiet and effortful and felt from the inside like a long slow exhale after holding a breath you'd been holding your whole life. His root went down — confidently, because roots always know which way down is — and then something else went up, reaching toward the warmth that came from above through the soil. [beat]

The first sun drop found him on his eighth day, when his first pale shoot broke the surface of the growing patch and felt actual light for the first time.

He stopped. Just for a moment.

The light was everything the soil's warmth had hinted at. It was direct and wide and arrived from all directions at once, which was not how he'd imagined it. It was warm in a different way from soil-warm — not enclosed and surrounding, but open and generous, offered freely, requiring nothing in return. [softly]

Tommy turned toward it, the way all his kind have always turned toward it, because some things don't need to be learned.

Over the following days the growing patch filled in around him — other seedlings breaking their surfaces, reaching their own sun drops, unfolding their first leaves into the light. A whole community of beginning things, each starting their own sunny growth tale in the same quiet patch of earth. [with growing realization]

Tommy grew. Not fast, and not without effort, but steadily, in the honest way of growing things that have the sun and the soil and the patience to become exactly what they're going to be.

The growing patch hummed with it, all morning and all afternoon.
