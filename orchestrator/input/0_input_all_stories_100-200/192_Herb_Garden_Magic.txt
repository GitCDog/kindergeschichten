On the kitchen windowsill, six small pots stood in a row, each one holding something different, each one contributing to a kind of magic that was completely invisible until the cooking began. [pause] This was the herb garden, and Harriet — a small, lush basil plant who considered herself the leader — presided over it with quiet authority.

Beside her stood rosemary, ancient and woody and smelling of a hillside in some distant warm country. Then thyme, low and serious. Then mint, who never quite followed the rules and had already escaped her pot twice. Then chives, bright and tidy. And at the end, parsley, abundantly leafy and always very calm.

Together they were the mini pots — the six green magicians who turned the food in the kitchen from ordinary to something else entirely.

"We had a good week," said rosemary one evening, as the kitchen settled into quiet. The cook had used all of them at least once, which they regarded as success. [softly] Harriet had gone into the tomato sauce on Tuesday. Thyme had been tucked inside the roasted chicken on Thursday. Mint had given herself to a yogurt sauce that everyone had declared transformative. Chives had topped the cream soup. Parsley had finished the fish. Rosemary had been woven into the focaccia, which the whole family had eaten in an afternoon.

[pause] "What I love," said parsley thoughtfully, "is that they never see us coming."

"What do you mean?" asked thyme.

"I mean that they smell us, and something in them wakes up before they've even tasted. The green magic happens first in the nose." [softly] This was true. Every cook knew that herbs spoke first in smell, and the smell alone could change a mood, could make a kitchen feel like warmth itself, could make an ordinary Tuesday evening feel like something a little more beautiful.

Harriet looked down the row of her mini pots — all six of them resting now in the evening light, having given their green magic generously to another week.
