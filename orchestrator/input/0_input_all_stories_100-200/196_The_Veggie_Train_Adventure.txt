The veggie train left the market station at nine in the morning, right on schedule. [pause] Vinny the veggie train was proud of this. Punctuality was one of his finest qualities. He ran on food tracks laid through the kitchen district, and his passengers — a varied and colorful collection of market vegetables — filled every car to the comfortable brim.

In the first car: cabbages, round and tightly packed, who took up more room than they intended and apologized repeatedly.

In the second: a family of root vegetables, all earthy and sensible, who had brought sandwiches for the journey.

In the third: the leafy greens, who required a slightly larger car due to their tendency to unfold dramatically.

And in the caboose, as always: one extremely large squash who had been given the last car specifically so that everyone else could travel comfortably. [softly] The squash did not mind. The caboose had the best views.

Vinny's travel fun was the joy of connection — of taking ingredients from one place to another, making the journey possible, being the thread that linked the farm to the kitchen to the table. Without him, things stayed where they were. With him, everything moved.

[pause] The food tracks curved through the morning market, past the cheese stall and the flower stand and the old lady who sold honey in small jars, and out the other side into the quiet residential streets where kitchens waited.

At each stop, passengers disembarked with purpose. The cabbages rolled magnificently toward the soup pot. The root vegetables marched in organized columns toward the roasting pan. The greens swept dramatically toward the salad bowl.

[softly] When Vinny pulled into the final station, all cars empty, tracks quiet, the morning's work done, he felt the particular deep satisfaction of something that has served its purpose completely.

He sat in the warm kitchen light and waited for tomorrow's market run.
