In the smallest pot on the windowsill, a minty herb leaf named Henny had discovered that she had a kind of magic — one that was entirely invisible until you got close. [pause] Her magic was her scent: cool and bright and fresh as morning air, with just a hint of sweetness underneath, like a cool breeze carrying the memory of rain.

She had first noticed it when a butterfly had stopped on her windowsill pot and hovered for a long moment, its wings fanning slowly, before landing gently on her leaf.

"You smell like the wild meadow," the butterfly said with something like wonder.

Henny hadn't known what to say. She had never been to a wild meadow. She had spent her whole life in this small pot, beside a kitchen window, being watered and sunned and occasionally snipped for tea.

But the butterfly's words stayed with her. [softly] She began to pay attention to the effect her minty breeze had on the creatures who passed her way. The bee who paused on the screen door and seemed to sniff the air with pleasure. The cat who pressed her nose against the pot in the evening and purred. The child who touched her leaves and then held her small hand to her face and breathed in slowly, going very still.

Fresh magic, Henny decided, was the best kind. [pause] It wasn't loud. It didn't flash or sparkle or make dramatic statements. It simply arrived on the air and made whoever breathed it feel, for just a moment, clean and calm and beautifully awake.

One evening, the cook plucked several of her leaves and made a mint tea, and Henny watched from her pot as the cup steamed and the whole kitchen filled with her scent. The cook sat down, wrapped both hands around the cup, and breathed in.

"There," the cook said softly, to no one in particular.

[softly] Henny understood. That one small word was enough. In one warm, steaming cup, her fresh magic had made someone feel exactly right — exactly calm, exactly comforted, exactly at home.
