Every morning on the quiet farm, before the roosters crowed and before the dew had dried from the grass, there came a sound that no one else seemed to notice — a soft, low, gentle tune drifting from the leek row. [pause] It was Louie the leek sprout, singing.

Louie had been singing since his very first morning above the soil. He didn't know where the farm song came from. It simply arrived with the dawn, rising up through his roots from the deep, quiet earth and out through his long, pale-green leaves in a low, steady hum.

The other vegetables had noticed, though none of them said anything at first. The carrots would lean slightly toward the sound. The onions would sway. Even the potatoes, deep in the earth, seemed to dream more peacefully on the mornings when Louie sang.

[softly] One morning, a small robin landed on the fence post beside the leek row and tilted her red-orange head.

"That is a beautiful quiet tune," she said.

"Thank you," said Louie, a little surprised. "I didn't know anyone could hear it."

"I have been hearing it every morning for three weeks," said the robin. "It sounds like what the ground would sing if the ground could sing."

Louie considered this. [pause] He had never thought of it that way. He had thought of the farm song as something small and private, something that just happened to him. But if the robin could hear it, maybe it wasn't as small as he had thought.

That morning, he let the song grow just a little louder. Not loud enough to startle anyone. Just loud enough for the whole leek row to hear clearly, and for the carrots to sway, and for the robins to gather along the fence in a quiet, attentive row.

The farm was full of sounds — wind and birds and tractors and rain. But in the early morning, in the long quiet moment before the day began, there was the leek song, low and steady and full of something ancient and warm.

[softly] Louie sang on, note by note, morning by morning, adding his quiet tune to the great, slow music of the living farm.
