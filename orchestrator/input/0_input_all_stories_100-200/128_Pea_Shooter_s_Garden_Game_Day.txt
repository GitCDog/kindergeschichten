The garden game had been planned for weeks, and the morning it finally arrived, the pea shooter named Pete was up before sunrise polishing his aim. [long pause]

The target fun had been set up along the back fence: tin cans arranged at different heights, a paper bull's-eye nailed to the old post, and a row of softly stuffed scarecrow shapes that the garden's residents had assembled with varying levels of enthusiasm and craft ability. Some were better than others.

Pete surveyed the course with professional attention.

He was a silly pea shooter by reputation — his best shots had names, his misses had elaborate explanations, and he kept a careful mental record of all his best moments for future retelling — but when it came to the actual garden game, he was serious. He had prepared. He was ready. [sighs]

The rules were simple. Three rounds: distance, accuracy, and target fun, which involved hitting the moving targets that Benny the green bean had agreed to swing on strings from the fence posts. Benny had accepted this role with good humor and requested a helmet, which had been fashioned from half a walnut shell.

The distance round went well. Pete's first pea arced cleanly in the morning air and struck the farthest can with a satisfying ping. The garden cheered. [beat]

The accuracy round went extremely well. He put three peas through the center ring of the bull's-eye in succession, which had not been done before, and stood back with his hands on his hips and tried to look unsurprised.

The target fun round was chaos, and Pete loved it.

Benny swung the targets in unpredictable arcs. They spun. They bobbed. One of them came loose and floated away over the leek patch, which was not planned but drew the biggest cheer of the morning. Pete ran after it, still shooting, laughing at the sheer silliness of it, his aim now wild and joyful and completely unbothered by accuracy. [softly]

He came last in the target fun round by a wide margin.

He didn't mind. [with growing realization]

Some rounds you win carefully, and some rounds you lose delightfully, and the garden game day had given him both in one morning, which was more than enough.

The garden settled into the warm afternoon, full of the particular happiness that comes from having played hard and laughed freely.
