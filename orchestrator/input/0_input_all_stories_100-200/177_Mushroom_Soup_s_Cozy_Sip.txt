The forest bowl of mushroom soup was the kind of thing that made cold evenings feel like a gift. [pause] Mira the mushroom soup had been simmering since midday — first the dried mushrooms soaking in warm water, then the fresh ones sautéed in butter with a murmur of thyme and cream added last, turning everything golden-brown and impossibly fragrant.

She smelled of the forest floor in the best possible way — not the wet or the cold of it, but the deep, woodsy, settled warmth of autumn earth and old trees and something alive and mysterious moving quietly beneath the leaves.

The evening was grey and still outside the kitchen window. [softly] Inside, every surface was warm. The steam from Mira's bowl rose in slow spirals. The light above the table was low and amber. The spoon was silver and heavy and clicked pleasantly against the ceramic bowl when it rested.

The person who received the bowl of soup wrapped both hands around it for a long moment before picking up the spoon. This was, Mira felt, exactly right. You should feel soup before you eat it. Soup speaks in warmth.

[pause] The first sip was careful. The next was slower. By the third, the person was quiet in a different way — not tensely quiet, but restfully quiet, the kind of quiet that comes when something warm has reached the center of you and settled there.

Mira had been many things before this moment — spores and fungus and forest floor and rain — and all of that was still in her, deeply and invisibly. But right now she was most usefully a cozy sip, a forest bowl warming a quiet person on a grey evening, which was one of the very best things she had ever been.

[softly] The spoon clinked gently at the bottom of the empty bowl, and the kitchen was warm, and the grey outside the window did not feel grey at all anymore.
