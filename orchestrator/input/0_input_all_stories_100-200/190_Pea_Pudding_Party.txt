The green dessert had arrived, and nobody quite knew what to make of it. [pause] Percy the pea pudding sat in his individual glass cups — vivid green, slightly wobbly, topped with a precise swirl of lemon cream — and he had the composed look of a pudding who knew he was unusual and was entirely comfortable with it.

Most puddings were chocolate. Or vanilla. Or caramel. Percy was pea — bright, playful pea, sweetened and blended into a silky smooth pudding that tasted, improbably, like a garden in spring condensed into one delicate, green, wobbly bite.

"You're very green," said the chocolate mousse beside him.

"I am," said Percy happily. "That is my best quality."

The fun spoon arrived for the youngest child at the party, who had been carefully skeptical of every green food since a broccoli incident the previous spring. [softly] She stared at Percy. Percy wobbled gently, which in pudding language means nothing threatening at all.

"It's green," she said.

"It is," said her father. "Try it."

She touched the surface with her spoon — felt the slight resistance and then the give, the way good pudding should behave. She brought a small spoonful to her lips and closed her eyes.

[pause] What happened next was described by her family for years afterward. Her eyes opened very wide. Then wider. Then she said, in a tone of absolute astonishment: "It tastes like CANDY PEAS."

Percy didn't know the precise meaning of candy peas, but the tone was unmistakably delighted, and that was enough.

[softly] The pea pudding party was a great success. Percy was eaten with enthusiastic fun spoons by everyone at the table, each one discovering the same happy astonishment — that something so unusually green could be so unusually wonderful.
