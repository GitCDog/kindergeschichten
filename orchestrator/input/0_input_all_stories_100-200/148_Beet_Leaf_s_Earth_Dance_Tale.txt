Every evening when the garden went quiet, something magical happened in the beet patch. [pause] A beet leaf named Beatrix would uncurl from her daytime stillness and begin to dance.

She couldn't explain it. It wasn't something she decided to do. It was something the earth did through her — a slow, swaying, red glow kind of dance that started in her roots and moved all the way to the highest tip of her stem.

The other beet leaves had noticed. They watched her quietly from the shadows, their own roots tingling in sympathy.

"What is she doing?" whispered a young beet leaf named Ned.

"She is doing the earth dance," said an old, wide leaf named Gran. "It is the oldest kind of magic. The earth remembers every root that has ever grown in it. When it is happy, it dances through us."

Ned watched Beatrix sway. Her color deepened in the dusk from green-red to a rich, dark crimson. Around her feet — if leaves could be said to have feet — the soil itself seemed to breathe and pulse with a soft, warm light. [softly] The whole beet patch smelled of rain and clay and something older and sweeter than either.

"Can I learn it?" Ned asked.

"You don't learn it," said Gran gently. "You listen for it."

Ned closed his eyes. He felt the cool soil around his roots, the memory of summer rain, the deep dark warmth that had been building since spring. [pause] And then — he felt it. A gentle pull, a hum in his veins, a rhythm too slow to count but too real to ignore.

He began to sway, just a little. Just enough.

Beatrix opened her eyes and smiled when she saw him. One by one, the other beet leaves began to feel it too, until the whole patch was moving in a slow, red-glowing earth dance under the starry sky.

No one in the garden ever saw it happen. But sometimes, when the wind was still, you could hear a soft humming rising from the beet patch, as if the earth itself were singing the oldest lullaby it knew.
