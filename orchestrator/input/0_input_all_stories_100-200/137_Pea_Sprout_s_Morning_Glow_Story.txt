The morning glow came first as color — a pale gold at the edge of the garden wall — and then as warmth, slow and even, arriving across the dew-covered garden like a tide coming in. [long pause]

A tiny pea sprout named Pearl had been waiting for it since before dawn.

She was new to mornings, being only eleven days old, and every morning so far had been different enough to be worth watching from the start. She had positioned herself at the edge of the garden bed where nothing taller grew in front of her, so the morning glow would find her without obstruction, and she had waited with the patient attention of someone learning something important. [sighs]

The dew drops were still on everything when the first light came.

The garden lit up one section at a time, the way a room lights up when someone carries a candle through it — sequentially, each part waiting its turn. The far wall first. Then the apple tree. Then the rows of taller vegetables. Then, finally, the garden bed where Pearl stood with her two new leaves spread wide, and the morning glow arrived. [beat]

The dew drops on her leaves caught it and held it.

Each one — and there were many, Pearl had counted them — became a small perfect light, round and bright, and for a moment she was covered in them, a tiny pea sprout wearing all the morning at once.

Then the warmth increased, and the dew drops began to shrink, and Pearl watched each one go in turn, the morning glow continuing even after they had gone because it had never been in the dew drops really — they had just been borrowing it, briefly, the way new things borrow the beauty of old things while they are still learning to make their own. [softly]

She thought about this.

Being new, she decided, meant you got to see everything as it was, without habit making it invisible. The morning glow was morning glow every morning, the same physics, the same angle of light — but for Pearl it was still remarkable because she hadn't yet had the chance to take it for granted. [with growing realization]

She intended to keep not taking it for granted for as long as she possibly could.

The garden was fully awake now, warm and bright, and Pearl was part of it, tiny and steady and turning her two new leaves toward the light.

[pause] Good night my little friend
