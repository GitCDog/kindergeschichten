Once upon a time, deep beneath a hillside where the roots of old trees reached down like fingers into the dark, there lived a brave badger named Bram. [long pause]
Bram was a digger — all badgers are — but Bram was the finest digger in the whole hillside. His tunnels were straight and true, with smooth walls and good airflow and the occasional comfortable widening where you could sit and think. He was proud of his tunnels the way a carpenter is proud of a well-made chair: quietly, and with good reason. [sighs]
But there was one tunnel Bram had not yet dug. The underground tunnel that led, according to the oldest badger he knew, all the way to the star crystals. [beat]
Star crystals were said to grow in a deep cave at the end of a very long tunnel — pure white stones that caught light from somewhere no one could explain and held it inside, glowing softly in the dark. The oldest badger had seen them once, as a young cub, and never forgotten.
"How far?" Bram asked.
"Very far," said the oldest badger. "Three nights of digging, at least. Maybe four." [softly]
Bram started on a Monday.
He dug through clay, through chalk, through a layer of compressed sand that took half a night by itself. He shored up the tunnel walls with flat stones as he went. He took breaks only when his paws demanded it. [pacing] On the third night he broke through into a space he hadn't made — a natural cave, rough-walled and high and very, very still.
He held up his small lantern.
[with growing realization] The star crystals were real.
They covered one entire wall — white and clear and many-faceted, each one holding a light that seemed to come from nowhere and everywhere. The cave glowed like a winter sky just before dawn. Bram stood in the middle of it with his lantern hanging forgotten at his side, breathing slowly.
He stayed until his lantern burned low. Then he dug a careful notch in the tunnel wall to mark the way. [softly]
He went home and slept for fourteen hours. And then he brought the oldest badger back, who stood in the glowing cave and was, for a long time, unable to speak.
Some things are worth every inch of the digging.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
