Uma was a brave umbrella — midnight blue with a curved wooden handle and a canopy that, when opened, was wide enough to shelter two people and a dog comfortably. She had been in many rain storms, but today's was different. Today the wind was involved.

Uma had been warned about wind fights before. Every umbrella knew: rain is manageable. Wind is the real opponent.

It began gently enough — a spirited gust that made Uma's canopy strain upward and pulled at her spokes. She held firm. Then a second gust, harder, from a different direction. Uma pivoted on her handle, redirected, and kept her charge dry.

Her charge today was a small elderly gentleman named Mr. Penn, who walked with a cane and was returning from the post office with a parcel under one arm. Mr. Penn was entirely unaware that Uma was conducting a battle above his head. He simply walked and let her do her work.

The wind came a third time, fierce and sideways. Uma felt two of her outer spokes flex dangerously. She bent into the gust — not away from it — leaning the way experienced umbrellas know to lean, accepting the pressure instead of fighting it head-on.

The rain came in sheets, hammering her canopy, but Uma's fabric was good and her seams held.

Mr. Penn turned a corner and the wind changed direction again. This was the hardest maneuver: the crosswind on a corner, where a lesser umbrella would have turned inside out and surrendered.

Uma dipped and swiveled and weathered the storm battle one more time.

And then — as storms often do — the wind abruptly stopped. The rain slowed to a scatter, then a drizzle, then a mist. Mr. Penn paused outside his gate and looked up.

"Good old brolly," he said quietly, and gave Uma's handle an appreciative pat.

Uma dripped. Her canopy was slightly asymmetrical from the exertion, one spoke a little bent. But she had kept Mr. Penn dry. Not a single drop had reached him.

She closed with a snap, having won exactly what she had come to win.
