In the soapy world of the kitchen sink, a tiny bubble named Pearl was born one ordinary Tuesday. She was perfectly round, with walls thinner than a single thought, and inside her was a small pocket of warm air that felt like a sigh.

Most bubbles live for just a moment — a pop and they're gone. But Pearl was determined to see the world before that happened. She floated up from the soapy water and rose toward the open window.

The kitchen was enormous from her point of view. The ceiling was as high as a sky. The window was a glowing rectangle of light, and beyond it — the outside world.

Pearl floated out into the garden on a gentle current of air. Everything was enormous and astonishing. A blade of grass was taller than a tree. A raindrop left over on a leaf was as large as a swimming pool. A butterfly passed so close that Pearl could see every tiny detail of its orange wings.

She drifted over a flower bed, catching the scent of lavender on the air inside her. A bumblebee flew alongside her for a moment, looked at her with his large compound eyes, then buzzed away about his business.

Higher she floated, past the garden wall, past the bird feeder, into the air above the street. From up here, the houses looked like toys. Children were playing on a patch of grass below, and one of them pointed up at Pearl and shouted, "Bubble!"

Pearl shimmered. The afternoon sun hit her walls and scattered into a tiny rainbow — red, orange, yellow, green, blue, violet — a full arc of colour inside a thing no bigger than a marble.

She felt the air inside her shift. She knew her journey was nearly done. But she had floated farther than any kitchen bubble had a right to float, and she had seen more than she had ever hoped to see.

With a last, gentle [pop], Pearl returned to the air.

And it was not an ending at all, not really. Every bit of Pearl joined the air that was already there, and the air moved through the garden and through the window and into the room where a child breathed it in without knowing. Pearl became part of the next breath, part of a sigh, part of the quiet air of a summer evening. She was gone and everywhere, all at once.
