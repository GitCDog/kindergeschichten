On the mantelpiece of the old library, between a brass inkwell and a stack of maps, stood a magic hourglass named Hara. Hara was taller than most hourglasses and filled with sand the color of sunset — not white or gold, but a deep amber that caught the light and held it.

Every hour, someone came to turn her.

This was the rule of the library: the hourglass must be turned on the hour. Who did the turning varied — the librarian, a regular visitor, once a very precise child of about seven who had taken the task completely seriously and turned Hara with both hands and great ceremony.

When Hara was turned, the sand fall began.

Hara loved the sand fall. She loved the way the sand didn't all rush at once — it trickled, one grain at a time, finding its way through the narrow waist between the two chambers. Each grain was small and independent, and yet together they made time visible in a way that a clock face never could.

A clock showed the abstract. Hara showed the actual substance of it.

One afternoon, a child sat in the reading chair nearest the mantelpiece and watched Hara for a long time. She said nothing. She simply watched the sand fall and thought.

Eventually she said to the librarian, "If you never turned it, would the sand stay at the bottom forever?"

"It would," said the librarian.

"So someone has to keep choosing to start time over," the child said.

The librarian looked at Hara for a long moment. "I suppose that's right."

Hara shimmered with something that felt, if a hourglass could feel, like being deeply understood.

The sand fall continued. It always did. Grain by grain, the amber grains piled on the lower chamber in a soft cone. Time magic was not in the sand — it was in the choosing. In the act of lifting and turning and beginning again.

Every hour, someone came.

And every hour, Hara began again.


The child came back the next day and turned Hara again herself. The librarian watched from across the room and said nothing. Some lessons are learned best without words — and the lesson of choosing to begin again was one of them.
