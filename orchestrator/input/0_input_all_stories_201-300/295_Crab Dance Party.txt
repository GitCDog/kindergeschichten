When the tide went out and the rock pools were revealed along the base of the headland, the rock pool community came briefly to life in a way that the high-tide world never quite managed.

Among the most enthusiastic participants was a playful crab named Clip.

Clip was a shore crab — dark greenish-brown, with a carapace the size of a large coin and claws that were more expressive than threatening. He lived in the pool between the big flat rock and the cluster of mussels, and he was the undisputed organizer of what had come to be known, in the loose mythology of the rock pool, as the beach fun assembly.

The side step was Clip's signature move.

All crabs moved sideways — this was simply the mechanics of crab locomotion — but Clip had elevated it into something more than functional. He would extend one claw forward and perform his sideways step with a flourish, a distinct pause, and then another step, that gave the impression of choreography.

The other rock pool residents had their own contributions.

A sea anemone pulsed its tentacles in a rhythm that no one had definitively called intentional but that was nonetheless consistent. A small blenny fish darted from shadow to shadow with a speed that might have been anxiety or might have been enthusiasm. Several periwinkles moved in slow deliberate spirals that completed approximately one circle per tide.

It was, Clip would concede, a loosely organized party.

But the best parties were.

When the tide returned and the pools were swallowed back into the sea, the crab dance party dissolved as naturally as it had assembled. Clip retreated to his spot beneath the flat rock and waited for the next low tide.

He always had more ideas for the next one.


Clip's current best idea was to incorporate the flow of water through the pool's inlet into the choreography somehow. The anemone was willing. The periwinkles were uncertain but present.

The tide came back. The rock pool filled. The side step was paused until next time.

Next time, Clip thought, from his spot under the flat rock, would be better. It always was.
