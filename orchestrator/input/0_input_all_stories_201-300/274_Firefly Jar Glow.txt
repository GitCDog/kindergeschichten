The jar had a lid with small holes punched in it, and inside the jar on a warm July evening, there was one firefly named Spark.

Spark had been caught very gently by a boy named Eli, who had been sitting in the garden with his grandmother watching the fireflies move through the darkening meadow. Eli had held the jar carefully and closed the lid, and Spark had found herself in a new and interesting situation.

The night glow began.

Spark did what she always did: she lit up. A cold green-yellow pulse, a second's worth of bioluminescence, then dark, then light again. From outside the jar, this must look like a little lantern. She knew this, in the way fireflies know things — not with words, but with the understanding that light catches the night glow.

Eli held the jar up and watched her.

He had never been this close to a firefly before. From the garden they were just drifting sparks — beautiful and imprecise. Inside the jar she was a specific creature: small black body, wings folded, the light organ at her abdomen pulsing with patient regularity.

"She's doing it on purpose," Eli told his grandmother.

"They all do," his grandmother said. "It's how they find each other."

Eli looked out at the meadow, where other fireflies were still moving through the dusk. He thought about Spark's light catching the attention of other lights. A conversation in pulses, held over a hundred meters of summer air.

He opened the lid.

Spark sat for a moment, as if considering. Then she flew — straight up, over the jar's rim, into the warm dark. Her light blinked once, twice, and then she was lost among the others, indistinguishable again.

But Eli knew which one was her. For a few minutes more, he watched and tried to follow the pattern of her light.

The meadow pulsed gently in the dusk.


Eli kept the jar on the table beside him for the rest of the evening, lid off. He would be ready if another firefly happened to wander in by accident. None did, but the possibility felt good.

At bedtime he took the jar inside and set it on the windowsill, and fell asleep thinking about the meadow pulsing with a hundred conversations in light.
