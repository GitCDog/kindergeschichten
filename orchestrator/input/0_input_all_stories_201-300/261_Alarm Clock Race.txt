Duke was a brave alarm clock — round and white with two metal bells on top and a hammer that struck them at exactly the moment required. He had been on the bedside table for twelve years and had never once failed to wake his owner, a boy named Ned, on time.

The wake mission was Duke's highest calling.

But Ned was an accomplished sleeper. He had developed, over the years, an extensive repertoire of sleeping-through-alarms techniques. He could silence Duke without fully waking. He could incorporate Duke's bells into his dreams. He could, on bad mornings, move Duke to the dresser and be back asleep in ninety seconds.

So Duke had adapted. He no longer simply rang. He had become strategic.

The morning race began at six forty-five.

Duke's first bell would ring, and he would listen. If he heard the bedclothes shift and the particular sound of Ned surfacing, he knew the morning was won. If he heard nothing but steady breathing, he knew round two was coming.

Round two came at six forty-eight. Three short rings, then three long ones.

On most mornings, this was enough. Ned would surface with a groan, reach out a blind arm, and tap Duke on the head — not turning him off, but pressing the snooze, which Duke found acceptable. Snooze meant: I acknowledge you. I am working on it.

On the mornings that required round three, Duke rang with everything he had. Both bells, full power, sustained for ten seconds. This was the alarm clock equivalent of a distress flare.

Ned would surface.

This morning was a round two morning. Ned snooze-pressed at six forty-eight, lay still for seven minutes, then sighed and put both feet on the floor. Duke watched him cross the room, running one hand through his hair, already becoming more awake with each step.

"Fine," Ned would usually say, to no one in particular.

Duke rested on the bedside table, his morning race run successfully. The day was beginning. His work was done.

He counted down the hours until tomorrow's wake mission, steady and ready.
