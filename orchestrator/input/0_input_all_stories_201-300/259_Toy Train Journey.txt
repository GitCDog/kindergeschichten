Beneath the Christmas tree — but it wasn't Christmas now, it was an ordinary Tuesday in March, which made the whole arrangement even more impressive — the magic toy train ran its oval track continuously, as it had done every evening for the past week.

The train was named Sterling, and his five carriages were named in order: Bert, Clive, Doris, Ed, and Fern. Together they constituted the most important railway in the living room, and they knew it.

Sterling blew his small whistle as he entered the first curve of the track world.

The track world was not large — an oval of about two meters — but Sterling had long ago learned that the interest of a journey lay not in the distance covered but in the attention paid. He knew exactly where the track wobbled slightly at the join near the bookcase. He knew the moment when the curve before the station ran perfectly smooth, the wheels finding exactly the right groove.

He carried cargo tonight: several wooden blocks arranged in Ed's open carriage, and a small rubber elephant that had been placed in Doris by a child before bed.

The rubber elephant seemed perfectly happy about this.

The journey ride circled and circled. Sterling kept his speed perfectly steady — fast enough to feel purposeful, slow enough to observe. The living room walls passed at a comfortable distance. The bookcase went by on the left, the armchair on the right, the rug dead ahead before the curve.

At some point the child who had arranged the train had fallen asleep on the sofa above, and now only the parents remained, reading their books. Occasionally one of them would look down at the moving track and the small steady train and feel a particular kind of calm.

Sterling ran his oval faithfully.

The blocks stayed in Ed. The rubber elephant looked ahead from Doris with an expression of complete contentment.

On the track world, all journeys were circular — which meant every ending was also a beginning.


Sterling ran until the clock chimed nine, and then a hand reached down and lifted him gently from the track and switched off the motor. The carriages came to rest in a line. The rubber elephant looked serenely at the bookcase.

In the morning, the oval would be there. The track world patient and waiting, and Sterling ready to begin another steady circle.
