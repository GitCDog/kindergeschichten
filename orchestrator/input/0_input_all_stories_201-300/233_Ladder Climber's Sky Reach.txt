Leo was a brave ladder climber who had been climbing ladders since he was old enough to reach the first rung. His ladder was tall and wooden, with paint-speckled rungs from years of helping people reach the places they couldn't reach on their own.

But Leo had one dream that was bigger than all the others: he wanted to climb so high that he could touch the sky.

He had heard people say it was impossible. Ladders ended. The sky went on forever. You could never reach one with the other.

Leo didn't see why he shouldn't try.

One morning he set up his ladder in the middle of the meadow, where the ground was flat and the sky above was wide and blue and cloud-scattered. He checked each rung — tap, tap, tap — to make sure all was solid. Then he began to climb.

The first dozen rungs were familiar. The world at this height was already a little different — he could see over the garden wall, could spot a bird's nest in the old oak, could see the glint of the stream through the trees.

He climbed higher. The high wall of the world fell away below him, and the meadow became a green square and then a green smudge. His knuckles whitened on the rails. The wind pushed at him from the side, but he pressed his face into it and kept going.

He reached the last rung.

He stretched one hand above his head as far as it would go — up, up, up — fingers splayed, reaching for the sky reach he had always dreamed of.

And then something extraordinary happened. A cloud drifted close, and its cool mist curled around his fingertips. Not solid, but real. Not a surface, but a presence.

He had touched it.

Leo climbed back down slowly, smiling all the way to the bottom. He folded up his ladder and carried it home under his arm. That evening, watching the clouds from his window, he could still feel the cool mist on his fingertips.

Some goals, he knew now, were not about reaching a finish line. They were about the joy of the climb itself.
