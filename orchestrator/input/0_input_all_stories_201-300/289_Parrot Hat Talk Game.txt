The silly parrot hat had been purchased at a seaside shop by a grandmother who had excellent judgment about hats and had looked at the display and said, "That one," immediately.

It was a wide-brimmed straw hat with a painted parrot on the front, feather wings spread, beak open in what the designer clearly intended as a cheerful expression but which looked, depending on the angle, either delighted or extremely agitated.

The hat's name was Poll.

Poll was taken to the beach and worn for approximately forty-five minutes before the wind took her off and she rolled across the sand toward a family eating sandwiches. The grandmother retrieved her with no loss of dignity and jammed her on more firmly.

The talk game began that afternoon.

The talk game worked as follows: when you wore Poll, you were required to speak in parrot phrases. This rule was invented by a seven-year-old named Sam who considered it non-negotiable.

"Pretty Poll! What's for dinner! Pieces of eight!" said Sam's mother, wearing Poll with the air of someone who had made peace with many things.

"Squawk! Storm ahead! Pretty Poll!" said Sam, when it was his turn.

The grandmother wore Poll for the final round. She was in her eighties and had a great deal of dignity and absolutely no interest in sacrificing it. She put Poll on her head and looked at the assembled family.

"Polly wants a biscuit," she said, in a tone of complete composure.

Everyone laughed including Poll, who could not actually laugh but whose painted parrot seemed, in that moment, to have deepened its expression into genuine delight.

The feather fun of the hat — the slight tickle of the decorative plume on the band — made whoever wore Poll smile involuntarily. Even the grandmother. Even on the walk back to the car when the wind was against them.


Poll was placed on the hat stand that evening and regarded the hallway with her painted parrot expression — which was, depending on the light, either jubilant or slightly affronted. Both seemed appropriate for a hat that had seen what she had seen.

She smelled of sunscreen and sea air and the kind of afternoon that holds in the memory for years.
