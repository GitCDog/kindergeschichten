The pair of drumsticks lived in the stick bag hanging off the side of the drum kit, and they came out every afternoon when the boy named Sam arrived home from school, dropped his bag in the hallway, and sat down at the kit with a particular sigh that meant: now I can actually breathe.

The drumsticks were named Left and Right (Sam's naming convention was practical if not imaginative), and they had between them accumulated several years of beat tap experience.

Left had a chip in her lacquer from a missed stroke on a rim. Right had a small crack near the top that had been discovered, assessed, and deemed non-structural. They were working drumsticks, not ornamental ones, and they were proud of it.

The beat tap began.

Sam always started with a simple pattern — four on the kick, hi-hat on every beat, snare on two and four. This was the skeleton, the thing you laid down first so everything else had somewhere to live.

Then he would start to build. A fill here: a run across the toms, coming back to the snare at the phrase-end. A variation there: opening the hi-hat on beat four to let the sound breathe.

Music joy was what Left and Right felt during the good sessions. Not when Sam played perfectly — he often didn't — but when he was lost in it, when his hands moved without him consciously directing them, when he stopped thinking about where to hit and simply hit.

The drum kit vibrated under them both. The kick drum sent a pulse through the floor that the cat, one room over, felt in its sleeping body and registered without waking.

The session ended when Sam's arms tired. He placed Left and Right back in the stick bag carefully, side by side.

They rested against the warm kit, still humming faintly with the music that had moved through them, waiting for tomorrow.


Left and Right had made music today that no one had written down and no one had recorded. It existed only in the memory of Sam's hands and the faint resonance still humming in the drum skins.

That was fine. Music joy didn't need to be kept to have been real. It had moved through the room and it had been good, and tomorrow they would do it again.
