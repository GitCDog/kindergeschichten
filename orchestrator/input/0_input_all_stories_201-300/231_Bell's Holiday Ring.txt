Up in the tall clock tower of a little village, hanging among a family of larger, louder bells, there was a tiny bell named Ling. Ling was the smallest bell in the tower by far — barely the size of an apple — and on most days, she worried that her ring sound was too quiet to matter.

The bigger bells boomed and clanged for weddings and festivals and important announcements. Ling would add her small, clear note at the very end, like a punctuation mark on a sentence no one had quite finished.

But then came the holiday, and everything changed.

It was the Festival of First Snow — the town's most beloved celebration, held on the morning the first flakes fell from the sky. The tradition was for all the bells to ring together in a great chorus of holiday cheer, filling the whole valley with sound.

The morning of the festival arrived cold and bright. Ling could see snowflakes drifting past the tower window, tiny and perfect.

The big bells swung into action. The great bass bell, who the others called Old Bram, sent a deep rolling boom out over the rooftops. The middle bells added their warm harmonies. And then it was Ling's turn.

She swung — and her ring sound flew out, clear and high and pure, above all the other notes. It was the note that sailed farthest, carried by the cold clean air over the frozen fields and through the windows of the farthest houses, where children were just waking up.

Later, a little boy came to the tower with his mother, and he said, "I heard a tiny bell — just one note, right at the top — and it sounded like the holiday itself."

His mother smiled and looked up at Ling, who caught the winter light and shone like a star.

Old Bram rumbled quietly, "Told you, little one. It was always your note they were waiting for."

That night, Ling swayed gently in the wind that found its way through the tower slats. Below her, the village glittered with candles and laughter and the soft crunch of new snow underfoot. Her holiday cheer rang on inside her long after her last note had faded.

She was a tiny bell. And she was exactly enough.
