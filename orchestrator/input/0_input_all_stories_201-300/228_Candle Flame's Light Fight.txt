In the very center of a stone mantelpiece, in a tall white candle, there lived a brave candle flame named Flick. Flick was small — just a teardrop of orange and gold — but she burned with a steadiness that surprised everyone who saw her.

The dark room was Flick's greatest challenge and her greatest purpose. Every evening when the lights went out, the darkness settled in from all the corners of the room at once, thick and soft and complete.

"Don't be afraid," Flick would whisper to the furniture, though the armchair and the bookshelf could not reply.

One stormy night, the light fight was worse than usual. The wind howled outside and rain hammered the windows, and every time a gust found its way under the door, Flick bent sideways and nearly went out entirely.

"I will not give in," she told the darkness.

The darkness did not answer. It never did. It simply waited, patient and heavy, for the flame to tire.

But Flick had been lit by a kind hand, and kind hands give flames a particular sort of courage. She straightened herself after every gust and pushed her glow outward — across the mantelpiece, over the arm of the chair, under the edge of the door.

A little moth appeared, drawn by her warmth, and fluttered nearby.

"You are very small to fight such a big darkness," the moth observed.

"Small is enough," said Flick. "Watch."

She breathed out her warmth in a long, slow wave. The circle of light around her grew, just barely, just enough. The armchair emerged from shadow. The bookshelf showed its spines. The corner where the cat slept lit up just enough to see the rise and fall of soft breathing.

The storm grumbled and moved on.

In the quiet that followed, the darkness did not disappear — but it retreated to the very edges, where it sat small and distant, held back by one brave candle flame no larger than a thumb.

When the candle burned low and Flick finally flickered out just before dawn, the first grey light of morning came through the curtains and took her place. The room was never without light for long.

Some fights, Flick had learned, were not about winning forever. They were about holding on until the morning came.
