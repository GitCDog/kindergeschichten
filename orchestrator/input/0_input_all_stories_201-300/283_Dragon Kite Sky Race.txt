The dragon kite's name was Flame, and she was constructed from red and orange ripstop nylon, with a long tail of yellow streamers and a face painted on her leading edge that showed a dragon mid-roar, which was appropriate for a kite heading into a sky fight.

Today was the annual kite race on the hill above town.

The hill was perfect for racing — a steady westerly, no trees above the launch point, and a long clear slope that let the kites build height before the race judges marked their positions.

Flame's flyer was a boy named Dan, who had made Flame himself over three wet weekends in January and had been waiting six months for today.

The wind race began at noon.

The judge blew a whistle, and twenty kites lurched into the sky at once. The hill was loud with snapping fabric and shouted instructions. Flame rose clean and fast — Dan had balanced her perfectly, her bridle set to exactly the right angle.

Beside Flame, a blue diamond kite surged upward at an angle that suggested she was going for height immediately. Two kite-lengths to the left, a bright yellow box kite — they were steady but slow. And directly above, already impossibly high, was a small silver delta that had launched perfectly and seemed to be operating on its own updraft.

Dan paid out string steadily, watching Flame's nose angle. Too steep and she'd stall. Too shallow and she'd lose height.

She climbed.

The sky fight wasn't about damaging other kites — it was about altitude, about staying stable while the others wobbled, about reading the wind's shifts before they arrived.

Flame held her line at forty meters when the blue diamond stalled and came spinning down. At fifty meters, the box kite began to oscillate dangerously.

Flame flew.

At the end of the race, she was still in the sky, tail streaming, the painted dragon still mid-roar.


Dan wound the string slowly as Flame descended, walking backward to keep her stable, his eyes on the painted dragon face until the last moment. She landed softly on the grass, wings folding.

He held her in both arms and looked at the empty sky where she had been. Next year he would make her lighter, more responsive. He already had ideas.
