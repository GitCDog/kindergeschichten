On the kitchen wall, between the clock and the window, hung a friendly calendar named Cal. Cal had twelve pages — one for each month — and on each page were the grid squares that held all the days of the year, waiting to be filled in.

Cal took his job very seriously. He was a time story in paper form, and every month he witnessed the writing of thirty or thirty-one days of a family's life.

January was quiet and clean-slated, full of resolution. The family wrote carefully in January, as though the year were new soil that shouldn't be scuffed. They wrote dentist appointments and birthdays and the date school started back.

By March, the writing had loosened. Crossings-out had appeared. One square had an arrow pointing to the next. A child had drawn a small cat in the margin of the sixteenth.

May was electric with activity — a trip, a birthday party, a school performance, a vet appointment for the dog, and one mysterious square that just said CHEESE in capital letters that no one could later explain.

Summer was different. Some weeks in July had nothing written on them at all, which was its own kind of time story. Empty squares in summer meant days without schedules, mornings that stretched into afternoons that slid into evenings without any particular structure. Cal found these the most interesting squares of all. What had happened on those blank days? He didn't know. He had only been told about the planned things.

By November, Cal was looking worn. His corners had curled. The pen holder attached to his spine had lost its pen. But November was full of things to count — days until a birthday, days until the winter holiday, the day count of weeks left in the school term.

December arrived and the squares filled up fast, as they always did.

On the last day of the year, Cal was taken down and carried to the recycling bin. He went without complaint. He had done his work. A whole year had passed through his squares, and not a single day had been lost.
