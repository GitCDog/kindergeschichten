Folded from a single crisp white page, a playful paper plane named Swift had one great dream — to win the wind race across the school yard. Every afternoon, children would launch their paper planes from the top of the wooden steps, and the one that flew the farthest won a golden paper star.

Swift had practiced all week. He had tuned his wings to slice the breeze just right and made sure his nose was perfectly sharp. His best friend, a crumpled paper crane named Origami, cheered from the bench.

On race day, the school yard was full of colour. There were striped planes, spotted planes, and one enormous blue plane with painted flames. Swift felt small beside them but stood straight and ready.

"On your marks, steady — fly!" called the teacher, and every plane took to the air at once.

Swift caught the first gust perfectly. He climbed higher than the others, angling upward into a warm pocket of moving air. For a moment, he seemed to glide forever. But then the big blue plane with the painted flames — its owner had thrown it with tremendous force — pulled ahead.

Swift did not give up. He remembered what Origami had told him: "Go with the wind, not against it." Instead of fighting the breeze that was nudging him sideways, he tilted just a little and let it carry him gently forward.

The blue plane began to tip and spiral. It was too heavy for the gentle afternoon wind, and it drifted down into the flower garden. Swift floated on, steady and calm, coasting on a long, smooth current of air until he touched down perfectly — right past the chalk finish line.

The children cheered. Origami jumped up so fast he tumbled off the bench. The teacher pressed a golden paper star onto Swift's wing with a smile.

That evening, Swift sat on the windowsill watching the sky turn pink and orange. He thought about how being light and patient had carried him farther than being big and loud ever could.

He folded his wings gently and drifted into a quiet, contented rest.
