In the kitchen drawer between the spatulas and the butter knives, there lived a silly spoon named Syl. Syl had a round bowl and a long handle and was made of silver that had gone pleasantly dull from years of use. She was excellent at soup and porridge and all the serious spoon duties — but what she really loved was the kitchen fun.

The kitchen slide began the first time Syl accidentally slid off the counter.

She had been resting on the edge of a bowl, slightly off-balance, when a draft from the window tipped her over. She hit the smooth kitchen floor and slid — in a magnificent arc — all the way to the base of the fridge.

It was the most fun she had ever had.

From then on, whenever the kitchen was quiet, Syl would inch herself to the counter's edge and launch a bowl slide down to the floor. The smooth floor was the perfect surface — it offered just enough friction to slow her down at the end without stopping the journey abruptly. She would spin clockwise as she went, the round bowl of her leading the way, the long handle streaming out behind.

The other utensils thought she was peculiar. The whisk disapproved. The potato masher was too square to understand. Even the ladle — who might have been expected to empathize — kept his opinions to himself.

But the measuring cups watched with interest, and one Wednesday, the small quarter-cup worked up the courage to try it herself. She was not quite as elegant as Syl — she bumped and clattered rather than sliding — but she laughed all the way to the fridge.

"I see the appeal," she admitted.

That evening when the family sat down to dinner, Syl was back in her drawer, perfectly composed. No one could have guessed she had spent the afternoon conducting experiments in kitchen aerodynamics.

Syl held her soup perfectly still and thought about the next opportunity.


The kitchen, it turned out, was full of adventures if you were willing to approach it from the right angle — which for Syl meant horizontal, moving fast across a smooth floor. She had no regrets about this philosophy.

And in the morning, she would be back in the drawer, round-bowled and ready, the most reliable spoon in the house.
