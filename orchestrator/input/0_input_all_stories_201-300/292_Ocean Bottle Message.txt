The magic ocean bottle had been sealed with wax and set afloat from a pier in September, and inside it was a single sheet of paper, folded twice, with a message written in careful cursive by a twelve-year-old named Fen.

The message float began.

The bottle was green glass — a wine bottle repurposed, cleaned, and re-sealed — and she rode well in the water, bobbing upright with most of her length above the surface, the wax-sealed cork holding firm against the salt.

The sea current took her south first, then east. She passed the harbor mouth and cleared the headland and entered open water. The days passed. Rain came, and the bottle rode it without difficulty. Seabirds investigated and lost interest. A large piece of driftwood came alongside for half a day before diverging.

The message inside was dry.

Fen had written: My name is Fen. I am twelve. I am sending this bottle because I want to know how far things can travel. If you find this, please write to me. The address was their house address, printed carefully.

The sea travel took four months.

The bottle was found by a retired fisherman walking a beach on a different coast entirely — several hundred kilometers from where she had started. He saw the glint of green glass in the tide wrack and went to investigate.

He took the bottle home, removed the cork, extracted the message.

He read it twice. Then he found a piece of paper and an envelope and sat down at his kitchen table.

Dear Fen, he wrote. Your bottle traveled well. It arrived in good condition, on a good morning, to someone who was glad to find it. I hope this finds you equally well.

He gave a distance and a date and his name.

The letter arrived two weeks later.

Fen read it four times.


Fen wrote back. The exchange continued through winter — four letters total, between a child who had sent a bottle and a fisherman who had found it, two people who had nothing else in common except a shared curiosity about how far things traveled.

The bottle itself sat on Fen's windowsill, green glass in the light, a keeper of the story it had started.
