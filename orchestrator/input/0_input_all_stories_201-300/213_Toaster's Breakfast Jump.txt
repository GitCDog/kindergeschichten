In the kitchen at number seven, among the kettles and the fruit bowl, stood a friendly toaster named Pop. He had a shiny chrome body, two wide slots, and a habit of humming very quietly to himself when no one was watching.

Every morning in breakfast town — as the toaster called the kitchen — was an adventure. The butter dish would slide, the jam jar would spin open with a pop (which always made Pop feel proud of his own name), and the orange juice would pour itself out in a golden stream.

But Pop's favourite moment was always the bread jump. He would receive his bread slices with great care, tuck them in gently, and begin the slow warming work. He'd feel the bread change beneath the heat — softening, then firming, turning from pale and doughy to golden and crisp.

Then, at precisely the right moment — not a second early, not a second late — he would release the spring.

[boing]

The toast would leap into the air in a perfect arc, spinning once before landing on the plate with a cheerful clatter. The children always laughed when this happened. It never got old.

One morning, a new type of bread arrived. It was thicker than usual and a little uneven. Pop accepted it carefully and began his work. But the spring had to be adjusted — too strong and the thick bread would fly off the counter entirely.

Pop was thoughtful. He let the heat go a little longer than usual, until the bread was perfectly golden all the way through. Then he released the spring at half strength.

The thick toast rose gently, turned a slow half-turn, and settled on the plate as softly as a leaf.

The child looked at it. "That's the best toast I've ever had," she said, and spread a thick layer of butter that melted slowly into all the little holes.

Pop hummed contentedly to himself. In breakfast town, every morning was worth the effort.

Later that morning, when the kitchen was quiet and the children had gone to school, Pop sat warm and still on the counter. The butter dish had been put away. The jam jar was back in the cupboard. But the smell of toast — warm and golden and good — still hung in the air, and Pop hummed softly to himself. Some jobs seem small, he thought, until you see someone's face when they are done just right.
