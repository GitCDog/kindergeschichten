Every morning, when the curtains were opened in the east-facing room, a cheerful sunbeam named Ray arrived.

Ray came at an angle that changed by a few degrees each day as the year turned — steeper in winter, more nearly horizontal in summer — and fell in a long stripe across the floorboards that moved as the morning progressed, rotating slowly like the hand of a clock tracking the sun's arc.

The warm hug was Ray's gift, and he gave it freely.

He fell first on the wooden floor, where the boards held his warmth and released it slowly through the day. Then, as the angle changed, he climbed the far wall, and whatever sat in his path received his attention: a row of books (their spines warm to the touch by nine o'clock), a small glass jar that broke Ray into a scatter of smaller lights on the ceiling, the sleeping cat.

The cat received the warm hug with the full dignity of its species: turning once, adjusting by fifteen degrees, and settling back to sleep directly in the center of the sun stripe.

Ray did not mind sharing.

Through the morning he traveled. By midday he had left the east-facing room entirely, moved on to wherever the sky light fell next. But the warmth he had deposited stayed in the floorboards and the wall for hours after his departure.

A warm room in the middle of a cool day carries that warmth forward. It holds it.

There was a woman who came to this room every morning specifically to sit in Ray's stripe with her tea, for the first twenty minutes of the day. She had done this for six years.

She did not think of it as spiritual. She thought of it as warm and good and the necessary beginning of the day — which was, perhaps, another way of saying the same thing.


The woman finished her tea. The stripe of sunlight had moved off her lap and was climbing the wall toward the window. She watched it go and didn't follow it — she had her own day to begin.

But the warmth stayed with her. Ray had deposited it into her before moving on, and she carried it into the morning the way a room carries the memory of afternoon light.
