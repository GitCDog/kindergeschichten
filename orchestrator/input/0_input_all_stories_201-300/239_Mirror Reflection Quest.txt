On the wall of the hallway hung a brave mirror named Vera. Vera had a gilt frame and a slightly wavy surface that made everything she reflected just a little more interesting than it was. She had hung there for forty years and had reflected approximately seventeen thousand people, most of them in a hurry.

One afternoon, a girl named June stood in front of Vera for a very long time. Not fixing her hair or checking her collar — just looking.

Vera looked back, as she always did.

"Do you think I look like myself?" June asked finally.

Vera considered this a remarkable question. She had never had someone ask it before. Most people checked whether they looked like what they wanted to look like — neat, tidy, presentable. June was asking something quite different.

Vera studied June's reflection carefully. It was wavy at the edges, as all her reflections were, and the light in the hallway gave June's face a golden quality that she probably didn't notice herself. June's hair was slightly wild. Her expression was serious in a thoughtful way, not an unhappy way.

"You look exactly like yourself," Vera thought, though she had no voice to say it.

June seemed to hear something anyway. She tilted her head, and her reflection tilted back. She made a small smile, and the glass world smiled back. She raised one hand — the copy quest of the mirror — and watched her reflected hand rise in perfect synchrony.

"Hello," she said softly to her own reflection.

Vera held the image steady and warm.

June had been having a difficult week. Vera could not have known this, but she understood it the way mirrors understand things — by reflecting without judgment. Whatever June brought to the glass world, Vera showed back to her honestly, without changing it or making it worse.

June pressed one palm flat against the glass.

Her reflection pressed back.

They held that for a moment, girl and glass, two hands almost touching with only the bright cold surface between them.

Then June stepped back, straightened her collar after all, and went on down the hall.

Vera shone quietly in the afternoon light. She had done what she always did. And today, that had been exactly enough.
