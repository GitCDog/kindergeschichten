On the shelf above the desk, between a trophy for swimming and a pile of comics, stood a friendly robot toy named Cog. Cog was approximately twenty centimeters tall, made of grey and silver plastic, with a round head, small red LED eyes, and a panel on his chest that had three buttons on it. None of the buttons did anything observable, but they looked important.

Cog's gear spin made a satisfying sound when you turned the dial on his back. Not a mechanical gear — just a ratcheting plastic mechanism that made the sound of productivity without performing any actual function. This bothered no one.

He had been on the shelf for four years, which in toy years was a substantial tenure. He had been through a phase of daily play, then a phase of occasional play, then a long phase of simply being present on the shelf as part of the room's furniture.

He didn't mind. A play friend didn't have to be active every day to be important.

This was demonstrated clearly on the afternoon that Milo came home from school in a bad mood and didn't want to talk to anyone and didn't want to do anything but sit at his desk and be left alone.

He sat at his desk.

After a while, without quite planning to, he reached up and took Cog off the shelf. He turned the dial. The gear spin sound went round and round.

He pressed button one. Nothing happened.
He pressed button two. Nothing.
He pressed button three. Nothing.

He held Cog in both hands and looked at the red LED eyes.

The LED eyes looked back, small and steady.

"Bad day," Milo said.

Cog had no way to respond. But he stayed in Milo's hands for twenty minutes, warm from being held, the gear spin making its small patient sound, present in the particular way that very good friends know how to be.


When Milo went to bed that night, he put Cog back on the shelf — not dropped, but placed. Cog stood between the trophy and the comics and looked out at the room with his small red LED eyes.

He was a play friend. That meant he was there for the good days and the bad ones both, without needing to be asked.

[pause] Good night my little friend
