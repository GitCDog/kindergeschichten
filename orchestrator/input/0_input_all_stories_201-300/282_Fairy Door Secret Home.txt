At the base of the old oak tree in the garden, half-covered by moss and a drift of fallen leaves, there was a little fairy door. It was painted blue — a deep, particular blue — with a tiny brass knocker shaped like an acorn and two small hinges that had been added with great care.

The door was built by a girl named Lena, who had put it there three years ago and checked it every morning since.

She had learned, over these three years, the etiquette of a secret home. You didn't try to open the door yourself — that would be rude. You knocked, if you chose to knock, very lightly. You left small things sometimes: a single dried flower, a glass bead, a piece of blue ribbon. And you checked in the morning to see if anything had changed.

Things had changed.

Not always dramatically. Once the glass bead was gone. Once there was a small mark in the bark above the door that might have been carved by something or might have been made by a beetle. Once — on the morning of a rainstorm — the doorstep was covered in tiny footprints in the mud that were much too small to be any animal Lena knew.

She had documented everything in a notebook.

She was twelve now and applied the scientific method to her observations, which was only responsible. But scientific method didn't exclude wonder. It just gave wonder better tools.

The tree base in which the door was set had grown slightly over the years, the bark encroaching millimeters each season. In another decade or two, the door would be fully incorporated into the tree's own structure. Lena thought about this sometimes.

She thought the tree was, perhaps, accepting it.

The secret home at the base of the oak existed whether or not it was occupied. And that certainty — the door, the knocker, the blue paint — was its own kind of magic.


Lena photographed the door each season — spring when the moss was bright, summer when wildflowers grew beside it, autumn when leaves covered the threshold, winter when frost traced the hinges. A four-season record, building year by year, of something small that stayed.

She didn't know what lived behind it. She wasn't sure the not-knowing was a problem.
