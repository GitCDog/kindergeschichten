High on the windy hill above the village of Tremaine, there lived a tiny whistle named Pip who had the most extraordinary sound call. One clear note — shrill, silver, and precise — that could travel three times farther than any other whistle in the valley.

Pip belonged to the head shepherd, and every morning at dawn the shepherd would put Pip to his lips and send the sound call rolling out across the hill. The sheep would lift their heads, and the sheepdog — a quick grey dog named Nell — would take her position. Without the whistle, the whole morning would be disorganized. With it, everything began.

But the best thing about Pip's note — the thing that made her special among whistles — was the echo hill.

On the other side of the valley, the rocky face of the hill caught Pip's call and sent it back. Not immediately, and not exact — the echo came half a second later, softer, curved around the edges by the air it had traveled through. One note became two. The hill turned Pip's single sound into a conversation.

One morning, a young girl visiting her grandfather heard Pip's call for the first time. She was standing at the edge of the field, and the sound call hit her and the echo wrapped around her and she felt it in her chest.

"What was that?" she asked.

"That's the shepherd's whistle," her grandfather said. "And the hill answering."

The girl stood very still and listened as Pip's note rippled across the valley and came back changed and softer.

"It sounds like the hill is saying thank you," she said.

Her grandfather considered this for a moment. "I never thought of it that way," he said. "But you might be right."

Pip rested in the shepherd's coat pocket that afternoon, warm and content. She had started the day's work and fed the sheep and moved the flock and sent her echo call three times across the valley.

And one small girl had heard the hill answer.
