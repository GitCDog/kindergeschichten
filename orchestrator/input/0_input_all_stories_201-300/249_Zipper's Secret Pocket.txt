On the front of a small green backpack, there was a zipper named Zip. Not the main zipper — the little backpack zipper that opened the narrow front pocket, the one most people forgot about. Zip was smaller than the main zipper, with a tiny pull tab shaped like a leaf, and she had learned to be content with modest importance.

Because the secret pocket was, in fact, the most interesting part of the bag.

The main compartment held predictable things. Books, packed lunches, a raincoat. Items that were chosen and placed there deliberately, carried and removed with regularity. Nothing in the main compartment was a surprise.

But the front pocket — Zip's pocket — was where the interesting things lived.

Over the years, through open and close cycles beyond counting, Zip had guarded a collection that included: a note passed in class that said I think you're nice, a small smooth stone that had been carried home from a beach three summers ago and never returned, a key to a bicycle lock for a bicycle that no longer existed, a packet of ketchup, a hair tie in blue, two coins from a country that had not been visited recently, a lucky bottle cap, and a photograph folded into quarters.

Each thing had arrived with intention and stayed by forgetting.

Zip never judged. She simply kept the secret pocket closed and let the treasures accumulate. When the small owner reached in without looking and felt the stone — smooth and cold and round — something always passed across her face. A memory. A softening.

That was the real secret of the secret pocket: it kept things that were too good to throw away and too small to make a fuss about.

One evening the child unzipped Zip very slowly and looked at everything inside. She took the photograph out, unfolded it, looked at it for a long time, and put it back.

Zip closed gently.

Some things didn't need to be explained. They just needed to be kept.


Something small and real and irreplaceable lived in Zip's pocket every day of the year. She was, in the end, not just a zipper on a front pocket. She was a guardian of small, quiet, important things — and that felt like exactly the right kind of work.
