The brave moonlight arrived every clear night through the skylight in the upstairs room, and for many years it had fallen on a sleeping child named Wren.

The night glow was softer than sunlight — cooler in color, less directed — a general luminescence that didn't so much illuminate as suggest. Shapes became visible but not sharp. The room existed in a middle state between day and dark.

Wren had grown up with this moonlight. She was eleven now and could tell, without looking at a calendar, approximately what phase the moon was in by the quality of what came through the skylight. Full moon nights were almost bright enough to read by. New moon nights left the room genuinely dark.

The dream watch was what Wren called it.

She had decided, at some point around age seven, that the moon was watching over sleeping things the way a lighthouse watched over the sea — not interfering, not directing, just being present. Reliable. The same light that had fallen on the same room for as long as the room had had a skylight.

The moon was very old. This was a fact that Wren had looked up and confirmed. The light that reached her had bounced from a surface four billion years old, traveled three hundred and eighty-four thousand kilometers, and arrived at her skylight having done nothing else between the moon and her room.

The night glow found her.

She had started staying awake some nights to watch the moonbeam move across the ceiling, which it did slowly, tracking the moon's own movement. By three in the morning it was gone, replaced by honest dark.

She never stayed awake that long.

She would watch for a while, feel the cool silver of it on her face, and close her eyes.

The dream watch would continue without her. It always did.


The skylight was there before Wren was born and would be there after. Moon and room and child were in temporary alignment, three things that happened to coincide for a particular number of years before life rearranged them.

For now, the dream watch was hers. The night glow found her. She turned in her sleep toward the light, as living things do.
