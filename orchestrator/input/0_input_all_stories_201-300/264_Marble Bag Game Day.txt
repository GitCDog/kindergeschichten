The marble bag was a small blue velvet pouch with a drawstring top, and inside it lived a tiny marble bag community of forty-three glass spheres, each one unique.

There was a large clear marble called the King, who had a blue swirl inside him and was never used as a shooter because he was too precious. There was a pair of identical red ones called the Twins, who were always placed in the same corner together. There were small yellowish ones, and deep green ones, and some with bubbles trapped inside that caught the light in a particular way.

It was game set day — which happened on Saturdays in the garden.

The rules were complicated and had evolved over three summers of friend play into something only the participants fully understood. In essence: shoot your marble to hit the others, collect the ones you hit, and the player with the most marbles at the end wins.

But the real game was the negotiation that happened before and after.

"I'll trade you the yellow one for two small reds."

"The yellow one has a crack."

"It's barely a crack. It still rolls straight."

This was the marble economy, which was as old as marbles themselves.

Today three children played: Finn, Jess, and a newer player named Arlo who had brought only five marbles but was developing an excellent eye. By the end of the afternoon, Arlo had twenty-two.

The older players were impressed.

"He's good," Jess told Finn, while Arlo pretended not to hear.

When the game was over, all the marbles were gathered carefully from the grass and placed back in the blue velvet pouch. The drawstring was pulled tight. The bag rattled satisfyingly.

The marble community resettled inside — the Twins together, the King in his usual spot at the bottom, the new acquisitions finding their places among the old ones.

The blue bag went back on the shelf until next Saturday.


Arlo went home with his twenty-two marbles in a borrowed bag. He would bring his own next Saturday. The marble economy was patient with newcomers, as long as newcomers were willing to learn both the game and the negotiation that surrounded it.

The blue velvet pouch waited on the shelf, full and ready for next week.
