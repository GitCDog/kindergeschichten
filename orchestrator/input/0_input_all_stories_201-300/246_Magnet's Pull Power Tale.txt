On the laboratory shelf between a jar of paperclips and a small box of iron filings, there lived a curious magnet named Max. Max was horseshoe-shaped and painted red, with silver poles at each end that hummed with invisible force. He was, in his modest way, one of the most powerful things on the shelf.

Max was fascinated by his own pull power. Not in a boastful way — he simply found it mysterious. He couldn't see the force. He couldn't turn it off. He reached out invisibly in all directions all the time, and things either came toward him or they didn't, and he could never quite predict which.

One morning, a child named Theo arrived at the laboratory for a school visit. Theo picked up Max and immediately walked to the whiteboard, where the metal trim ran along the edge.

Max felt the pull power surge and — click — he stuck.

Theo peeled him off and carried him to metal land, which was what Theo called the tray of different materials set out for testing. There was wood and plastic and fabric and coins and paperclips and a key and a foil wrapper.

Max hovered near each one in turn.

Wood: nothing. Plastic: nothing. Fabric: nothing. Coins: a slight, uncertain tug.

Key: click. The key leaped to Max's pole as though it had been waiting.

Paperclips: they came in a chain — the first to Max, the second to the first, the third to the second — a line of silver loops hanging from his pole like a tiny metallic caterpillar.

Theo stared at the chain of paperclips and said, "How does it do that?"

Max would have liked to explain. He would have said: I reach through the air with a force I cannot see. I have no eyes, no hands, no voice. But I can make things move. I can pull them toward me across a distance. And each thing I pull becomes, briefly, like me — reaching forward and pulling the next one in.

Pull power was, in other words, a kind of kindness that traveled through chains.
