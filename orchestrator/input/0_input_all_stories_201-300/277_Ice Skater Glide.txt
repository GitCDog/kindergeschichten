Before the light came, there was the ice.

The frozen lake spread smooth and grey in the early morning, and a brave ice skater named Mira was the only person on it. Her breath made small clouds. Her blades made the only sound — a soft, continuous hiss, like wind through a very narrow gap.

The glide path was hers alone.

Mira had been skating since she was four. She was eleven now, and the ice was not something she thought about anymore — it was something she moved through, the way a fish moves through water: without having to consider the medium itself.

She pushed off on the left blade and glided. Then right. The strokes were long and even, each one adding speed, the momentum carrying forward in a smooth, uninterrupted line. At the far end of the lake she turned — a wide, banked curve — and came back on the glide path.

The morning light arrived slowly. First a pale grey-gold on the horizon, then spreading upward until the ice caught it and held it, throwing back a scattered brightness.

Mira tried a spin at the center of the lake. She brought her arms in, and the world turned faster around her — trees, shore, pale sky — all rotating in a clean circle. She came out of it with her arms wide, checking her position, blades already finding the straight line again.

There was nothing she could say about this that adequately described it.

The frozen lake in early morning was a world before the day began — quiet in a way that daytime never was, with its hissing blades and the cold air and the blue-grey ice underfoot, perfect and unmarked except by the tracings of her own path.

By midmorning others would come. The lake would fill with beginners and dog walkers on the edges and children who fell more than they glided.

But this hour was hers.


The ice would soften by afternoon, losing its early-morning perfection, becoming slightly pitted and marked. But for now it was unmarked except by her, and she moved through it as if it were made for exactly this.

In a way, this morning, it was.
