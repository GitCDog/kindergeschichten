At the very edge of the galaxy, in a region where the stars were packed closer together than anywhere else in the local neighborhood, there was a curious star cluster named Haven.

Haven was a globular cluster — ten thousand stars arranged in a rough sphere, bound together by gravity, moving together through the galaxy as a single vast family. The stars at Haven's center were close enough that on any planet orbiting them, the night sky would never truly be dark. Stars would fill the view in every direction, a permanent luminous scatter.

No one lived on those planets, as far as anyone knew. But if they did, they would know a sky that humans on Earth, looking at Haven from four hundred light-years away through telescopes, could only imagine.

The galaxy map showed Haven as a small smear of light in the constellation Hercules. To the naked eye, it was barely there — a fuzzy patch that sharpened, through binoculars, into something with texture and depth.

Through a telescope it was astonishing.

Ten thousand suns, each one a star. Each one, potentially, with planets. Each one having burned for twelve billion years — older than the solar system, older than Earth, old enough to have watched galaxies form and merge.

Haven was not curious about humans. Haven was simply Haven — old and gravitationally patient and moving through the galaxy at its own pace, its ten thousand members orbiting their shared center in cycles of hundreds of millions of years.

But space wonder worked in one direction.

A child on Earth looked through a telescope at a faint smear in Hercules and felt the size of things — not in a frightening way, but in the way that makes the world feel more rather than less. More full. More mysterious. More worth paying attention to.

Haven shone across four hundred light-years and said, simply: here. We are here.


The child who had looked through the telescope that evening carried Haven away with them — not in a jar or a photograph, but in the particular widening that happens when you understand, briefly, how much there is. Haven would be there next clear night. And the night after. Twelve billion years old and patient with the attention of a curious child.

The galaxy map had many such wonders. This had been one.
