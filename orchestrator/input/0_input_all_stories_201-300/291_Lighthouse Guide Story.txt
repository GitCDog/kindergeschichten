On the point of rock where the coast bent south and the shipping lane curved inward toward the harbor, there was a lighthouse named Luce. Luce had been there for a hundred and twelve years, and in that time she had guided approximately forty thousand ships through the narrows.

She didn't keep a count. But she was steady, and steadiness adds up.

The shore guide system worked simply: one flash every five seconds, visible for twenty-two miles in clear weather. Ships knew to count the flashes and look for the pattern that was uniquely Luce's — one-pause-one — distinguishing her from the group-flash lighthouses to the north.

The beam light reached out across the water in all weather. Rain didn't stop it. Fog reduced its range, but a lighthouse's job in fog was actually more important than in clear weather. The fog signal — a deep horn that sounded every thirty seconds — added its own voice to Luce's warnings on the worst nights.

Keepers had lived in Luce's base for the first eighty years. The last keeper, a man named Arne, had tended her flame through two storms that had bent the metal ladder and once removed the walkway railing entirely.

Arne had been replaced eventually by automation. Luce's light was now controlled by a timer and monitored remotely. But she didn't find this diminishment. She still shone. The ships still passed. The pattern was still one-pause-one.

One night a small sailing boat caught in an unexpected squall made the narrows safely by counting Luce's flashes, and the two sailors aboard did not know the lighthouse's age or history. They knew only that the light was there and that it meant: here is the safe channel. This is where to go.

Luce shone her beam into the wet dark.

That was the whole of it, and it was enough.


The light cycled on as it always had. One-pause-one. Through the long night, and through all the nights after it, the beam swept the water and the water received it and the ships that needed it found their way.

Luce was one hundred and twelve years old and entirely reliable. She intended to remain so.
