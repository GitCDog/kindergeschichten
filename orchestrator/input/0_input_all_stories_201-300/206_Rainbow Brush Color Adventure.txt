In the corner of a dusty art room, there sat a magic rainbow brush that nobody used anymore. Her name was Iris, and she had bristles in every colour — red, orange, yellow, green, blue, and the deepest violet you have ever seen. But without a painter's hand to guide her, she had not made a single brushstroke in months.

One evening, after the last child had gone home and the art room fell quiet, Iris decided she had been still long enough. She hopped off her jar, dipped herself into a pot of red paint, and stepped onto the big white paper that lay stretched across the table.

She began small — a tiny red apple in the corner. Then she dipped into orange and added a glowing sunset behind it. Yellow came next, spreading warmth across the paper like a morning field of wheat. Green gave her rolling hills and a forest at the edges. Blue brought the sky and a river running through the middle of her colour world.

When she reached the violet paint, Iris paused. Violet was for special things. She thought about what the colour world still needed. Then, slowly and carefully, she painted a small house on the hillside — with a violet roof and warm light in the windows.

A paint splash landed on her bristles and she laughed, twirling across the paper in a great curling arc. The colours swirled and blended in swirling spirals of light. She did not worry about staying inside the lines or making it perfect. She simply painted the world as she felt it.

By the time the moon rose above the art room windows, the paper was full of colour — alive and glowing. Iris stepped back and looked at what she had made. It was the most beautiful thing she had ever seen.

She settled back into her jar, her bristles soft and warm with paint. The colour world she had made was real now, in the only way that mattered — it existed, and it was wonderful.
