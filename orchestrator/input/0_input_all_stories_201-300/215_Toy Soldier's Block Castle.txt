On the top shelf of the playroom, lined up in a neat row, stood a regiment of toy soldiers. The bravest of them was a small red-coated soldier named Drum. He had a painted-on expression of great seriousness, though if you looked very closely, the corners of his mouth curved slightly upward.

One evening after the children had gone to bed, the playroom declared war — not a real war, but a friendly building competition. The building blocks on one side of the room challenged the toy soldiers on the other to see who could build the tallest, strongest block castle.

Drum accepted the challenge without hesitation. "We'll use strategy," he told his regiment, and set immediately to work planning.

The blocks team went high and fast, stacking quickly in a rush of colour. But Drum built carefully and slow. First a wide, solid base. Then strong corner towers. Then walls that connected each tower, with small gaps for windows.

"You're going too slowly!" called the tallest blue block from across the room.

Drum did not answer. He kept building. His regiment passed each block up carefully, one at a time, testing each new level before adding the next.

By midnight, the blue blocks had built a tower three times the height of Drum's castle. It swayed slightly in the draught from the window.

Then the window blew open. A gust of cool air swept through the playroom war zone. The blue tower shuddered and leaned... and leaned... and came down with a tremendous [crash], sending blocks rolling across the floor.

Drum's castle did not move. The wide base held firm, the corner towers stood steady, and the walls held every block in place.

The playroom was quiet for a moment. Then from somewhere under the scattered blocks came a small voice: "That's a very good castle."

Drum allowed the corners of his painted mouth to curve just a little more upward.

The next morning, the children came into the playroom and found the castle still standing. They did not know about the playroom war, but they recognized quality when they saw it. One child immediately began adding towers on top. Another built a drawbridge. By the end of the afternoon, Drum's careful foundation had become the grandest structure the playroom had ever held. He stood at the gate and tried not to look too pleased about it.
