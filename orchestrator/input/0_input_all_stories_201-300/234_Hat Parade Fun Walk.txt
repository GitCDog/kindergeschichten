Every year in the little town of Millbury, the Hat Parade wound its cheerful way down the main street, and every year it was the greatest fun walk anyone could remember. The hats were the stars of the show — not the people wearing them, though the people were quite nice too.

This year's parade began at noon.

First came Topsy, a tall silk top hat so old that his brim had developed a distinguished wobble. He had been in seventeen parades and had the confidence to prove it. He processed down the fashion lane with great dignity, bow-legged slightly to one side.

Behind him bounced Sunny, a wide straw sun hat trimmed with yellow ribbons and a cluster of paper sunflowers. She was new to parades and could barely contain her joy. She bobbed with every step.

Then came a line of red fezzes, worn by the town's model train club. They marched in perfect formation, fezzes bobbing in unison, which made the spectators laugh every single time.

A small girl had entered her cat's hat — a tiny knitted beanie with two ear-holes — and the cat wore it with an expression of profound indignity that everyone agreed was the funniest thing they had ever seen.

At the very end of the fashion lane fun walk came an enormous papier-mâché hat shaped like a galleon in full sail, carried by six people and wearing an expression of pure hilarity. The crowd roared and applauded.

When the parade ended, all the hats gathered in the town square. Topsy rested on a bench. Sunny traded ribbon-compliments with a bonnet from the next street over. The fezzes broke formation and went to find ice cream.

And the small girl's cat sat under a tree with the knitted beanie askew on his head, pretending very hard that he didn't secretly love the whole affair.

That evening, as hats were hung up and parade flags folded away, Millbury settled into its quiet evening rhythms. And somewhere in the town, a little girl was already sketching next year's hat — something magnificent, with feathers.
