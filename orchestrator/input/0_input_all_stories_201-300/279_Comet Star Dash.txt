Once every seventy-three years, a comet named Cass swept through the inner solar system on her long elliptical path, and everyone who happened to be alive and looking at the right part of the sky at the right time got to see her.

Cass didn't think of this as a performance. She thought of it as a commute.

The space trail she traveled was two hundred and fifty years long in total — from the sun to the far edge of the solar system and back — and the portion through the inner system, visible from Earth, was the fast part. A star dash through the warmth.

The sun made her glow.

As Cass approached, its radiation boiled off the ice on her surface — billions of tons of water and carbon dioxide, instantly vaporized, forming a tail that streamed away from the sun for millions of kilometers. This tail — her space trail — was what the watchers on Earth could see.

She couldn't feel it. She was moving too fast and was too old for this to feel like anything other than familiar.

She had seen empires rise and fall during her long orbits. She had passed the Earth when there were no cities anywhere on it. She had been recorded by astronomers using stone instruments, then paper, then glass lenses, then electronic sensors. Each time someone had seen her, they had felt they were seeing something ancient.

They were right.

Cass was very old indeed.

The star dash through the inner system took fourteen months. The far end of her orbit took seventy-two years of freezing dark.

But she didn't mind the dark. The dark was where she thought her long, slow thoughts about orbits and gravity and the great patience required of objects that measure time in centuries.

In seventy-three more years, she would be back.

She always came back.


The watchers on the hilltop saw a fuzzy star move slightly against the fixed ones over the course of several nights, and one of them said it was the most beautiful thing she had ever seen.

Cass had heard this before, across many centuries. She received it without vanity, moving on her long certain path, cold and ancient and reliable as anything in the universe.
