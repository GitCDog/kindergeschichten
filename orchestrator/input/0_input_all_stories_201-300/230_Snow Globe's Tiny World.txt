On the highest shelf of the bedroom, between a stack of old books and a clay pot that held a single dried flower, sat a curious snow globe named Clarence. Inside Clarence's glass belly was a tiny world: a miniature village with three houses, a bridge over a painted river, and one small evergreen tree dusted with what appeared to be eternal snow.

Every day, Clarence watched the room from his high shelf and wondered what went on inside his tiny world when no one was shaking him.

One Thursday afternoon, a girl named Ivy climbed onto her chair, reached up carefully, and lifted Clarence down. She held him at eye level and peered through the glass with one enormous curious eye.

"Hello in there," she said.

Clarence felt the familiar tingle of attention.

Then Ivy lifted him up and gave him one long, slow shake. The snow flew up in a white storm inside the glass — a thousand tiny flakes swirling around the miniature houses and the bridge and the little tree. Shake magic filled the entire tiny world.

Ivy set Clarence on her palm and watched the snow settle.

It fell on the rooftops first, then drifted onto the bridge, then floated down to cover the path between the houses in a soft white layer. Last of all, one final flake settled onto the top of the evergreen tree, where it balanced perfectly.

"It always lands there last," Ivy said softly, as if she had noticed this before.

Clarence thought about his tiny world — the houses that never changed, the river that never moved, the snow that always returned to the same places. Some people might think a world like that was boring. But Clarence knew the truth: there was something deeply comforting about a world where everything always settled gently back to where it belonged.

Ivy carried Clarence to the windowsill where the afternoon light came through. Inside his glass, the snow glowed gold.

She gave him one more small shake — just a gentle one — and watched the flakes rise and fall.

"Good world," she whispered.

Clarence agreed with all his heart.
