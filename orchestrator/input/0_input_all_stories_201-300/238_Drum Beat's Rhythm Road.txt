In the bottom drawer of the music room, nestled between a tambourine and a slightly bent triangle, lived a little drum beat named Boom. Boom was round and tight-skinned and made of cherrywood, and he lived for the moment a pair of sticks touched his surface and set the whole room vibrating.

Today was music march day, and Boom had been taken out and placed at the front of the classroom.

The children assembled their instruments. There were recorders and glockenspiels and shakers and — at the back — one very serious child with a xylophone twice her size. The teacher raised her hands, and the rhythm road began.

Boom led the way. His beat was steady and deep and perfectly even — one two three four, one two three four — and as the other instruments found their footing, they lined up behind his pulse. The recorders found the melody. The shakers added shimmer. The xylophone chimed in on every third beat with something that sounded like sunlight.

The music filled the room and spilled out into the corridor.

A passing teacher stopped outside the door and listened. A janitor further down the hall paused his work and tapped his foot without realizing it. A bird on the windowsill turned its head sideways, listening with one eye.

Music did that, Boom knew. It didn't stay where you put it. It moved.

The music march ended with a final tremendous crash of everything at once, and then silence — the wonderful, ringing kind of silence that only comes right after music stops.

The children looked at each other and grinned.

Boom was warm from the exertion. His sticks were returned carefully to their hooks. The rhythm road he had laid down was gone now, dissolved into the air, but it had been real — everyone in the room had felt it, had moved without meaning to, had breathed a little differently.

That evening, back in the bottom drawer, Boom listened to the building settle around him and kept his own private rhythm — not with sticks, but with the memory of the afternoon.

One two three four. One two three four.
