The two members of the glove pair had been together for three winters, and in that time they had developed a partnership that worked precisely because they were different.

The left glove — whose name was Gin — was adventurous and slightly careless, frequently ending up in pockets wrong-side-out or dropped on the way to school. The right glove — whose name was Reg — was methodical and warm-retaining and always emerged from the wash fully right-side-in.

Together, they were an excellent pair.

Snow day arrived overnight. The children woke to a world gone white and silent, and the call went out: school cancelled, get your coats.

Gin and Reg were pulled from the drawer and pulled onto two cold hands, and then the winter play began.

The snow was perfect packing snow — not too dry, not too wet — and the first snowball was thrown within two minutes of going outside. Gin, on the left hand, was responsible for scooping. Reg, on the right hand, shaped and compressed and aimed.

The snow toss was glorious.

Three children, two dogs, and one thoroughly confused cat were involved at various points. A snowman rose at the garden's edge, wearing a pot for a hat. A snow fort took shape along the wall. Gin and Reg were sodden by midmorning but deeply happy.

At lunch, the gloves were laid on the radiator to dry. They steamed slightly and smelled of cold.

"Good morning," said Reg eventually.

"Best one this year," Gin agreed.

They dried in companionable silence, side by side.

That afternoon, the children went out again. Gin and Reg — warm and dry — met the cold air afresh. The snow had been compacted by the morning's play, and new territory was being opened up along the far wall.

The snow toss recommenced.

Winter play, the glove pair knew, was best done wholeheartedly. There was no half-measure with snow. You were either in it or you weren't, and Gin and Reg were always entirely, enthusiastically in.


When the children came in for dinner, Gin and Reg were peeled off cold red hands and dropped on the doormat without ceremony. They lay there happily, side by side, still damp, still together — a pair that worked best when they worked as one.
