On the morning of the great migration, a little feather named Flynn was left behind. He had come loose from a passing goose somewhere above a meadow and had spiraled gently down to rest on a fence post. Below him, flowers nodded in the breeze. Above him, the whole sky stretched wide and blue.

Flynn was not sad, exactly. He was curious.

A moment later, the wind arrived.

It came first as a whisper, lifting Flynn's edge and tilting him sideways. Then it came as a proper gust, and Flynn was off — launched into the open air on the most wonderful wind ride he had ever imagined.

He turned and tilted and rose and dipped, reading the air the way a sailor reads the sea. Below him the meadow swept past in a blur of green and yellow. A startled rabbit looked up and blinked. A dragonfly veered sharply to avoid a midair collision.

The wind carried Flynn up over a stone wall, across a country lane, and over a pond where his reflection rippled in the dark water below. He had never seen himself from above before. He was small and pale and perfect, he thought.

The wind ride curved and looped and finally, gently, began to ease. Flynn felt the air beneath him soften, and he began the long, slow float downward. This was the soft landing — the most peaceful part of being a feather. No crashing, no bumping. Just a gradual, cushioned settle.

He came to rest on the surface of the pond, where the water held him like a tiny grey boat.

A frog on a lily pad watched him with one large golden eye. "Traveled far?" the frog asked.

"All the way from the fence post," Flynn said.

The frog seemed impressed. "Will you go further?"

Flynn looked at the sky, where clouds moved slowly in the direction the geese had gone. "When the wind comes back," he said, "I will go wherever it takes me."

The frog nodded wisely and dove into the water with a soft plop.

Flynn floated on the still pond, rocking gently with the small ripples, watching the clouds drift overhead. He was a little feather in a very large world, and that, he had decided, was exactly enough.
