In the bottom of the dressing-up box, beneath a velvet cape and a crown that had lost two of its jewels, there lived a magic wand stick named Spark. Spark was a slim piece of smooth birch wood painted silver, with a star glued to one end that had, over years of use, become slightly askew.

Spark had been through thousands of spells.

Not real spells, in the technical sense — Spark was honest about this. He could not make things levitate or transform a toad into a prince. But he had been present for every magical moment that the children of this house had ever decided to create, which amounted to something more interesting than mere levitation.

He had been wielded by a knight, a wizard, a fairy queen, a pirate, and once — memorably — a very young child who had used him as a soup stirrer, which the others had declared a misuse of magical implements.

The light trick was Spark's specialty.

When held up in sunlight, the star on Spark's end caught the light and threw small patterns across the walls — scattered, dancing points that moved when the wand moved. Children who had never believed in anything magical in their lives would sometimes stop when they saw those points of light moving across the ceiling and feel something they didn't have words for yet.

The wand wasn't making the light. That was physics: sunlight, reflection, the geometry of a small tilted star.

But the hand that held the wand was choosing where to point it. And the eye that watched the spell spark dance was deciding, in that moment, that it was worth attending to.

Spark had learned that magic was mostly this: choosing to pay attention to something small and finding, in that attention, something larger.

He rested in the dressing-up box that evening, star slightly askew, silver worn thin at the grip. Ready for tomorrow's spell.


Spark had learned that magic was not a separate thing from the ordinary world. It was the ordinary world, seen with enough attention. He was a stick painted silver, a star glued to one end, made by a child's hands on a rainy afternoon. And he was the vehicle for a thousand imagined adventures.

Both things were equally true.
