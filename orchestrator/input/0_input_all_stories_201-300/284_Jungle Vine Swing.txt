In the part of the garden where the old apple tree grew close to the garden fence and a thick vine had wound its way up both and connected them in a satisfying arc, there was a swing that no one had installed.

It had simply grown there, the vine thickening over years until it was strong enough, and then one summer a child had grabbed hold of it and swung out over the garden bed below, and that had been that.

The vine's name, in the family's informal mythology, was Jules, and Jules had been a jungle vine swing for six summers.

The swing path was fixed by the vine's attachment points: you began on the fence side, grabbed the vine about shoulder height, ran three steps, and launched. The arc carried you out over the flower bed — three feet of air between your boots and the begonias — and then back in a smooth return.

The green ride was different depending on who was doing it.

The father, who was tall, barely left the ground and mostly used it to straighten out his back, which he would not admit. The mother had declined the invitation every summer thus far. The youngest child had learned on Jules and now swung with a confidence that startled visitors.

The oldest child — who was eleven now and had been swinging on Jules since she was five — could launch and angle her weight so that the arc was longer than it should have been, her feet nearly touching the top of the fence on the upswing.

This was years of practice stored in muscle and momentum.

Jules held them all. The vine was old enough to be trusted and still young enough to be flexible, which was the ideal combination for anything that holds people up.

That evening, the swing path hung still in the cooling air. Tomorrow there would be small hands on it again, and the green ride would begin once more.


Jules, the vine, was aware of none of this in words. She was simply a vine that had grown well, in the right place, at the right time, and had become useful. Vines could not take credit for their location. But Jules held firm through every season and every launch and every return, which was the vine equivalent of loyalty.
