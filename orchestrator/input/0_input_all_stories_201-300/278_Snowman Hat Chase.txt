The hat had been there since Tuesday.

It was a green woolly hat with a red bobble, and it had been placed on the snowman's head by a silly child named Max who had run out of the house with it in his excitement to build and had set it on the snowman without really thinking, and then gone back inside, and then forgotten about it entirely.

The snowman — who the family called Gerald — wore the hat with an air of uncomplicated satisfaction.

The hat chase began on Wednesday when Max remembered.

He came outside and stopped on the garden path. "Oh," he said, looking at Gerald.

Gerald looked back with his coal eyes.

"That's my hat," Max said.

Gerald had nothing to say about this.

The problem was that Gerald was taller than Max, and his hat was at the very top. Max tried jumping. He tried a stick. He tried asking his older sister Jess, who declined. He tried stacking two garden pots, which was not successful.

By this point the hat chase had attracted an audience: Jess, their father, and the dog, who had come outside for reasons unrelated to hat logistics.

"Why didn't you just use a different hat?" Jess asked.

"This is my best hat," Max said.

The winter wind arrived at exactly this moment and lifted the hat off Gerald's head. It sailed upward in a lovely arc over the garden and landed at the far end of the lawn.

Max sprinted.

He reached the hat before the dog did, which required impressive speed. He returned wearing it, slightly breathless.

Gerald stood bare-headed in the garden with the expression of a snowman who had temporarily held something important and was now at peace with having returned it.

The hat chase was over. Max's ears were warm. The winter had delivered an unexpected assist.


Max went back inside and reappeared with a different hat — an orange one — and went back out to place it on Gerald. Because Gerald had stood hatless for several minutes, and that seemed unfair.

Gerald received the orange hat with the same uncomplicated dignity he had worn the green one. He was not particular about hats. He was a snowman, and winter was long, and he had all the patience in the world.
