In the village of Porthenny, where the lanes were narrow and the nights came early, there was a magic lantern named Luma who had hung at the corner of Harbor Lane and Cliff Road for longer than anyone could remember. Her glass panels were amber, so her light fell warm and golden, and her flame burned with a steadiness that made people feel safer just by seeing it.

Every evening as dusk folded in from the sea, Luma would glow to life. She was a night guide — not just for feet, but for hearts.

The fishermen who came home late from the water would look up for Luma's amber glow path first. It meant the village was still there. It meant they were home. One old fisherman named Carew said he had never once come home in thirty years without spotting Luma first through the sea mist, and the sight always made him breathe easier.

Children walking home from evening lessons would stay in Luma's pool of light as long as they could before the darkness of the side lanes reached for them. They didn't run between streetlamps — they walked between them, stepping from one safe circle of warmth to the next.

One winter, a great fog settled over the village for five days and nights. The sea disappeared. The other streetlamps vanished into white. But Luma — standing at the highest point of Harbor Lane — sent her amber warmth out into the fog and people navigated by her alone.

A lost traveler who had missed the road in the fog heard stories afterward about a lamp that glowed like a warming ember, and said: "I thought I had conjured it in my panic. It was too golden to be real. But I followed it, and it brought me straight to the inn."

Luma had not moved an inch.

On clear nights, she watched the stars. On stormy nights, she watched the rain. On fog nights, she concentrated her flame and pushed her glow path out as far as it would go.

She was not the largest light in the world. But along one small lane in one small village, she was the most important one. And that was exactly enough.
