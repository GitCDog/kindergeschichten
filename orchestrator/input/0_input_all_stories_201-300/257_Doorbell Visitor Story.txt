Ding-dong. Ding-dong.

That was the voice of Bella the doorbell, and Bella had been speaking those two notes for twenty-two years from her position on the door frame of number fourteen Primrose Street. She was white and oval and slightly yellowed by sun, and she took tremendous interest in the visitor tale that unfolded each time she was pressed.

She could tell a great deal from how she was pressed.

A firm, single press was usually the postal worker or a confident adult. Two quick presses close together suggested impatience or a child. A single gentle press — the kind where the finger barely touched her — was almost always someone nervous: a new neighbor introducing themselves, a child selling raffle tickets, a person not quite sure if they had the right house.

Once, a very small hand pressed her so softly that Bella wasn't sure the ring tone had even sounded, and when the door opened, a four-year-old stood there holding a dandelion.

"For you," the child had said to the startled woman inside.

Bella had replayed this visitor story many times since. It was one of her favorites.

There was the ring tone of delivery day, which came with a rush of anticipation from inside the house. There was the ring tone of birthday guests, arriving in clusters of two and three, each press slightly more festive than the last. There was the ring tone of old friends who hadn't visited in years and pressed her just a little longer than necessary, as if savoring the act of arriving.

And there was the quiet ring tone of evening, when the children had been brought inside and the street had gone still, and someone pressed Bella on their way home from a late walk because they liked the sound of it.

Bella rang each time with the same two notes: ding-dong.

But what happened next was always different.

The door would open. Someone new or familiar would be there. A story would begin.


Bella could not answer when she was pressed. She could only ring. But in her twenty-two years on the doorframe of number fourteen, she had learned that the ring itself was enough — that sound carried across the threshold and said, simply: someone is here. And someone inside would come.
