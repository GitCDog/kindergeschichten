High on the mainmast of the toy ship named Valor, which stood on the shelf in the playroom between a globe and a wooden castle, there flew a brave pirate flag named Black.

Black was a small rectangle of dark fabric — actually deep navy, not truly black, because the craft shop had been out of black felt the day the flag was made — with a skull and crossbones stitched onto her in white thread. The stitching was not perfectly even. The skull leaned slightly to the left. The crossbones were more X-shaped than parallel.

She was, the child who made her said, "scary enough."

The wind roar happened whenever the playroom window was open and a breeze found its way to the shelf. Black would snap to attention, her fabric filling with air and crackling with a sound that was, in the acoustics of a small room, remarkably convincing.

The ship mast held her steady.

The toy ship Valor had a crew of twelve wooden figures, hand-painted by the same child who had stitched Black's skull. They stood in various positions: two on the bow, three on the rigging, one at the wheel with a painted expression of great concentration, and the rest distributed across the deck in attitudes of seamanship.

Under Black's wind-snapping authority, the crew held their positions.

When the window was closed, Black hung limp. The skull and slightly-left-leaning crossbones faced the room without drama. But when the wind came — when the playroom filled with the sound of outside air, and Valor creaked slightly on her shelf, and the globe next to her turned a degree or two — Black roared.

She was not a real pirate flag, on a real ship, on a real ocean. But she was the most real thing in the playroom when the wind blew through.

And in a child's imagination, that was entirely sufficient.


The child who had made Black took her down once — just once — and restitched the skull's leftward lean into an upright position. Then reconsidered. Then put the lean back, because the imperfection was part of what made her Black, and not a different flag entirely.

She was restored to the mast with the skull leaning left, as it had always done, and the wind, when it came, didn't notice.
