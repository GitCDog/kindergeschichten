At the bottom of an old wooden box, under a tangle of old shoelaces and a broken watch, there lived a curious key named Clink. Clink was made of brass, with a pattern of tiny leaves along his bow and a perfectly cut blade that had not fit anything in years.

He spent his days wondering what he unlocked.

Every key belonged to something — that was the fundamental belief that Clink held close. Every cut was a question, and somewhere out in the world was the answer. He simply had not found it yet.

One afternoon, a girl named Mira was helping her grandmother clear out the wooden box. She reached in and lifted Clink out into the light, turning him over in her fingers.

"Where does this go?" she asked.

Grandmother tilted her head. "I lost door somewhere," she said slowly. "There is a door in the garden shed I haven't been able to open for twenty years."

Mira's eyes went wide. A secret lock. A lost door. A key in her hand. The story was practically writing itself.

They walked to the garden shed together, Mira holding Clink carefully as though he were fragile — though brass keys are not fragile at all, just patient. At the back of the shed, half-hidden behind a rose that had grown across it, was a small low door set into the stone wall.

Mira crouched down and slid Clink into the lock.

He fit.

She turned him, and felt the tumblers move — one, two, three — and then a click so satisfying it seemed to echo in the stone.

The door swung inward.

Beyond it was a small garden room, entirely hidden behind the wall — stone-floored, open to the sky above, with an old bench and a climbing rose and a bird bath filled with rainwater. A robin sat on the edge of the bath and regarded them without alarm.

Grandmother pressed her hands to her face. "I had forgotten," she whispered. "This was my favorite place."

Clink gleamed in the afternoon light, his secret lock fulfilled at last.

Some keys wait a long time. But they are always, always right to wait.
