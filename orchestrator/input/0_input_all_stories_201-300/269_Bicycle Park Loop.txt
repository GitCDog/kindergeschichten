Blue was a friendly bicycle — blue-framed, as her name suggested, with silver handlebars and a basket on the front that could hold a library book, a water bottle, and a small dog if the dog was willing to sit still, which the small dog sometimes wasn't.

Her owner was a woman named June, and every Saturday morning they rode the park loop together.

The park loop was not a long route — exactly two kilometers of path that wound through the park's various sections: the rose garden, the duck pond, the bandstand, the sports fields, and back to the main gate. One circuit took about twelve minutes at a comfortable pace.

June and Blue did it three times.

The first loop was always the quietest — the park still damp from the night, the paths nearly empty, the roses just beginning to catch the morning light. Blue's wheels made a soft hiss on the path, and the basket held the water bottle and the library book and sometimes a piece of toast wrapped in a paper bag.

The second loop was when the park started to fill. Dog walkers. Joggers. A man who brought a folding chair to the bandstand every Saturday and read a newspaper with great deliberateness.

The third loop was Blue's favorite. By then, June had warmed up and they went a little faster, taking the corner by the duck pond at a gentle lean, the air moving past them in a clean rush. June would sometimes hum a little. The ducks never looked up from the water; they were used to Blue by now.

The road ride ended at the main gate.

June would lock Blue to the rack — always the same rack, second from the left — and go inside for tea.

Blue waited in the sun, her basket empty, her frame warm from the morning. The park went on around her: feet and wheels and dogs and the distant cry of gulls over the playing field.

Next Saturday was already quietly waiting.


June always unlocked Blue without looking — her hands knew the motion the way they knew most familiar things. Three years of Saturdays had built that into her fingers.

Blue didn't mind being left at the rack. She was warm from the ride and perfectly positioned to watch the park go on without her. It always would, and she would always be back.
