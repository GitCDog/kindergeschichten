On a clear Saturday morning, a curious chalk named Blanche was lifted from the box and carried outside. The sidewalk stretched before her — a wide, flat, grey expanse of nothing, just waiting to become something.

The hand belonged to a girl named Rosa, and Rosa had plans.

"We're going to make a city," Rosa told Blanche.

The color street art began at the garden gate. Rosa drew a road first — a broad stripe of grey chalk (she had other colors too, but started with grey to honor the sidewalk's own nature). Then buildings rose along the sides: tall thin ones, short wide ones, one with a round window, one with a flat roof where a chalk pigeon sat looking philosophical.

Blanche moved across the concrete in long swoops and short precise strokes. She loved the texture of the sidewalk — rougher than paper, more resistant, but in a friendly way. She could feel each pebble grain through her length.

The city grew.

Rosa added a park with chalk trees and a chalk pond where chalk ducks floated in a circle. She drew a bakery with bread loaves in the window and a bicycle propped against the wall. She drew a small chalk cat sitting on a chalk step outside a chalk house.

A neighbor came to look.

"Can I draw something?" the neighbor's son asked.

"Yes," said Rosa, "you can do that building on the corner."

The boy drew a rocket ship on the corner building. "It launches from the roof," he explained.

Rosa considered this. "The city has a space program," she agreed.

By afternoon, half the block had gathered. Someone drew a dragon flying over the park. Someone else added a submarine in the chalk pond. The city had grown impossible and wonderful and entirely its own.

That evening, rain came and washed the color street art away.

But the sidewalk kept a ghost of it — a faint impression in the grey concrete — and Blanche rested in her box knowing she would make another world tomorrow.


Blanche had made worlds before — quiet ones, careful ones — but this city had been made by many hands, and it was better for it. She rested in the box that evening knowing she had not just left a mark on the pavement. She had started something that other people had continued.
