On the dresser in the girl's room, among the hairclips and the small clay bowl full of rubber bands and the photograph from the seaside, there was a tiny music box named Tune.

Tune was oval, painted pale pink, and when the little key on her back was wound, a small mechanism inside began to turn and a soft tune played. The tune was twelve notes long and repeated for as long as the spring held tension, slowing gradually as it wound down, the notes stretching and softening until the last ones were almost too slow to be music.

Then silence.

On Tune's lid was a small dancer — a brass figure, no taller than a thumb, that turned on a spindle in time with the music. She had one arm raised and one foot lifted, permanently mid-spin, permanently in the position of someone who has just caught the rhythm.

The soft tune was the same every time. It had no name that anyone knew, though the girl called it the morning song because she wound Tune every morning after waking, while she was still in bed. The music and the light coming through the curtains and the smell of breakfast from downstairs had become, over years, the same thing — they arrived together and meant the same thing: the day was beginning, and it was safe to begin.

The spin dance of the brass figure caught the morning light.

Twelve notes. Repeat. Slowing. The spring reaching its end.

The girl lay on her side and watched the dancer turn, feeling the music in the room around her without quite listening to it — the way you don't listen to birdsong, but feel better for it being there.

The last note stretched and softened.

Then Tune was quiet, the dancer still mid-spin, waiting to be wound again tomorrow.


The girl reached up and wound Tune again. The spring held. The soft tune began from the start, and the brass dancer turned as she always had, mid-spin, permanently in the moment just before the music and the movement settle into something known.

Tune played until the spring ran down, and the room was quiet, and the morning had properly begun.
