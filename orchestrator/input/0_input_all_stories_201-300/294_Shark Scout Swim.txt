Scout was not a fierce shark. She was a brave shark, which is a different thing entirely — brave being the quality of doing necessary things despite the difficulty, whereas fierce is more about teeth, which Scout certainly had but preferred not to make a fuss about.

Scout was a reef shark, three years old, and her deep sea territory covered approximately two square kilometers of the reef shelf below the drop-off. She knew every coral head, every cleaning station, every current.

The swift swim was her best skill.

On the open-water side of the reef, the current ran fast and Scout would use it — not fighting it but reading it, finding the channels where it accelerated and riding those, covering distance with minimal effort. Other fish scattered at her approach, which she had made her peace with. She didn't take it personally.

She was scouting.

The scout swim had practical purposes: she was checking the reef's condition, noting where the fish populations were concentrating, updating her mental map. This was the deep sea equivalent of a morning commute combined with a daily inspection.

One morning she encountered something unusual near the drop-off.

A diver — a human in a black wetsuit, equipped with fins and a camera, hovering in the water with the particular careful stillness of someone who very much hoped Scout would come closer.

Scout considered the situation.

The diver was stationary, which was the correct behavior. Scout made one slow pass at a distance of four meters — enough to assess, not enough to alarm. The diver did not move. The camera made a small click.

Scout banked left and continued her scout swim.

Behind her, the diver surfaced and said to the person in the boat: "She looked right at me."

Scout was two hundred meters away by then, already on the next stage of her route. She had noted the diver and filed the encounter.

The reef was normal. The morning was good.


Scout completed her morning route and arrived back at the reef's center by mid-morning. She had logged everything: the fish concentrations, the temperature change at the drop-off edge, the diver who had behaved correctly.

She settled into the mild current above the coral and let the water move over her gills. The reef was well. The deep sea was as she had left it. All was as it should be.
