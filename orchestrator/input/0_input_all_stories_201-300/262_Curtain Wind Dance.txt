In the bedroom at the end of the hall, where the window faced west and caught the afternoon light perfectly, there lived a pair of curtains named Cora and Cloud. They were pale blue — nearly white in full sun — and they were made of a very light cotton that responded to the slightest breath of air.

The wind dance happened on the days when the window was left open.

Cora would feel the first movement of air and stir — a slow lean into the room, her hem rising from the floor in a smooth curve. Cloud, beside her, would follow: not quite the same movement, not quite the same timing. They were a pair, but each curtain had her own way.

On busy wind days, the window dance was dramatic. Both curtains would fly up toward the ceiling, billow outward into the room, then fall back again. Then fly up again. There was a rhythm to it — irregular but satisfying — that the family would sometimes pause to watch.

A cat named Felix had been startled by the curtain movement twice and now regarded Cora and Cloud with deep personal suspicion from the doorway.

On still days, there was no dance. Cora and Cloud hung straight and quiet, barely touching the air. These days were peaceful but less interesting. The wind was their partner, and without it they were only fabric.

But on soft evenings — the best kind — the breeze came through the window in long, slow waves that moved the curtains in a gentle, unhurried undulation. Not flying, not dramatic. Just a deep inhale and exhale of white cotton in low golden light.

The child who slept in this room loved to fall asleep watching it.

Cora and Cloud would continue their wind play long after the eyes on the bed had closed, the gentle movement becoming part of the room's breathing, inseparable from the sounds of evening — distant traffic, a bird, the settling of the house.

They danced all night, without an audience, because the dancing was its own reward.
