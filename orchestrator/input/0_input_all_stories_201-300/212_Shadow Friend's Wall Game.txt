On the wall beside the window, when the bedside lamp was on and the curtains were closed, a little shadow friend named Dim would appear. Dim was made entirely of darkness — a soft, quiet shape that stretched and shrank depending on where the light fell.

Most shadows just copy whatever moves in front of the light. But Dim was different. Dim had ideas of his own.

Every night, just before sleep, the child in the bed would hold up her hands and make shadow animals on the wall. A rabbit with two fingers for ears. A dog with a floppy thumb. A bird that could actually flutter its wings. Dim would watch each one carefully, then, very slowly, begin to play his own light game.

He would stretch himself into shapes the child's hands hadn't made — a whale, a dancing bear, a tiny elf running across the wall world. The child would gasp with delight and try to copy him back.

One evening, Dim showed her something special. He stretched himself very wide and very thin until he covered the whole wall — and in the middle, he made the shape of a night sky, with a tiny flickering star in the centre.

"How did you do that?" the child whispered.

Dim didn't answer — shadows can't talk. But he held the shape for a long, peaceful moment while the child looked at the star. It didn't flicker the way real stars do. It held still, steady and warm.

Then the child turned off the lamp, and Dim disappeared into the general darkness of the room, where all shadows go when the light goes out. But the child kept the image of that little star in her memory as she drifted toward sleep.

Every night after that, she looked forward to seeing what shape Dim would make next. The wall world felt like a secret they shared, just the two of them, in the quiet before dreams.

The child told her mother about Dim, and her mother smiled and said it was just the way shadows moved in the light. But every evening, when the lamp went on, the child would look at the wall and wait. And every evening, Dim would be there, ready for the next game. Some friendships do not need words. Some of the very best ones are made entirely of light and dark and the quiet space between.

[pause] Good night my little friend
