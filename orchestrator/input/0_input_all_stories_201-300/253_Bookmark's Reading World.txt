In the novel on the nightstand, there lived a tiny bookmark named Page. Page was a strip of blue ribbon sewn to the binding — not a loose bookmark that could fall out or be used elsewhere, but a permanent resident of the book, attached at the top and living inside it always.

Page had been through every chapter.

This was the great privilege of being a bookmark: you were present for all of it. While a reader might skim or skip or leave a chapter for later, Page was always there — pressed between the pages, feeling the words on either side of her, holding the exact spot.

The page saver.

She had been in this book for four reading months, which was how Page measured time: not in days, but in sessions. Twenty-minute reading worlds before bed. Stolen twenty minutes at lunch. One long Sunday afternoon session that had moved her forty pages in one go.

The story she held was about a girl who traveled to the moon. Page had been the first to know what was on each page — she had pressed against it before the reader's eyes arrived, had felt the weight of the words in the dark between readings.

She knew the ending. The girl came back from the moon carrying a small stone that glowed faintly in the dark. She gave it to her grandmother. The end.

Page held her current position — chapter seventeen, page two hundred and twelve — and waited for tonight's session. The reader was getting close now. Page could feel it in the way the chapters were coming faster, the sessions longer.

Tonight the girl would reach the moon.

Page settled gently between the pages and thought about what it meant to hold a story in place. Not to tell it, not to write it — just to mark the exact spot where reading had paused, so it could begin again without being lost.

Reading worlds didn't end when the light went off. They waited, held by a blue ribbon, for the light to come back.
