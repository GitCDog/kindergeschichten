Older than the coral reef she rested on, slower than every other creature in the shallows, and more patient than weather, there was a sea turtle named Shore who had been making the same journey for forty-seven years.

Shore was a curious turtle — not curious in the sense of constantly questioning, but curious in the sense of attending. She noticed things. The temperature of the current. The angle of the light through the water. The particular quality of the ocean ride at different depths.

Her shell was a map of her history.

Every mark on Shore's carapace told a story: the long pale scratch from a boat propeller she had not heard in time (she had learned to hear them better after that). The barnacle colonies on her right side, which she allowed because they were not troublesome. The chip near the rear that had happened in a way she no longer fully remembered.

The slow travel was not a disadvantage. It was her nature.

She had crossed the ocean four times in her life and would likely cross it twice more before the end. She knew the ocean the way only slow travelers know it — in detail, in depth, in the way each stretch of deep water has its own character.

Fast things moved through the ocean and saw its surface. Shore moved with it and knew what was underneath.

The ocean ride today was warm and clear, the visibility good, a mild current running in the direction she wanted to go. Shore adjusted her angle slightly and let the current help. She was heading for the feeding grounds she had visited every second spring for twenty years.

She knew where they were without thinking about it. The knowing was in her body, in the same deep place that told her when to surface and when to dive and how far the shore was.

Tonight she would float near the surface and breathe the night air.


Shore had outlived many faster things. She had survived two warm years that had bleached the reef and come back to find it slowly recovering. She had navigated oceans in which the slow travel was, paradoxically, the most efficient path.

She surfaced in the night and breathed the cool air and looked at the stars, which she navigated by, as her kind had done for millions of years. Then dove again. The feeding grounds were close now.
