Sable was a magic paintbrush — sable-haired, as the best brushes are, with a perfect tip that came to a fine point when wet and held its shape through an entire painting session. She had been part of the art studio for eleven years and had made, by her own loose count, somewhere between five hundred and a thousand pieces of work.

The shape world was what Sable thought about when she was resting in the jar.

Every painting was a shape world. The painter — a woman named Greta — would begin by blocking in large forms: rectangles of color, ovals, curves. These were the bones of the piece, rough and without detail. Then Sable would move in smaller circles, building the surface, and the shapes would become more specific: leaves, faces, the curve of a shoulder, the particular way light fell on a piece of glass.

Art magic happened in the space between the large shape and the small one.

Today Greta was painting a still life — a bowl of fruit with a cloth folded beside it, and behind everything a wall of faded blue. Sable moved from paint to canvas and back, measuring color, building form.

The apples were hardest. Apples looked simple — round, red — but their surface was not uniform. Light fell differently on each side. The shadow underneath was not black but a deep violet-blue. The highlight was almost white. Sable mixed and mixed, testing each color on the corner of the canvas, until the apple began to look like itself.

"There," Greta would say sometimes, when a passage suddenly worked.

Sable knew the feeling. It was the moment when the shape world aligned with the actual world — when the flat paint stopped being paint and became, briefly, a window.

She was rinsed carefully at the end of each session, her hairs combed back to a point, left in the jar with the handle up.

Ready for tomorrow's shape world.


Greta cleaned Sable with warm water and a small bar of soap, working the paint out of the hairs from the ferrule down. She did this carefully, the way you care for something useful and trusted. Sable came back to a clean point, ready.

The shape world of tomorrow's canvas was already waiting, blank and full of possibility.
