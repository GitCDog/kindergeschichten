At the far end of the school desk, standing in a chipped ceramic mug alongside a family of pens and a rather snobbish ruler, lived a curious pencil tip named Pip. Pip was small and sharp and absolutely brimming with ideas.

Every morning, Pip waited eagerly for a hand to reach into the mug and choose her. When the hand belonged to Milo — a boy with messy dark hair and a mind that never stopped spinning — Pip knew something extraordinary was about to happen.

One Tuesday, Milo sat down at his school desk with a completely blank piece of paper in front of him and a homework assignment that read: "Invent something new."

Milo stared at the paper. The paper stared back. Pip could feel Milo's uncertainty traveling all the way down through his fingers.

"Come on," Pip whispered, though Milo could not hear her. "Let me help."

Milo tapped Pip lightly against the paper — once, twice — and then something wonderful happened. An idea spark flew! It wasn't visible to anyone but Pip, but she felt it crackle like a tiny bolt of electricity from the tip of her graphite straight onto the page.

Milo's hand began to move. First a wobbly circle. Then two wheels. Then a little basket on top.

"A bicycle that carries a garden!" Milo murmured, leaning forward with shining eyes.

Pip danced across the page — lines became spokes, spokes became wheels, the basket filled with tiny sketched flowers and a miniature watering can. The idea spark had become an idea fire, and it lit up the whole school desk with its warmth.

By the time the school bell rang, Milo had three full pages of drawings and notes, and Pip's tip was worn down to a soft, rounded nub.

Milo held her up and looked at her. "I think I need to sharpen you," he said, and he sounded genuinely grateful.

As Pip spun inside the little pencil sharpener, she felt herself grow sharp and ready again. A curious pencil tip's work was never finished, after all. There were blank pages waiting all over the world, full of problems that needed solving and stories that needed telling.

And every single one of them needed just one tiny idea spark to begin.
