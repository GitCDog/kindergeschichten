Nell was a friendly blanket — soft and wide, woven in stripes of cream and blue, with a fringe that tickled the floor when she was spread out flat. She had been in the family for a very long time, passed from grandmother to mother to child, and she carried warmth in every thread.

Tonight was a star nap night.

Star nap nights happened when the sky was perfectly clear and the child who owned Nell — a boy named Sam — dragged his pillow out to the back garden and lay on the grass to look at the sky. And whenever Sam went somewhere to look at the sky, Nell went too.

Sam arranged her on the grass with great care, smoothing her edges and fluffing her center, then lay down in the middle and pulled her up to his chin. The cozy night wrapped around them both — cool on Sam's face, warm everywhere else.

Above them, the sky was extraordinary. The stars were so numerous that they seemed to be spilling over each other, pressed close in the dark blue, more than any one person could ever count.

"That one," Sam said, pointing to a bright star near the horizon, "is mine."

Nell didn't know which one he meant, but she believed him.

They lay there for a long time, Sam's breathing growing slower and deeper, Nell keeping him perfectly warm against the cool ground. One by one, Sam's thoughts seemed to drift upward to join the stars — questions about space and time and whether any of those tiny lights had anyone looking back.

Nell held him steady while he thought.

A moth landed on her fringe and rested for a moment before moving on. A cat from next door passed through the garden and paused to look at the strange arrangement of boy and blanket on the grass before deciding it was none of her business.

The stars turned slowly overhead.

At some point, Sam's eyes closed and his breathing deepened into sleep. Nell kept the warmth in and the dew out, as she always had, as she had done for Sam's mother and her mother before.

Above them, the star nap night held its quiet watch.
