On the wall beside the bed, at the exact height reachable by a six-year-old's arm, there was a collection of stickers that had grown over three years into something that defied easy description.

It had begun with a single curious sticker — a round one with a yellow sun — placed there by a child named Mia the morning after she learned that stickers could go on walls. From that moment, the wall trip had been underway.

The collection now included: fourteen star stickers in four sizes, a rainbow (foil), seven animals including a very large elephant who anchored the top-left corner, three vehicles, two flowers, one improbable dinosaur, a slice of pizza, something that might have been a rocket or a very determined carrot, and a collection of small smiley faces in the bottom corner that Mia had applied during a particularly enthusiastic afternoon.

Each sticker was a travel mark from a different moment.

The star cluster on the right came from a packet given by a dentist, which was unusual since dentist visits were generally not celebrated. The elephant was a birthday present from an aunt who had good taste in stickers. The pizza had come from a birthday party and been considered too important to put anywhere except the wall.

When Mia had friends come to stay, the sticker wall was always part of the tour.

"That one," she would say, pointing to the foil rainbow, "is the best one."

"What about that?" her friend would say, pointing at the possible-rocket/possible-carrot.

"That's complicated," Mia would say, in a way that discouraged further questions.

The wall itself seemed perfectly content with its collection. Walls don't mind stickers the way some people say they do — or at least, this wall seemed to take a certain satisfaction in the accumulated evidence of a child's enthusiasms, one small moment of joy pressed flat and kept.

Mia fell asleep facing the sticker wall every night, her travel marks glowing faintly in the nightlight.


The sticker wall was not just a wall. It was a map of enthusiasms — small, flat, permanent. Mia added to it slowly, with the seriousness of a curator. Each sticker placed was a decision, and once decided, the wall held it forever.

That, in the end, was the best thing about stickers. They didn't ask to be explained. They just stayed.
