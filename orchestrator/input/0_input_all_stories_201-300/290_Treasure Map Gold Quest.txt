The curious treasure map had been drawn on old brown paper — really just ordinary paper soaked in tea and dried in the oven, which the children had read about in a book and attempted with great concentration.

The result was convincing. The paper felt crinkled and antique. The X mark had been drawn in red marker. The coastline was wiggly in the right way. There was a small skull and crossbones in one corner (slightly lopsided, as skulls and crossbones often were in homemade contexts) and a compass rose that was oriented correctly because the map-maker had checked twice.

The hidden gold quest had been organized over three days.

It began with the map itself. Then the code written in lemon juice on a separate sheet, which had to be held near a lamp to become visible. Then the first clue, left under the kitchen clock, which pointed to the second clue, left inside a wellying boot in the hallway.

The third clue was the longest and the best: a poem, of sorts, that rhymed imperfectly but described the location of the treasure clearly enough to be solved.

The treasure itself — the hidden gold — was a tin of golden foil-wrapped chocolate coins, buried shallowly in the garden in a biscuit tin.

Five children followed the X mark.

They dug with the small garden trowel and found the tin eighteen centimeters down, which was just deep enough for authenticity.

The coins were divided exactly, which required some complex arithmetic that was done seriously and correctly.

That evening, five children ate golden chocolate and the treasure map lay on the kitchen table, slightly more crinkled than it had started, with a small muddy thumbprint on the compass rose.

Evidence of a proper quest.


Fen, who had helped organize the quest, wrote everything down in a notebook: the design of the map, the codes used, the number of clues, what worked and what was confusing. She had already begun planning the next one for a different occasion.

The hidden gold tin was washed and placed on the shelf. It would be reused. Good treasure containers deserved second quests.
