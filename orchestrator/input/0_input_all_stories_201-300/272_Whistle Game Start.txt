The brave whistle blower was a short whistle with a chrome barrel and a plastic mouthpiece, and she belonged to a referee named Pat. Every Saturday, she went to the sports field in Pat's jacket pocket, and when the moment came, she did the thing she was made to do.

The game start.

There was nothing else quite like it. The two teams faced each other, twelve players a side, studded boots on wet grass, breath making small clouds in the cold morning air. Everyone waiting. The energy of the match coiled tight and ready.

Pat raised the whistle to his lips. The whole field held one collective breath.

Then the team call split the morning: one sharp blast from the chrome barrel, clear and carrying.

And the game began.

The whistle directed the whole match from then on. She called fouls, halftime, goal kicks, boundary calls. She was the voice of the game's rules — not Pat's opinion, not the crowd's wish, but the objective call, made from the field's center, impartial and immediate.

Good whistling was precise. One blast meant stop. Two meant something different. The length of the blast, the sharpness of it, carried information that the players learned to read as easily as language.

At halftime, the teams gathered in their huddles and Pat stood alone at the center circle with the whistle hanging on its lanyard. The field was quiet except for the wind and the distant sound of another match on the next pitch over.

Pat turned the whistle in his fingers. Twelve years of Saturdays. Hundreds of game starts. Thousands of calls. The chrome was worn smooth in the spot where his thumb rested.

Second half began.

One sharp blast. Clean and certain.

The teams ran toward each other, and the match was alive again.


After the final whistle — two long blasts, match over — Pat walked off the field with the teams, the chrome barrel warm from his hand. Both teams were muddy. The score had been close.

He tucked the whistle back into his pocket and drove home through the Saturday morning, ready for next week's game start.
