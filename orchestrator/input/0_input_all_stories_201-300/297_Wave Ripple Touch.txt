At the edge of the harbor, where the water was still and the boats rocked gently at their moorings, a tiny wave ripple named Rip was completing a journey that had begun seven hundred meters away.

Rip had been born when a boat had moved through the harbor mouth. The boat's hull had displaced water in two directions, and the displaced water had pushed outward in a widening circle, thinning as it spread, softening as it traveled. By the time Rip reached the harbor wall, she was not much — a gentle rise and fall of perhaps two centimeters.

The sea motion had carried her here. Now she would arrive.

Shore touch was what Rip had been moving toward since her creation, without being able to think about it or plan for it. She was water moving through water, a pattern of energy in a medium that held it temporarily and passed it along.

She reached the harbor wall.

The stone was old and dark with salt and algae, carved by centuries of exactly this: waves arriving, touching, reflecting back into the water. Rip met the wall and her energy was returned — a small reflected ripple heading back the way she had come, already diminishing.

A child sitting on the harbor edge was dangling his feet above the water and watching the surface. He had been watching for five minutes, noticing the way the water moved without apparent cause — small surges, tiny oscillations, the constant gentle motion of the harbor surface.

He saw Rip arrive at the wall. He didn't know that was what he was seeing. He saw a small ripple touch the stone and reflect.

"The water never stops moving," he said to his grandmother.

"No," she said. "Even when it looks still."

He thought about this. Rip's reflected ripple moved back through the harbor, already too small to see.


The child pulled his feet up and looked at the harbor for another minute.

"Is the water resting?" he asked.

"Never," his grandmother said. "But it knows how to be quiet about the motion. That's different from resting."

He considered this. The harbor surface continued its small, continuous movement — never still, never loud. Sea motion held without announcement.
