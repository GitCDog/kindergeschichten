When the lights went out at bedtime, the little flashlight beam search began.

It was a tradition in the bedroom. Oliver would lie in bed, the room dark except for the glow of the streetlight through the curtain, and he would hold his small flashlight above the covers and begin.

The flashlight was named Ray, and Ray's beam was precise and round and could reach from Oliver's bed to the far wall in one clean line.

The night search started with the ceiling.

Ray illuminated it square by square — the corner near the door, where a spider sometimes built a temporary web; the patch above the window where a shadow always gathered; the center, where the light fitting hung, a plain circle of white plastic.

Nothing unusual. Report noted.

Then the walls. Left wall: bookshelf, books in tidy rows, a poster of a blue whale with size reference markings. Right wall: wardrobe, door slightly open, the hanging coats visible through the gap.

Ray paused at the slightly open wardrobe door.

Oliver had learned, through systematic nightly investigation, that what he feared might be inside wardrobes was never actually inside wardrobes. But the investigation was still worthwhile, because knowing required looking.

He pointed Ray directly into the gap. Coats. A pair of trainers on the wardrobe floor. A jumper that had fallen off its hanger. The hidden toy he had been looking for for three days — a small wooden frog — was visible right at the back.

Success.

Oliver got out of bed, retrieved the wooden frog, and brought it back under the covers. Ray swung one more time across the ceiling, the night search complete.

All clear.

Oliver turned Ray off and settled into the dark. The wooden frog fit perfectly in his palm. The streetlight made its quiet pattern on the curtain.

The night search was done. The room was safe. And tomorrow, Ray would be fully charged and ready for another investigation.


The wooden frog sat on Oliver's pillow from that night onward, reliably there every bedtime. And every night, Ray conducted his careful investigation — not because the room was dangerous, but because looking carefully at ordinary things was its own quiet kind of comfort.
