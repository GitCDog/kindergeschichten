Axle was a brave skateboard who had seen the park in every season — from wet autumn leaves that made the concrete treacherous, to summer when the whole park smelled of warm tarmac and sunscreen. He knew every crack in the rink, every bump on the half-pipe approach, every spot where the wheel spin worked best.

His owner was a boy named Carlos, who had been riding for two years and was getting very, very good.

Today Carlos arrived at the park with something new in mind.

He stood at the top of the half-pipe for a long time. Axle waited under his feet, four wheels ready. Carlos had dropped in dozens of times — it was no longer frightening. But today he was trying something at the bottom: a revert. A rotation of the board at the apex of the opposite wall.

He dropped in.

The wheel spin sang against the smooth surface as they rolled down, picking up speed in a clean rush that Axle loved above all things. Carlos crouched low, and they hit the bottom and swept up the opposite wall — and at the top, Carlos shifted his weight and the board spun beneath him, a full one-eighty, and they came back down rolling in the opposite direction.

Clean. Perfectly clean.

Carlos whooped. He didn't usually whoop. It was a trick worth whooping over.

He did it again. And again. The third time, a slightly older skater watched from the side and nodded with genuine appreciation.

"Nice revert," the older skater said.

Carlos said thank you and tried to look like this was normal.

Axle felt the park tricks settling into his muscle memory — not his muscles, exactly, but the grain of his deck and the calibration of his wheels, adjusted by thousands of small corrections over two years of riding. He was not just a board. He was the record of every attempt Carlos had made, including the ones that ended on the ground.

This one had not ended on the ground.


Axle felt the heat of the afternoon in his grip tape and the particular looseness of his trucks after a session well-skated. He was not new. He was not fresh from the factory. He was experienced — and in the world of skateboards, experience lived in every scratch and scuff and adjusted bolt.
