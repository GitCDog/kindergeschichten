On the back of the door in the hallway hung a cheerful button coat named Chester. Chester was deep red wool, with five large brass buttons running down the front and a stitched pattern of leaves along the collar that had been done by hand many years ago. He was old and well-loved and slightly moth-nibbled at the right sleeve, and he considered this all deeply dignified.

Chester's favorite time of day was morning, when someone reached for him.

Each button told a story, if you knew how to listen. The top button had been replaced twice — it was slightly different in color from the others, a brighter gold, and it came from a different coat entirely, given by a neighbor who had an exact match. This button had traveled, in other words, and it carried the warmth of two different coats.

The second button had been re-sewn one winter by an eight-year-old who had just learned to stitch. The thread was doubled and slightly crooked, and the button sat at a very faint angle — but it had held for nine years and showed no signs of letting go.

In thread town terms, this was excellence.

The leaf stitching along the collar was the work of Chester's original owner, who had been a teacher of needlework. Each leaf was slightly different — some fat, some narrow, some with careful veining traced in a darker thread. No machine had made them. Each one had been thought about.

Chester kept people warm. That was his purpose, and he was proud of it. Children had worn him to school on cold mornings and arrived warm. He had been pulled over shoulders at outdoor concerts when the evening turned sharp. He had wrapped around grief and celebration alike — coats are present at both.

Now a young child named Mira sometimes pressed her face into Chester's chest and breathed in the deep woollen smell of him, which she said made her feel safe.

Chester held her in his sleeves and let the stitch fun of his history settle around her like a hug.
