On the flat roof of the old farmhouse, accessed by a stepladder kept leaning against the back wall, a curious telescope named Long stood on his tripod every clear night and looked at the sky.

Long was not a professional telescope — he was a medium-sized amateur instrument with a four-inch aperture, bought by a retired teacher who had always meant to learn the constellations and had, in the end, actually learned them.

The night view from the farmhouse roof was remarkable. Far from city lights, the Milky Way was visible as a genuine smear of brightness across the dark — not a textbook diagram but a real thing, present and enormous overhead.

Long loved the star watch above all other activities.

Through his eyepiece each night came a different sequence of objects: the moon, when it was up, filling the lens with its grey-white craters so sharp you could trace the shadows; Saturn, tiny but unmistakable, its rings tilted at whatever angle the orbit provided this year; Jupiter with its cloud bands and the four dots of its Galilean moons, different positions each night as they moved in their faithful circles.

Tonight was a double star night.

The teacher's granddaughter had come to stay, and she was being introduced to Albireo — a star that looked single to the naked eye but through Long's optics split into two colors, one gold and one blue, close together at the top of the constellation Cygnus.

The girl pressed her eye to the eyepiece and was quiet for a moment.

"They're two different colors," she said.

"They are," her grandfather said.

"Have they always been there?"

"For millions of years."

The girl stood up straight and looked at the sky without the telescope. The star was invisible to her unaided eye — just part of the general scatter of light. But she knew it now. She knew what was inside it.

Long waited patiently on his tripod for the next person who wanted to see.


The girl came back to the roof the next clear night and asked to see Albireo again. Then she asked about Saturn. Then she asked how far away the stars were and listened with great seriousness to the answer.

Long held steady on his tripod through all of it, the night view open and enormous above them both.
