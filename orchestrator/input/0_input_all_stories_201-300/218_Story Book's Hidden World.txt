On the middle shelf of the library at the end of the hall, there sat a magic story book named Page. She had a dark blue cover embossed with golden stars and smelled of old paper and afternoon tea. But her most remarkable quality was known to very few: she had hidden pages.

Everyone who opened Page saw the first story — a tale about a sailor who crossed three seas and found a chest of singing seashells. It was a good story. Children read it often. But those who read it very slowly, and with great care, and who stayed very quiet afterward, sometimes heard something else — a faint rustle from deeper inside the book.

One rainy evening, a child named Oliver read every single word of the sailor's story without rushing. When he finished, instead of closing the book, he sat quietly with it in his lap, listening.

The rustle came. And then, when he turned what should have been the final page, he found another page beneath it — then another, and another. He had found the word world.

The hidden pages contained stories no one had written down yet. They were stories of the dreams books have when they rest on the shelf at night: stories about a library that existed underwater, a letter that was never sent but knew everything its writer felt, a map that could only be read by moonlight.

Oliver read them all. He read until the rain stopped and the room had gone from grey to pink to the deep blue of early evening.

When he gently closed the book, he found a small slip of paper that had not been there before. On it, in tiny careful writing, were the words: Thank you for reading slowly.

He placed the slip inside the cover and put Page back on the shelf. She felt warm, and full, and entirely happy. The hidden pages had been found at last.

Page rested on the shelf that night with a deep contentment she had not felt in years. The hidden pages had been there a long time — long enough that she had begun to wonder if anyone would ever find them. But Oliver had. He had brought exactly the right thing: not a quick read, but a slow, careful, listening kind of attention. That was all the hidden pages had ever needed.
