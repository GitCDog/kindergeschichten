There was once a cheerful paint drop named Dot, and she lived at the very tip of a paintbrush balanced on the edge of a canvas. The studio around her smelled of linseed oil and possibility, and Dot thought it was the most magnificent place in the world.

Dot was cerulean blue — a color so deep and clear that when you looked at her you could almost see the sky inside her.

One crisp morning, the painter's hand tipped the brush just so, and Dot fell.

The canvas jump was exhilarating — a brief, breathless plunge through the cool air — and then she hit the white canvas with a magnificent color splash that sent tiny ripples spreading outward in all directions.

"Oh!" said Dot, looking at herself spread across the canvas. She had not known she could be so large.

The painter studied her for a long moment, head tilted. Then the brush returned — this time loaded with a warm yellow — and placed a new drop beside Dot. Then green. Then a deep red. Each color found its splash-spot, and slowly the white canvas began to fill with a world.

Dot found herself becoming the sky of a painting — a wide, sweeping sky above a field of wheat and a farmhouse with smoke curling from its chimney. She had not imagined she could become a sky. She had just been a drop.

Nearby, the yellow settled into sunlight and the red became a barn. The green rippled into trees along a distant hill. Together they made something that hadn't existed that morning, and wouldn't exist in the same way ever again.

When the painter stepped back at last and cleaned her brush, the studio fell quiet.

Dot — spread wide and luminous across the top of the canvas — watched the light from the window move slowly across the painting as the afternoon wore on. She had started the day barely the size of a raindrop.

Now she was the whole sky.

And that, she thought, was the most wonderful kind of splash story imaginable.
