In the bakery on the corner of Elm Street and Mill Road, there was a cupcake named Cup who considered herself the finest thing on the display shelf, and she was probably right.

Cup was vanilla sponge — light and even-textured, risen to a perfect dome. Her frosting was a generous swirl of cream cheese, applied with a piping bag by the baker's careful hand. And her top — her crowning achievement — was covered in sprinkles.

Not ordinary sprinkles. The bakery joy sprinkles were a special mix that the baker had developed over many years: small discs and cylinders in every possible color, with a few gold stars scattered in to catch the light. When Cup was placed under the display case spotlights, the sprinkle top turned her into a small, contained celebration.

She knew it.

Customers who came to the bakery would sometimes stop in front of the case for a long time before choosing. Cup watched them through the glass with benevolent confidence. She knew they would come to her eventually. The sprinkles guaranteed it.

A little girl arrived with her mother just before noon. She pressed both hands against the glass and left warm foggy prints.

"That one," she said immediately, pointing at Cup without hesitation.

Cup felt the profound rightness of being chosen.

She was placed in a small white box, tied with a ribbon, and carried out into the world. The journey was brief — across the street to the park, where the girl sat on a bench and untied the ribbon with great ceremony.

The first bite was through the frosting and the sprinkle top together. The girl closed her eyes.

Cup experienced, in her final moments, the complete satisfaction of having been made for something and arrived at it precisely. The sponge was light. The frosting was rich. And somewhere in the sprinkle mix, a gold star caught the light for one last moment.


The baker made twelve more cupcakes that afternoon. Each one received the sprinkle mix with equal generosity. Each one sat under the spotlights with the same small radiance.

But the baker knew — and the regulars knew — that the first cupcake chosen on any given day was the one that had been chosen on purpose, with full attention. That was its own kind of bakery joy.
