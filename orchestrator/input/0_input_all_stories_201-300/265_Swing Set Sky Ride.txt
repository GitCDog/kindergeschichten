At the back of the garden, standing on four metal legs with a slight lean to the left that everyone had long ago stopped noticing, was a cheerful swing set named Set. Set had two swings — one blue, one yellow — and a horizontal bar across the top from which things were occasionally hung: a wind chime, once a bird feeder, currently a jump rope that no one had climbed down to retrieve.

The sky push was the whole point of swinging, and everyone who had ever sat in one of Set's seats knew this.

You pumped your legs — back on the upswing, forward on the fall — and the seat rose with each pass until you were very high and the sky was closer than it usually was and the garden below had shrunk to something manageable. At the peak of the sky push, there was one still moment between going up and coming down where you were, briefly, neither.

This was the moment everyone was chasing.

Lily had been swinging on Set since she was small enough to need the blue baby seat, which had long since been replaced by the regular one. She knew exactly how many pumps it took to reach maximum height — eleven — and she knew the exact feeling of the chain going slightly slack at the top, a tiny looseness that meant she had made it.

Today her little brother Max had learned to pump his own legs.

It had taken most of the afternoon. He had started by dragging his feet on the ground in frustration, then watched Lily demonstrate, then attempted again with fierce concentration. And then — there — his legs had found the rhythm, and the swing had begun to move on its own.

"I'm doing it!" he had said, with a joy so pure that Lily had laughed without meaning to.

The park joy of the swing set was this: it was available to anyone who found the rhythm. Age didn't matter. It was a matter of learning, once, and then you always knew.
