The hopscotch grid had been chalked onto the school playground by a teacher on a Tuesday, and by Wednesday afternoon it had become the center of the playground universe.

The number path began with one and ended with ten, laid out in the traditional arrangement: single squares for one, two, four, five, seven, eight, and ten; paired squares for three, six, and nine. The chalk was yellow, which showed up well on the grey concrete.

The jump game had rules that were universal and also immediately subject to variation.

Throw the stone. Hop to the stone's square on one foot — hop, hop — jump pairs with both feet — hop, hop — reach the end, turn, return, pick up the stone, hop back.

If you stepped on a line, you lost your turn.

If you missed your square, you lost your turn.

If the stone landed on a line, you threw again.

By Thursday, the original rules had been elaborated. Someone had added a rule about singing a specific song while jumping. Someone else had added a ruling about the color of shoes permitted on the final square. A very small child named Otto had developed a personal rule involving spinning twice on square six which no one had officially sanctioned but no one had stopped either.

The number path was worn by Friday. The ten had started to fade. The three had been redrawn over the original, leaving a ghost of itself visible beneath.

A girl named Bea was the unofficial champion — not because she had won the most turns, but because she had never once stepped on a line. She moved through the number path with a particular focused lightness, as if her feet knew something about gravity that other feet didn't.

On Friday, before the bell, she showed Otto how to do the jump at six without spinning.

Otto tried it. He stepped on the line.

"Again," Bea said, and waited.


Otto tried the jump at six again without the spin. He landed cleanly, one foot in the square.

Bea nodded. "Again," she said.

Otto went again. Clean. He grinned the kind of grin that only appears when a thing you couldn't do five minutes ago is suddenly something you can. The number path held all the squares in their proper places, waiting for whoever needed them next.
