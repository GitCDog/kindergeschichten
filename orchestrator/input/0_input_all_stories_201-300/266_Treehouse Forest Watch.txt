High in the arms of the oak tree at the end of the garden, accessed by a wood ladder of seven rungs and a hatch in the floor, was a treehouse named Top. Top had been built by a father and daughter over two consecutive summers, and though his planks were weathered now and his roof needed a new felt square in the northwest corner, he was solid and proud and perfectly suited to his purpose.

The forest watch was Top's highest duty.

From his platform, the whole garden was visible, and beyond the garden wall, the top of the next-door hedge, and beyond that — in the right weather, when the air was clear — the hill above the village with the old windmill on it.

The girl who had built Top — who was now thirteen and still climbed the wood ladder several times a week — called the forest watch her thinking time. She would sit cross-legged on the platform with a blanket and a book she usually forgot to open, and she would look out at the green world and let her thoughts move slowly, unscheduled.

From up here, small things became visible that weren't visible from the ground.

The sparrows' nest in the hedge corner. The way the wind moved through the oak's upper branches before it reached the lower ones, so you had a moment of warning before the gust arrived. The fox, who crossed the garden at approximately the same time every evening, always from the left and always heading right, always in a hurry, always alone.

Top kept watch over all of it.

On rainy days, the rain on the roof was very loud and very satisfying. On windy days, Top moved very slightly — not enough to worry about, just enough to remind you that you were up in a tree, in the air, part of the garden's living world rather than a spectator of it.

The girl would always climb down eventually. But she always looked back up.


Top held the girl's weight and the weight of her thoughts and the weight of the seasons that had moved through his planks. He was proud of all of it. The wood ladder swayed very slightly in the evening wind, and the view from the platform was, as always, worth the climb.
