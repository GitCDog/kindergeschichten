Every morning, a cheerful lunchbox named Lunch was packed with great care by a mother who took the contents very seriously. Lunch was red with a yellow clasp and just the right size for a reasonable lunch, and inside each day a different arrangement of food friends would assemble for the school journey.

Today's crew was particularly good.

There was half a sandwich — tuna, with a little cucumber for crunch — wrapped in a piece of wax paper and settled on the left side like a comfortable old friend. Next to the sandwich sat a small pot of yoghurt, which was new and slightly nervous about the lunch experience. Three crackers occupied the right corner in a tidy stack. A small bag of grapes completed the party.

"First time?" the sandwich asked the yoghurt.

"Is it that obvious?" the yoghurt said.

"You'll be fine," the sandwich assured him. "The clasp opens smoothly and the child always uses a spoon."

The grapes — who were experienced — said nothing, just arranged themselves more comfortably in their bag.

Snack time arrived at school.

The clasp popped, and light flooded into Lunch's belly. A hand reached in and distributed the food friends across the lunch table. The sandwich sat beside a friend's sandwich. The crackers were eaten with a focused pleasure that suggested genuine appreciation. The grapes disappeared one by one, each with a small sound.

The yoghurt was last. The spoon arrived, and it was, as promised, used with care. The yoghurt turned out to be quite delicious, and the child scraped the pot clean.

"Better than expected," the yoghurt announced to no one remaining.

On the way home, Lunch was lighter and smelled pleasantly of tuna and sugar. The food friends were gone but had left a warmth behind — the particular satisfaction of a lunch well packed and well received.

That evening, Lunch was rinsed and left open to air on the counter, ready for tomorrow's assembly.

Another crew. Another day. Another snack time story.


Each day a slightly different group assembled inside Lunch — sometimes apple slices, sometimes cheese cubes, once a very exciting piece of birthday cake wrapped in foil. Each assembly was thoughtful. Each was consumed with gratitude. And each empty lunchbox that returned home told a quiet success story.
