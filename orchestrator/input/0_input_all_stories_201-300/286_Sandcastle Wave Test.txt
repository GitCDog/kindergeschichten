On the beach at low tide, in the wide band of firm damp sand between the water line and the dry beach above, two children built the sandcastle.

It was an ambitious castle — four towers connected by walls, with a moat that required significant effort to dig to the required depth. Its architect was a girl named Bea; its labor force was her younger brother Kit, who was excellent at carrying water in a red bucket.

The wave test would come, as it always did. The tide was turning.

Bea knew this. The castle's location had been chosen deliberately — not above the wave line, where the sand was too dry and crumbly, but in the exact zone where the sand held its shape perfectly under pressure and the threat was real.

The first wave came well short.

The second came closer.

The third wave ran up the beach in a long, thin sheet of white water and reached the moat, which filled to the top and then held. The castle walls were untouched.

"It's working," Kit said.

The fourth wave was larger. It overtopped the moat, washed against the outer wall, and receded. The wall held, though it was now visibly wet and the surface had dissolved into a smoother curve.

The fifth wave came fast.

It broke higher than expected and swept through the moat, against the wall, against the west tower, and the west tower leaned and fell inward in a satisfying slow collapse. The moat filled entirely. The main gate — a grand arch that Bea had been especially proud of — was swallowed.

The sixth wave finished everything.

The beach world reclaimed the sand.

Kit looked at where the castle had been. "Should we build another one?"

Bea looked at the receding wave. "Yes," she said. "Higher up."

They carried the red bucket up the beach and began again.


The second castle was built further up the beach, in firmer sand, with a wider moat and a lower profile. It lasted three more waves than the first one.

Bea took note of the design improvements. Kit carried the water bucket with enthusiasm undiminished by failure. The tide continued its patient advance, which was neither victory nor defeat — just the sea being the sea.
