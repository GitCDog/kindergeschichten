In the corner of the garden under the apple tree, the sandbox held its grain world in patient abundance. It was a wooden sandbox with four sides and no roof, and the sand inside it was pale and fine and endlessly cooperative.

The castle build had been underway since nine o'clock.

Its architect was a boy named Will, who approached sandbox construction with a seriousness that impressed even the garden robin, who had come to sit on the apple branch above and observe proceedings.

Will had a system. First, the outer wall — packed sand, firmly tamped, built in sections with a small break in one side for the gate. Then the inner courtyard, smoothed flat and bounded. Then the towers, each one a cylinder of sand packed around Will's cupped hands and then carefully slid off to leave a hollow column.

The grain world had its own rules. Wet sand held. Dry sand fell. Will had learned to keep one small bucket of water nearby and add it in controlled amounts — too much and the sand became soup, too little and the towers crumbled at a touch.

The castle had three towers now. Will was working on the fourth.

The grain world of the sandbox was in some ways the most honest construction material there was. It didn't pretend. If a wall was badly packed it fell. If a tower was too tall it toppled. The sand gave immediate, truthful feedback.

Will built a small gatehouse over the break in the outer wall. He lined the inner courtyard path with flat pebbles collected from the garden edge. He pressed a twig into the highest tower as a flag.

He sat back and looked at the castle.

The robin tilted its head.

"Good," Will decided.

He left the castle standing and went inside for lunch. The sandbox held it faithfully while he was gone, the grain world patient and still in the afternoon shade of the apple tree.


When Will returned after lunch, the castle was still standing. The towers had not fallen. The pebble path was still in place, and the twig flag leaned very slightly in the breeze, as all good flags should.

Will sat down beside it and began adding a second courtyard. There was always more to build.
