Once upon a time, in a forest where the pine trees grew tall and the mornings smelled of resin and cool earth, there lived a brave bear cub named Beau. [long pause]
Beau was round and brown and had ears that were slightly too large for his head, which his mother said he would grow into. He had been alive for one summer — which meant he knew a great many things already, and suspected he didn't know nearly enough, which is the beginning of wisdom. [sighs]
Today was the day of the first honey trail. [beat]
Every bear cub had to do it. Find the honey tree alone — using nose and memory and the things your mother had taught you — and bring back proof. Not a lot of honey. Just enough to show you could find it.
Beau's mother had shown him the forest trail three times. Each time she had said the same things: follow the smell, not the path. Trust your nose before your eyes. And if you get lost, sit still — the forest will remind you where you are.
[softly] Beau set off alone in the early morning, when the mist was still in the valleys and the birds were just starting up.
He put his nose to the air. Pine. Damp earth. Something sweet — very faint — from the north. He turned north. [pacing]
The forest trail wound through bracken and over a root-covered ridge. Twice he lost the honey smell and had to backtrack. Once he sat very still on a mossy stone and waited, as his mother had said, until the forest reminded him — and it did, in the form of a bee, humming past his left ear in a direction that smelled suddenly right. [with growing realization]
He followed the bee.
The honey tree was an old oak, hollow at its heart, buzzing with a sound that Beau felt more than heard. He reached in carefully — very carefully — and brought out one small pawful of dark, thick, extraordinary first honey.
He ate a little on the spot. [softly] It tasted like the whole summer condensed into one amber mouthful: flowers and warmth and time and patience and the particular satisfaction of having found the thing by yourself.
He carried the rest home in his cupped paw, not spilling a drop.
His mother said nothing when he arrived. She just touched her nose to the top of his head, which, in bear, means everything.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
