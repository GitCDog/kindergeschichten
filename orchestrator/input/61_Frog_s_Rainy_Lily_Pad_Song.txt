Once upon a time, on a lily pad pond that sat in a hollow between two gentle hills, there lived a friendly frog named Fletcher. [long pause]
Fletcher was green — a particular warm green, like moss in good light — and he lived on the largest lily pad in the pond, which he had claimed not by force but by arriving first every morning and sitting there with such peaceful authority that no one had ever thought to challenge him. [sighs contentedly]
Fletcher loved rain. [beat]
Not all frogs love rain — some find it excessive. Fletcher loved everything about it: the smell before it arrived, the sound of the first drops on the lily pad surface, the way the pond surface broke into a thousand tiny rings all overlapping each other, the coolness of it on his smooth green back, and most of all the music it made.
Because the lily pad pond in the rain was a concert.
The drops on the lily pads made a soft percussion — some pads large and resonant, some small and high. The drops on the water surface made another layer. The reeds at the edge caught the rain in a different way entirely, a rustling whisper. [softly] And over all of it, from the hills beyond, the low sound of water finding its way down through grass and soil to the pond.
On rainy days, Fletcher sang. [with growing realization]
Not to anyone in particular. Not for any reason other than that the music of the rain asked for a voice to go with it, and Fletcher had a voice — a warm, round, resonant croak that had been called by the pond's other inhabitants, at various times, surprisingly pleasant.
He sang from his lily pad through every rainstorm, his throat pulsing, his eyes half-closed, the rain cool and constant around him. [pacing]
Other frogs joined him sometimes. The newts listened from the reeds. A heron, sheltering under the willows at the pond's edge, stood very still for a very long time.
When the rain stopped, Fletcher sat quietly in the returning sun, the pond surface still and steaming slightly, and felt that particular peace that comes after music. [softly]
He had learned that every kind of weather has a gift in it, if you know how to listen.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
