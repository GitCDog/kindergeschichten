Once upon a time, in a forest where the oaks grew thick and the autumn light fell golden through the canopy, there lived a cheerful chipmunk named Chester. [long pause]
Chester had rust-red fur with five perfect stripes running from his nose to his tail, cheeks that could hold an impressive quantity of acorns, and a personality that could most accurately be described as enthusiastic. He approached every part of his day at full speed, which in a chipmunk is already quite fast, and the acorn race was the event he looked forward to most all year. [sighs with barely contained excitement]
The acorn race was a forest tradition. [beat]
Every autumn, the squirrels and chipmunks of the forest competed to see who could collect and bury the most acorns in a single day. It was part sport, part practical necessity — the buried acorns would feed everyone through winter — and part excuse for a great deal of friendly shouting across the canopy.
Chester had a strategy this year. [softly]
Rather than running the usual route — oak to hole, oak to hole, back and forth all day — he had spent a week mapping. He knew where every productive oak was. He had pre-dug six holes in a careful pattern that minimized backtracking. He had practiced his cheek-loading to maximum capacity. He had, he felt, thought of everything.
He had not thought of the squirrel. [with growing realization]
Hazel the squirrel lived three trees over and had the longest legs of any squirrel in the forest. She covered twice the ground Chester did in any given run. By mid-morning she was already ahead by thirty acorns. [pacing]
Chester ran faster. His cheeks reached capacity. He buried, ran, loaded, buried. The forest fun of it — the cool air, the smell of fallen leaves, the sound of other chipmunks chattering — pushed him on.
By afternoon, he was still behind. [beat]
He came in second. Not a close second, either.
[softly] Hazel found him at the finish line, cheeks still full, breathing hard. "Good race," she said.
Chester chewed his last acorn thoughtfully. "Good race," he agreed. He looked at his full holes, scattered neatly across the forest floor. He had buried more acorns today than ever before in his life.
Second place, he decided, was still a very good place to be.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
