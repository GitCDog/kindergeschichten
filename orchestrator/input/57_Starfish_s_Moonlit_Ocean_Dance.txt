Once upon a time, on the sandy floor of a shallow cove where the water was clear as glass and the moonlight reached all the way down, there lived a sparkly starfish named Pearl. [long pause]
Pearl had five arms — as starfish do — each one tipped with a tiny point that caught the light in a way that was, she had been told, rather dazzling. She was the brightest creature on the ocean floor, and she knew it, and she tried very hard not to be vain about it, with mixed success. [sighs]
What Pearl did not know was that she could dance. [beat]
She had never tried. Starfish are not known for dancing. They are known for being beautiful and still, for clinging to rocks at low tide, for being generally ornamental. Pearl had accepted this as her role in the great ocean arrangement and had been quietly, if slightly restlessly, content with it.
Then came the moon tide.
The moon tide happened only once a year — when the moon was full and at its closest to the ocean and the water grew so clear and so still that the moonlight lay on the surface like a solid floor of silver. [softly] On that night, the light reached the ocean floor in long silver columns that swayed very gently as the surface moved.
Pearl woke in the middle of the moon tide and found herself in a column of moving silver light. [with growing realization]
She lifted one arm. The light caught it. She turned slowly — starfish-slowly, which is not fast at all — and the light moved with her, glittering from each of her five tips in turn. She turned again. She extended two arms wide and the column of moonlight framed her like a stage.
[pacing] She danced. Slowly, entirely, with complete and unhurried commitment. Five-pointed, silver-tipped, ancient and new all at once.
The fish gathered at the edge of the light and watched without making a sound.
The moon moved overhead. The silver column shifted. Pearl followed it, dancing from one pool of moonlight to the next. [softly]
She danced until the moon set and the ocean floor went dark and ordinary again.
She lay still on the sand, arms spread wide, and thought: I did not know I had that in me. But I did. I always did.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
