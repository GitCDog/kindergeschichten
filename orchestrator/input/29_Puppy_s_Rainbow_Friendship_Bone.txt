Once upon a time, in a house with a bright red letterbox and a garden full of lavender, there lived a magic puppy named Biscuit. [long pause]
Biscuit had soft brown ears, a nose like a small wet button, and a rainbow collar that shimmered with every color you could name — and a few you couldn't — whenever the light hit it just right. The collar had been a gift, though Biscuit couldn't quite remember from whom. He only knew that it glowed warmest when he was doing something kind. [sighs contentedly]
Biscuit's best skill was finding things that were lost — not keys or socks, but the invisible kind of lost. The feeling of being lonely in a new place. The ache of an argument that hadn't been mended yet. The hollow that comes from forgetting you are loved. [beat]
He could smell these things the way other dogs smelled bones. And when he found them, he did what he did best: he showed up, and he stayed.
One afternoon, Biscuit sat at the garden gate and sniffed the air. Something four houses down needed attending to. [softly]
He squeezed under the gate — just barely — and trotted down the pavement.
Four houses down lived an elderly man named Mr. Fenwick who had moved to the street last winter and hadn't spoken to anyone since. His curtains were always closed. His path was unswept. [with growing realization]
Biscuit sat at his door and scratched once.
After a long moment, it opened. Mr. Fenwick looked down. He was the sort of man who pretended not to like dogs. [beat]
Biscuit sat. He offered his paw.
Mr. Fenwick looked at the paw for a very long time. Then he crouched down — slowly, the way old knees require — and shook it.
[pacing] "I suppose you'd better come in, then," he said gruffly.
The rainbow collar blazed so brightly that Mr. Fenwick had to blink.
Biscuit stayed for the whole afternoon. He sat on the rug while Mr. Fenwick talked — about his wife, about his old house, about how strange it was to start over at seventy-three. He didn't ask for anything. He just listened.
When Biscuit finally trotted home, Mr. Fenwick stood in his open doorway and watched him go. For the first time in months, the curtains stayed open.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
