Once upon a time, in a garden where the morning dew collected in perfect droplets on every petal and leaf, there lived a tiny fairy named Fern. [long pause]
Fern was no taller than a thimble, with hair the color of moss and wings like a dragonfly — thin and iridescent, catching the light in shifting greens and blues. She lived in a dew drop castle: a magnificent little palace built entirely from crystallized morning dew, perched at the tip of the tallest foxglove in the garden. It caught the sunrise every morning and threw tiny rainbows across the grass. [sighs contentedly]
Fern loved her castle. But she loved dancing most of all — and her greatest dream was to dance with a butterfly. [beat]
The butterflies of the garden were beautiful and fast and always busy. They flickered from flower to flower with great purpose and didn't often stop. Fern had tried to introduce herself seventeen times. Each time, the butterfly was already gone by the time she got the words out.
One dewy morning, Fern climbed down from her castle and sat at the edge of a large daisy to wait. [softly] The sun came up. The dew began to sparkle. And then — settling gently on the daisy right beside her — came a butterfly. Large wings, deep orange with black trim, perfectly still.
Fern held her breath.
[quietly] "Hello," said the butterfly. "I've seen you trying to catch us."
Fern went pink. "I only wanted to ask if you'd like to dance."
The butterfly tilted its wings — the way a person might tilt their head. [with growing realization] "I've never been asked that before," it said.
"No one asks butterflies to dance?"
"No one ever stops long enough to ask us anything." [beat]
The butterfly spread its wings fully — enormous beside tiny Fern — and she spread hers in return, dragonfly-thin and shimmering. And there, on the dewy daisy in the early morning garden, while the dew drop castle caught the first light above them, a tiny fairy and a butterfly began to dance.
[pacing] They moved through the air together slowly, trailing light, while the garden held its breath around them.
It was the most beautiful thing the foxgloves had ever seen.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
