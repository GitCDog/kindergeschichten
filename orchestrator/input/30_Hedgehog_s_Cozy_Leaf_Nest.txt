Once upon a time, in a garden where the autumn had turned everything gold and copper and the smell of woodsmoke drifted over the fence, there lived a cozy hedgehog named Hazel. [long pause]
Hazel was the sort of hedgehog who believed deeply in comfort. She believed in thick socks — metaphorically, since she didn't wear them — in warm corners, in the smell of rain on dry ground, and most particularly in a good pile of autumn leaves. [sighs with great contentment]
Each autumn she built herself a nest. Not just any nest. The best nest. It was her finest project of the year. [beat]
She had spent all week selecting leaves. Not the crispy ones that crumbled — too loud in the night — and not the damp ones that smelled of mud. The perfect ones: thick, dry, fragrant with the particular smell of late October, that color halfway between rust and gold. She carried them one by one to the hollow under the old garden wall.
She arranged them. She rearranged them. She tried a different configuration. She rearranged them again. [pacing]
Then she found it — tucked under a damp log nearby — the warm blanket. A small square of felted wool, left by some gardener and then forgotten, soft as clouds, the color of autumn heather.
[with growing realization] Hazel held it in her small paws and felt, very simply, that the universe had been paying attention.
She lined the inside of the leaf nest with the warm blanket, turned around three times in the way that hedgehogs have always done, and settled in.
[softly] The garden grew quiet. The last leaves let go of the branches and drifted down. Somewhere a fox called once and then was silent.
Hazel pulled the warm blanket snug around herself, tucked her nose under her paws, and breathed in the smell of autumn leaves and contentment.
Sometimes, she thought drowsily, the whole world narrows down to just this — a small, warm, perfect place — and that is more than enough.
She was asleep before the thought was finished.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
