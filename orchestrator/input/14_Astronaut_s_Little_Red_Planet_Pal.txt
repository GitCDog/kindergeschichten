Once upon a time, in a small silver rocket parked on a hill behind the science museum, there lived a tiny astronaut named Milo. [long pause]
Milo wore a puffy white suit with a goldfish-bowl helmet, and his rocket was painted red with yellow stars. He had traveled to many places — past the rings of Saturn, around the moons of Jupiter, through fields of glittering asteroids — but he had never, not once, been to the red planet. [sighs] He had always wanted to go. Tonight, he decided, was the night.
He pressed the big green button. The engines rumbled. The rocket lifted slowly off the hill, past the rooftops, past the clouds, past the dark blue edge of the sky — and into the stars. [beat]
The red planet grew larger in his window. Larger, and larger, and larger — until it filled the whole glass with its dusty orange-red glow.
Milo landed with a gentle bump. He stepped outside. [softly] The ground was rust-colored and soft beneath his boots. The sky above was a pale, pinkish violet. Two small moons hung overhead like quiet lamps.
"Hello?" he called, just in case.
[with growing realization] Something moved behind a large red rock.
A small creature peered out. Then another. Then six more, in a row — round-faced, large-eyed, each one a different shade of orange, all staring at Milo with enormous curiosity.
His martian friends had been there all along, waiting for someone to say hello. [beat]
The largest one stepped forward and pressed its hand — which had only three fingers — gently against Milo's glove. Milo pressed back.
They spent the night exploring craters together, collecting interesting pebbles, and drawing pictures in the red dust. The martian friends showed him where the ice hid under the rocks. Milo showed them how to do a proper zero-gravity somersault, which they found absolutely hilarious. [pacing]
When the two small moons began to set, Milo knew it was time to go home. He waved from the door of his rocket. His martian friends waved back — three-fingered waves, all eight of them, in a row.
[softly] He carried their friendship all the way home through the stars.
Some of the best friends in the universe, he decided, are the ones you haven't met yet.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
