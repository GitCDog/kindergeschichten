Once upon a time, in a river that wound through a quiet corner of Australia where the gum trees leaned over the water and the evenings smelled of eucalyptus and damp earth, there lived a playful platypus named Percy. [long pause]
Percy was aware that he was unusual. He had thought about it quite a lot, over the years, and had come to a comfortable peace with it. He had a bill like a duck, a tail like a beaver, fur like an otter, and laid eggs, which last detail tended to surprise people most. He could feel electrical signals through his bill, which was how he found things in the riverbed in the dark. He had venom in his back feet. He was, he felt, comprehensively interesting. [sighs with philosophical satisfaction]
The river bill games were his invention. [beat]
Every evening, when the river went golden with the lowering sun, Percy would play. He had a particular game with river rocks — finding them with his bill in the murky bottom, sorting them by size and shape by feel alone, arranging them in patterns on the riverbed that no one but Percy ever saw. Another game: catching the current just right to spin in slow circles near the surface, bill pointing up, watching the sky revolve overhead. [softly]
His favorite game required a willing partner, and tonight that partner was a young turtle named Nell who had started coming to this bend in the river because Percy was reliably entertaining.
The game was simple: Percy would dive to the bottom of the river and, using his bill, locate something interesting — a smooth stone, a piece of water-polished wood, a shell — and bring it up without Nell seeing what it was. She had to guess what it was before he opened his bill. [with growing realization]
"Stone," said Nell.
Percy opened his bill. A stone. [beat]
"Wood," said Nell.
Percy opened his bill. A piece of wood, curved in a perfect arc. [pacing]
"This one," said Percy, surfacing with something clearly round and smooth — "will surprise you."
Nell thought. "Stone again."
Percy opened his bill. A small perfect sphere — a river pearl, white as the moon, formed over years around a grain of sand.
[softly] They both stared at it for a moment, bobbing gently in the golden river.
"Keep it," said Percy.
Nell tucked it carefully against her shell. The duck-billed fun of the evening, she thought, was always better than she expected.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
