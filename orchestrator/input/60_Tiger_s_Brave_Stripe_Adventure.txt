Once upon a time, deep in a jungle where the light came through in tiger-colored bars of gold, there lived a tiny tiger named Taj. [long pause]
Taj was young — his paws were still slightly too big for him and his tail had not yet fully decided what to do with itself — and his stripes were the newest, brightest orange stripes in the entire jungle. But Taj had a problem. His roar was not ready. [sighs]
Every tiger eventually found their roar. It was a rite of passage — the moment when the sound that had been building inside you finally found its way out, big and golden and true, the kind that rolls through the jungle and makes the leaves tremble. Taj's older brothers had found theirs at six months. His sister at five. Taj was eight months old and producing, at his best efforts, something that landed somewhere between a cough and a suggestion. [beat]
"Don't force it," his mother told him. "It comes when it's ready."
This was not the advice Taj wanted. [softly]
He practiced alone, deep in the jungle where no one could hear. He tried making himself feel large. He tried making himself feel brave. He tried remembering the courage stripes — the inner ones, close to his spine, that were said to be not just decoration but something more, something carried in the blood of every tiger. [pacing]
One afternoon he found himself at the edge of the jungle, looking out at the open grassland beyond. He had never gone past the tree line before. [with growing realization]
He stepped out into the open.
The sky above was enormous. The grass stretched away in every direction. The wind moved across it in long slow waves. Everything was bigger than he had expected. He felt very small.
And then, from deep in his chest, without trying, without warning — it came. A sound that started in his bones and rolled upward through his whole body and out into the open afternoon air. A jungle roar. His jungle roar. [abruptly]
The grassland birds startled up from the grass.
Taj stood in the open, stripes blazing in the sun, and the echo of his own voice came back to him from the distant trees.
[softly] He walked back into the jungle, tail raised, paws confident in the long grass.
He had not found his roar by trying to be brave. He had found it by stepping into something larger than himself.
The end. Sweet dreams, little one.
